[[semianalysis]] analyzes the April 2025 Trump "Liberation Day" tariff executive order — a baseline 10% ad valorem on all imports with country-specific reciprocal rates (Taiwan 32%, South Korea 25%, Japan 24%, EU 20%, China 145%) — and maps the winners and losers across the AI infrastructure stack.

**GPU Loophole via USMCA.** The most important finding is a structural tariff bypass: data processing units (HS 8471.50/8471.80) and related parts (HS 8473.30) qualify as USMCA "originating goods" regardless of actual manufacturing origin, making Mexico- and Canada-routed GPU servers duty-free. Taiwan-direct shipments face 32%. Foxconn is building a $900M GB200 server assembly plant in Mexico operational by early 2026. Mexico already supplies ~67% of finished US server imports; that share is accelerating. NVIDIA is well-positioned — NVLink backplane final assembly occurs in Mexico. AMD benefits from both the Mexican ODM shift and CPU exemptions. Google TPU (Celestica Mexico board + US rack) and Amazon Trainium 2 (Wiwynn Mexico rack) similarly exploit the loophole.

**Datacenter CapEx Impact — Manageable.** Even in an upper-bound scenario with 15% higher colocation lease costs, GPU cluster TCO rises only ~2% because servers dominate spend. Equipment-only tariff increases land in the mid-to-high single digits. Diesel generators are least impacted (very high domestic assembly); UPS systems are most impacted (electronics-heavy, less than 50% domestic). High-power transformers are 80% imported, creating a structural bottleneck tied to domestic GOES steel deficiency.

**Optical Modules — Material Headwind.** Blended tariff rate estimated at ~40% on optical modules (27% ex-China) given production concentrated in Thailand, Vietnam, China, Taiwan, and Malaysia. Lumentum's EML/VCSEL fabs are entirely US-based, providing insulation; its CW laser back-end in Thailand remains exposed. Coherent has 6 US fabs and 4 European fabs, giving it production flexibility. Broadcom has two Singapore fabs (10% tariff) plus one US facility. Chinese optical vendors (Innolight, Eoptolink, TFC Optical) have been pre-emptively relocating capacity to Thailand.

**Wafer Fab Equipment — Structurally Disadvantaged.** US fab equipment costs rise ~15% from tariffs on ASML (Netherlands) and Tokyo Electron (Japan), which are irreplaceable for leading-edge logic and DRAM. With cost pass-through, US-produced chips would carry a >32% premium over Taiwan equivalents — exceeding the Taiwan tariff rate itself, neutralizing the onshoring incentive. 300mm wafer blanks and photoresist are effectively zero-domestic-supply items imported almost entirely from Japan.

**Macro Risk.** Rising 10-year rates combined with tariff uncertainty may constrain AI infrastructure capital deployment more than direct cost effects. Delayed capex could reduce training runs at frontier labs.
