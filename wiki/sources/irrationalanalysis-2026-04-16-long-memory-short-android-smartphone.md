[[irrationalanalysis]] pitches a market-neutral long/short pairs trade: long DRAM memory producers, short Android smartphone exposure. The thesis is framed as an exercise in paired investing — buying $X of one leg while shorting $X of the other to maintain 0% net exposure.

**The Long Leg — DRAM Memory**

The author has historically been bearish on memory due to cyclicality, citing brutal 2022–2023 DRAM downturns where Micron's CEO was publicly pleading for Samsung to cut supply. However, the current setup is different: AI inference demand is pushing out the next memory downcycle by at least 3 years, and cleanroom capacity constraints are expected to persist for 18–24 months. Given these conditions, the author argues it is safe to be long DRAM — specifically MU (Micron), SK Hynix (000660.KS), and Samsung (005930.KS) — for the next 12–18 months. Ongoing HBM supply concerns (Micron HBM4 base die issues, Samsung yield/reliability problems, SK Hynix potential fixes) are noted but treated as noise relative to the structural supply tightness.

**The Short Leg — Android Smartphones**

The short thesis rests on several converging factors. First, modern flagship Android phones (the author uses a Samsung Galaxy S23 Ultra as a personal example) have become so capable that upgrade cycles are stretching — reviewer MKBHD's annual smartphone videos have become increasingly nitpicky because there is genuinely little to differentiate new models from prior-year flagships. Second, LPDDR DRAM for smartphones is in acute shortage, making new Android devices more expensive and harder to source. Third, Apple is aggressively buying up available LPDDR at premium prices, simultaneously crowding out Android OEMs and taking market share. Apple is described as "out for blood," almost deliberately setting a higher market price for LPDDR to sabotage competition.

**The Battery / Longevity Angle**

A supplementary argument reinforces the short: smartphone degradation is almost entirely battery-driven (Li-Ion capacity and voltage loss from charge cycles, heat, and deep charge/discharge). Crucially, battery replacement is cheap (~$20–$50 total) and straightforward, meaning consumers facing expensive new-phone prices have an easy, low-cost alternative to upgrading. This further compresses Android OEM unit volumes and revenue.
