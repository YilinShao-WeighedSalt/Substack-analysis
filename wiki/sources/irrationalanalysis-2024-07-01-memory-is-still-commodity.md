[[irrationalanalysis]] covers Micron's Q3 FY24 earnings with the thesis that memory remains a commodity cycle business — "this time is not different." The stock surged pre-earnings on HBM enthusiasm and then crashed in after-hours as the guidance disappointed buy-side expectations.

**Earnings reaction:** All HBM capacity through FY25 is sold out with pricing locked in, making the print a classic sell-the-news event. The critical slide that broke the stock: Micron guided to ~$13B in FY25 capex against sell-side consensus of ~$10B, erasing the gross-margin expansion thesis. Higher HBM margins are offset by the higher capital intensity required to produce it — the commodity treadmill continues.

**HBM market dynamics:** Micron management reiterated that HBM bit CAGR will exceed 50% over several years, FY25 output is already committed, and the company is targeting DRAM-equivalent market share in HBM by end of 2025. The author dismisses Micron's claim to be among the "biggest AI beneficiaries in semiconductors," asserting that AI's structural winners are logic (NVDA, AVGO, TSM), WFE/semicap (AMAT, CAMT, ONTO, LRCX, KLAC), and SaaS — not memory, which remains cyclical and commodity-priced.

**Samsung HBM conspiracy theory:** Samsung failed Nvidia HBM qualification in April 2024 due to heat/power issues, with yields reportedly below 30% versus SK Hynix's 60–70%. The author's theory: Samsung knew its parts were off-spec, cut yield aggressively, re-sampled to Nvidia, and was called out. A rumored re-qualification attempt in July is dismissed as too soon to reflect a real fix — Samsung is likely just tightening parametric yield thresholds further. The author explains parametric yield in detail: voltage/clock targets are set to cover all functional dies in a SKU, so accepting lower yield always trades profitability for quality, not the reverse. Samsung will eventually qualify but at low volume and deeply disadvantaged economics.

**Micron vs. SK Hynix power premium:** The post details how Micron extracts a higher ASP for its 30%-more-power-efficient HBM3e by enabling Nvidia to remove power-delivery (VRM) components from boards designed around SK Hynix thermals. Both parties win — Micron charges more, Nvidia lowers BOM cost — a deliberate pricing strategy the author believes was explicitly modeled internally.

**WFE/Semicap view:** The author holds AMAT, CAMT, and ONTO (in that order by size). Micron's higher-than-expected capex is unambiguously positive for semicap; the author believes the AI capex bubble has at least 18–36 months to run before a violent correction. He is not adding but is not selling either, choosing to "embrace the bubble."

**NAND foreshadowing:** Multiple references to enterprise SSD/NAND as an underappreciated AI angle — Micron's storage BU just turned operating-profitable after 8–10 quarters of losses, and data-center SSD demand is tripling. A dedicated NAND/SSD idea is flagged as work-in-progress.
