[[irrationalanalysis]] published this note on August 4, 2024, as an engineering-driven explainer on the reported Nvidia Blackwell delay and its investment implications.

The author reports that Nvidia is delaying its Blackwell GPU platform by at least three months due to design flaws related to advanced chip packaging. The root cause lies in Blackwell's requirement for CoWoS-L packaging (as opposed to the CoWoS-S used by prior Nvidia GPUs). CoWoS-L uses a finer micro-bump pitch to support higher-density die-to-die signaling (e.g., a 10 TB/s interface) and improved power integrity, but the smaller bumps are harder to align and more susceptible to thermal-cycle-induced cracking — yielding lower manufacturing yields and reliability risks over time.

The author's technical hypothesis is that Nvidia will re-spin the logic dies to modify the bump-out layer (top metal layers of the die), removing, spacing out, or reconfiguring bumps to improve yield and reliability margins. This type of re-spin is consistent with the reported three-month timeline.

From an investment standpoint, the author holds more than half of their portfolio in NVDA shares and explicitly states they will not sell, citing Nvidia's multi-year technological lead over competitors. They did, however, sell covered calls against the majority of the NVDA position as a hedge against near-term volatility.

On competitors: the author covered their AMD short prior to publication, rotating that short position into QCOM instead. They note that the Blackwell delay is likely to increase AMD MI300/MI325X order volumes over the near-term, making AMD an "attractive gambling opportunity" for speculative call buyers. The author references AMD CEO Lisa Su's comments in recent earnings about having 2025 datacenter GPU visibility, now viewing that as potentially informed by prior knowledge of Blackwell's delay.

The author's core long thesis on NVDA is unchanged — the delay is a short-term engineering setback, not a competitive disruption.
