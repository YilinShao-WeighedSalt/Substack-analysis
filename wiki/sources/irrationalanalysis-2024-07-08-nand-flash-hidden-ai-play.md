[[irrationalanalysis]] argues that video generative AI models — specifically OpenAI's Sora — could trigger an underappreciated demand shock for enterprise NVMe SSDs, making NAND flash a contrarian AI adjacency play. The thesis originates from a simple observation: large-scale video model training requires local, pre-processed, compressed video datasets (e.g., scraped YouTube content) because live API pulls are too slow. This data must live on fast local storage, and NVMe SSDs are the only viable medium at the required throughput and density.

The post provides a technical primer on NAND flash: it is non-volatile, the most commoditized corner of semiconductors, and its economics are dominated by cost-per-bit rather than performance differentiation. Key technology dimensions include layer count (200+ layers in 3D NAND), bits-per-cell (TLC dominant, QLC growing), and the critical insight that NVMe drive performance is more dependent on the DRAM cache and logic controller than on the NAND chips themselves.

The author uses Western Digital's June 2024 "New Era of NAND" investor webinar as a lens to illustrate how badly the industry has suffered — from Chinese supplier YMTC flooding the market with cheap supply to brutal CapEx intensity — while noting WDC's aspirational pivot to hybrid bonding and premium pricing rings hollow given the commodity nature of the business. WDC and Kioxia (formerly Toshiba Flash, owned by Bain Capital) are described as sub-scale players attempting a merger repeatedly blocked by SK Hynix. Bain is now pushing a Kioxia IPO to refinance ~¥900bn in debt coming due.

On investment vectors: (1) Samsung, SK Hynix, and Micron all make NAND but are better characterized as DRAM/HBM plays; (2) WDC and Kioxia are structurally challenged pure-plays mired in corporate drama; (3) merchant NVMe controller vendors Phison (TPEX:8299) and Silicon Motion (SIMO) are called out as the cleanest ways to play NVMe growth without owning commoditized NAND fabs. The author notes that Marvell and Microchip also exist in this space but are not considered serious NVMe controller players.

Conclusion: NAND is a poor long-term investment by historical total-return metrics, but the combination of AI-driven inattention (all focus on HBM), historically low bit-growth expectations, and a potential video-model-training demand shock makes it an interesting tactical opportunity.
