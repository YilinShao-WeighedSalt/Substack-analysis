Published by [[irrationalanalysis]] on August 9, 2024, this post presents a forensic dissection of Intel's Q2 2024 earnings call, arguing that management used a technically plausible but misleading excuse — the shift of Meteor Lake production from Oregon to Ireland — to conceal a more fundamental and damaging problem: collapsing average selling prices (ASPs).

The author's central conspiracy theory is straightforward. Intel's gross margin came in roughly 400 basis points worse than expected despite revenue being broadly in line with guidance. CFO Zinsner and CEO Gelsinger attributed the miss primarily to the decision to accelerate Meteor Lake production from Oregon's technology development fab (TD fab) to the production fab in Ireland, framing it as a cost-saving move that came with a temporary elevated wafer-cost headwind. The author finds this explanation deeply suspicious: moving high-volume production from a research fab to a production fab is the obvious, always-planned outcome for any mature product. Why would that timing have changed, and why would it catch management by surprise only three months after the prior guide?

The author's alternative explanation centers on ASP erosion. A Dell internal pricing leak had revealed that Qualcomm is charging OEMs roughly half what Intel charges for comparable Meteor Lake-class laptop chips, yet Qualcomm is still earning ~52% gross margins — above its corporate average. Intel, by contrast, is running at 38-40% and declining. If OEMs are resisting Intel's prices and negotiating them down sharply (which the author believes the Dell leak was designed to force), then the real gross margin headwind is not depreciation or fab ramp economics but revenue per unit falling faster than costs. The Oregon-to-Ireland narrative conveniently absorbs the blame into a one-time COGS story and distracts analysts from modeling a structural ASP step-down.

Supporting the thesis: AMD is gaining client share, Qualcomm has a credible Snapdragon X Elite product, and MediaTek/Nvidia are collaborating on a third alternative. The Raptor Lake reliability disaster further damaged Intel's OEM relationships. The author argues sell-side models (citing Stacy Rasgon at Bernstein and Vivek Arya at BofA) are too optimistic on gross margins and that 30–35% gross margin in 2025 is entirely plausible. At that level, the ~$40 billion "effective cash" buffer Rasgon cites would be rapidly consumed.
