[[irrationalanalysis]] argues that a severe capacity panic is brewing in leading-edge logic (5nm-class and below) that most investors have missed because attention has been consumed by memory and advanced packaging shortages. The post surveys the three non-TSMC foundry players — Intel Foundry and Samsung Foundry — and concludes that TSMC's dominance is about to be stressed by demand it cannot absorb, while the alternatives remain materially inferior.

**Failure history and context.** The author catalogues the compounding failures at Intel (the 10nm/Intel 7 debacle driven by "cobalt" interconnect and mismanagement under "Murthy") and Samsung (multiple failed 3nm iterations, SF3 renamed SF2 without real PPA improvement). The core thesis is that recognizing mistakes fast is what separates winners from losers, and Intel took ~7 years to learn that lesson.

**Intel 18A.** The author has been bearish on Intel 18A yield for 18 months and claims vindication: Intel IR is openly admitting poor parametric yield to buy-side. However, under Lip-Bu Tan, the "queen sacrifice" gambit (threatening to exit leading edge) has worked. 18AP is viewed as a credible fixed iteration, with Apple reportedly interested and advanced packaging showing real traction ahead of logic revenue.

**Samsung Foundry recovery.** SF2 (functionally a 3nm-class node renamed) is mediocre but now half-decent at blown-up area. Tesla signed an 8-year deal to fill idle Taylor, TX capacity — likely at negative gross margins for Samsung initially, but the learning value justifies it. The Qualcomm–Samsung dynamic is key: Samsung DX forced Qualcomm to single-source TSMC for 2024–2025 Galaxy chips because SF3 was nonviable; for 2026 Samsung's own Exynos is back at 20–30% share, and Qualcomm is reportedly considering SF2 for 2027 Snapdragon derivatives. Rumors also place AMD (CPUs/datacenter) and Google (TPUs) exploring SF2.

**Panic conclusion.** The author sees Intel, AMD, Google, and potentially Apple all scrambling toward Samsung SF2 — not because it is good, but because TSMC has zero spare capacity and Intel 18AP is not usable externally until 2027. The recommendation is to go long semicap/WFE broadly, with personal holdings skewed toward advanced packaging, memory, and metrology names.
