[[globalsemiresearch]] surveys the rapid rise and equally rapid cooling of OpenClaw-derived AI agent products in China as of early 2026, analyzing five dimensions: product evolution, ecosystem competition, business model, security risks, and policy guidance.

OpenClaw — called "crayfish" (小龙虾) colloquially in China — exploded in popularity in early 2026 as an open-source AI agent capable of autonomously controlling computers. Within weeks, market sentiment reversed: "OpenClaw uninstallation services" appeared on Xianyu and Xiaohongshu for tens to hundreds of yuan, driven by security warnings from China's National Internet Emergency Center and Ministry of Industry and Information Technology, high token costs, and fears of AI misoperation.

**Product Evolution.** The core localization strategy of Chinese manufacturers is reducing technical complexity. Tencent's **QClaw** embeds agent control inside a WeChat mini-program, enabling zero-installation remote computer control via voice or text; its "Inspiration Square" pre-loads task templates. NetEase Youdao's **LobsterAI** replaces command-line interfaces with a GUI and supports WeChat Work, QQ, DingTalk, and Lark natively. Zhipu AI's **AutoClaude** achieves one-click local deployment in under three minutes. On deployment models, local deployment (Tencent, Zhipu, NetEase) prioritizes data privacy, while cloud hosting (Kimi Claw, MiniMax MaxClaw) trades control for convenience. Ecovacs' **"Eight Realms" (Bajie) butler robot** is positioned as the world's first home robot running the OpenClaw architecture, adding a physical robotic arm to perform tasks in 3D space (organizing items, delivering objects), priced above 10,000 yuan. Xiaomi's **miclaw** (built on the in-house MiMo model) and Huawei's **Xiaoyi** embed agent capabilities into mobile/IoT OS layers.

**Ecosystem Competition.** Tencent leverages WeChat/QQ as social entry points for QClaw; Alibaba's CoPaw binds to DingTalk; ByteDance's ArkClaw integrates with Lark and the Douyin API for e-commerce/livestream automation. NetEase pursues a cross-platform neutral stance. Capability differentiation: Kimi Claw and Baidu OpenClaw target knowledge retrieval (long-document analysis); Zhipu AutoClaude and Alibaba CoPaw target office automation (50+ preset skills); Tencent QClaw targets consumer life services. On open source: NetEase LobsterAI is the only fully MIT-licensed product; the majors (Tencent, Zhipu, Alibaba) run a "branded computer" closed-skill model; ByteDance's enterprise offering adds intranet-level identity and access controls.

**Business Model.** The post (partially paywalled) introduces the concept of "shrimp farming economics" — the real money in the OpenClaw wave goes to infrastructure and "shovel sellers," not end users.
