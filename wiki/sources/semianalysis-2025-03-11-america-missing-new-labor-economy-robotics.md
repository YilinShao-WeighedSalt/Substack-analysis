[[semianalysis]] argues that general-purpose robotics has reached an inflection point — finally viable after decades of failed attempts — and that China is positioned to capture this transformation entirely while the US stands structurally unprepared.

China now controls ~50% of the global industrial robot market (up from 30% in 2020) and installed 276,000 robot units in 2023, more than the traditional Western Big Four (FANUC, ABB, Yaskawa, KUKA) combined. Chinese humanoid companies raised $769M in 2023 and over $990M in H1 2024; Unitree's G1 humanoid is commercially available at $16,000 with zero American components, using a Rockchip RK3588S processor rather than Nvidia silicon. UBTech and Agibot are already in or approaching mass production.

The supply chain analysis is damning for Western competitiveness. China controls 90% of global neodymium magnet production and 93% of refinement, 80% of battery cells, 80% of DRC cobalt output, and 80% of Indonesia's battery-grade nickel. Building an equivalent robot arm costs ~2.2x more in the US than China. US factory construction for batteries runs 46% higher per GWh than Chinese counterparts. Northvolt (state-backed Sweden) filed for bankruptcy; LG paused its $5.5B Arizona battery plant.

For semiconductors, the post tracks localization risk across four categories. In MCUs, NXP faces the most direct competitive threat from Gigadevice and Geehy Semi among 60+ Chinese fabless entrants. Infineon is already losing Chinese OEM design wins. TXN and ADI hold high-margin analog positions that are increasingly exposed to customized Chinese alternatives. Nvidia's Orin chip dominates among Western robotics startups but the Unitree G1 case directly challenges its inevitability in the segment. Rockwell holds only ~7% of servo motors with limited robotics upside. The traditional industrial robot incumbents — FANUC (6954.T), Yaskawa (6506.T), and ABB — are characterized as complacent with low R&D ratios and rising China exposure. MP Materials is identified as a rare US-positive actor in rare earth supply chain reconstruction, though scale is 5–10 years away.

The post concludes that without a sustained multi-decade national strategy and domestic supply chain reconstruction, "all labor is handed over to China to own in perpetuity."
