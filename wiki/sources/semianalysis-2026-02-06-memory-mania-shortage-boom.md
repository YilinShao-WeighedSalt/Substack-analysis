[[semianalysis]] argues the current memory market is entering a structural shortage that is larger and longer-lasting than any cycle in the past forty years, driven by AI infrastructure demand rather than the consumer platform shifts that defined prior supercycles.

**Why this cycle is different.** DRAM scaling has effectively stalled: density has grown only ~2x over the past decade versus roughly 100x per decade historically. Capacitors are now storing tens of thousands of electrons, shrinking signal margins to near-physical limits. This means new supply requires entirely new fabs, not process shrinks — translating to 3–5 year lead times and billions in capital. Supply growth is now constrained by both capital discipline and physics simultaneously.

**Historical context.** The article traces four prior memory cycles: the 1993 Windows PC boom (DRAM per device up ~4x, gross margins above 50% before a 60%+ price collapse); the 2010 smartphone-plus-hyperscaler wave (LPDDR demand surge but limited pricing power); the 2017–2018 server cycle (record margins, followed by 2019 oversupply); and the COVID era (supply dislocations from logistics without aggressive capex expansion). Each cycle self-corrected within 18–24 months. The authors contend AI breaks that pattern.

**AI as structurally different demand.** HBM consumes approximately 3x the wafer capacity of standard DDR5 per bit delivered. With HBM now representing roughly 23% of all DRAM wafers and hyperscalers locking up 70% of available bits via long-term contracts in Q1 2026, conventional DRAM supply is being cannibalized. Contract prices surged 90–95% quarter-over-quarter in Q1 2026. DDR4 prices accelerated sharply from the April 2025 base. The authors state the supply-demand imbalance is deteriorating, not normalizing, and project tightness persisting through 2027.

**HBM4 competitive dynamics.** SK Hynix is identified as the dominant supplier: it holds approximately 70% of NVIDIA's Rubin HBM4 allocation, with HBM samples running at approximately 10 Gbps/pin matching spec. Samsung holds the remaining ~30% of Rubin allocation. Micron's HBM4 samples exhibited materially lower pin speeds, leading SemiAnalysis to conclude Micron receives zero allocation in the initial Rubin builds. Micron's base-die design using an internal DRAM process — rather than a leading-edge logic foundry — is cited as the root cause. A potential recovery depends on a base-die redesign targeting a Q2 2026 re-qualification window, but by then SK Hynix and Samsung will have locked in production slots.

**Supply discipline.** Post-COVID, all three major memory suppliers have shifted toward capital-return over bit-growth maximization, pricing for profitability rather than share. This structural behavioral change, combined with the physics constraints, means the usual mechanism for cycle termination — a flood of new capacity — arrives slower and less aggressively than in prior cycles.
