[[irrationalanalysis]] reacts to the launch of Semianalysis's InferenceMAX benchmark — an open-source LLM inference benchmarking platform — arguing that the data yields clear and surprisingly bullish conclusions for both Nvidia and AMD, though for very different reasons.

The author's methodology for reading the benchmark: focus on reasoning workloads (highest output-sequence-length to input-sequence-length ratio available), prefer FP8 over FP4 (the latter heavily favors Nvidia), and evaluate cost on a rental basis as the worst-case pricing scenario. Under this configuration, three models are compared: AMD performs surprisingly well despite significant technological disadvantages relative to Nvidia.

**Nvidia thesis:** The InferenceMAX data confirms that GB200 NVL72 inference economics are exceptional. The NVL72's system-level engineering advantages translate directly into massive profitability for operators running Nvidia hardware. The author holds approximately $240K in Nvidia shares (average cost basis ~$13.91/share) and ~$87K in Nvidia call options across various strikes and expirations, and calls Nvidia "comically undervalued" given the demand backdrop and hardware superiority on the most important workloads. Nvidia published its own blog post highlighting the InferenceMAX results the same day.

**AMD thesis:** AMD's competitive performance on the benchmark, despite a roughly 22-percentage-point gross margin disadvantage versus Nvidia, is explained by the OpenAI warrant deal. The author examines the AMD SEC 8-K filing showing that OpenAI received warrants (effectively call options on AMD shares) tied to purchasing AMD hardware. The structure functions as a floating discount: because OpenAI receives equity value tied to AMD's stock price, they are compensated for the margin AMD cedes to compete. The author draws a parallel to Amazon's use of warrant deals with Astera Labs, Marvell, Fabrinet, and AAOI, but characterizes the OpenAI–AMD arrangement as more impactful in scale. The last warrant tranche vests at a $600/share AMD strike price, which the author takes as a concrete near-term price target implied by the deal structure. The author holds zero economic exposure to AMD but is nonetheless constructive on AMD given the warrant-driven pricing logic.

**Key takeaway:** Warrant deals are "sneaky discounts" that distort reported gross margins and should be understood as effective product discounts. OpenAI, with 30–300 estimated internal inference engineers, knows its own workload economics precisely and engineered the warrant to make AMD hardware economically viable — a rational and sophisticated move.
