[[globalsemiresearch]] examines the emerging threat of Chinese indium phosphide (InP) substrate producers to the incumbent oligopoly, drawing a direct parallel to the silicon carbide industry where companies like Tianyu, Sanan, and TankeBlue systematically displaced Western players through aggressive capacity expansion and cost reduction.

## Demand Context

Goldman Sachs projects 800G optical module shipments exceeding 34 million units in 2026, with 1.6T modules surpassing 25 million units. Each module requires InP substrates for laser chip fabrication. The post highlights a critical and underappreciated dynamic: CW (Continuous Wave) laser power requirements for CPO (Co-Packaged Optics) and OIO (Optical I/O) architectures are escalating from 70–100mW toward 200–400mW. Higher power devices require larger die areas and suffer dramatically lower per-wafer yields — a 3-inch wafer producing 400mW CW sources yields only ~200 dies vs. several times more for 70mW devices. This means the migration to silicon photonics may not reduce InP consumption as widely assumed; it could paradoxically increase it. 2026 new demand is estimated at 100,000–200,000+ wafers in 3-inch equivalent terms.

## Incumbent Oligopoly

Three players control over 90% of global InP substrate supply: Sumitomo Electric (~42% share, ~800,000 wafer annual capacity), AXT/Beijing Tongmei (~36% share, ~300,000 wafer capacity), and JX Nippon Mining & Metals (~13% share, ~200,000 wafer output). All three are capacity-constrained: Sumitomo is vertically integrating into optical chips and consuming supply internally; AXT is running near full utilization at ~25,000–26,000 wafers/month; JX Metals has no announced expansion plans. The real shortage is concentrated in 3-inch and 4-inch high-quality semi-insulating substrates. 6-inch wafers remain in R&D.

## The Chinese Threat

The structural setup mirrors the SiC industry trajectory. Chinese producers are ramping capacity aggressively. The key barrier is not furnace equipment (supply is adequate and costs manageable in China) but process control during crystal growth — achieving consistent yields across the boule. Chinese producers currently lag significantly on yield versus incumbents. However, the post argues the strategic pieces are falling into place faster than most global analysts recognize. Downstream customers are aggressively locking in supply through framework agreements amid export control uncertainty, inflating short-term order data but signaling the severity of supply anxiety.

## Investment Implications

The post is constructive on the structural InP demand thesis but raises supply chain disruption risk for Western incumbents if Chinese producers close the yield gap. AXTI and Sumitomo face a medium-term threat analogous to what happened to Western SiC players. Near-term, all incumbents benefit from tight supply and rising prices (Chinese market prices rose ~20% YoY in early 2026).
