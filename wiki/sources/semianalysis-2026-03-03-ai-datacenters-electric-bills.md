[[semianalysis]] argues that the ~15–20% surge in electricity bills for PJM-area households is driven primarily by flawed capacity market design, not by AI datacenter load growth per se — while Texas (ERCOT) has absorbed equivalent AI buildout with minimal price disruption.

## The PJM Capacity Market Problem

PJM's Base Residual Auction (BRA) cleared at $270/MW-day for the 2025/26 delivery period — a 9.3x increase over the prior year's $29/MW-day — and has since hit a FERC-imposed cap of $329/MW-day in consecutive auctions. This single auction mechanism translates to roughly $16 billion in total capacity payments annually, or ~$25–30/month per household above the pre-AI-boom baseline. The price is set not by a real market but by PJM's proprietary Variable Resource Requirement (VRR) curve, an artificial supply-demand schedule driven by internal load forecasts. SemiAnalysis contends that PJM's datacenter load projections are systematically too high — PJM cut its own datacenter load forecast by 800 MW in 2024 and another 1.1 GW in 2025 relative to the prior year's outlook — and that construction delays, GPU supply chain bottlenecks, and platform bring-up issues mean real datacenter load will underperform the forecast inputs that drove the 9x price spike.

## ERCOT: Same Demand Shock, No Price Crisis

ERCOT operates an energy-only market with real-time scarcity pricing (ORDC) instead of a centralized annual capacity auction. Despite projecting 77.9 GW of potential datacenter load by 2030 — more than double the prior year's forecast — ERCOT applied haircuts of ~50% to developer interconnection requests and pushed assumed in-service dates back 180 days. Forward energy prices in Texas rose only 11–17%, similar to PJM's forward energy market (distinct from PJM's capacity market). ERCOT's single-state regulatory structure allows it to move faster on market reforms; PJM must navigate FERC and 13 states.

## Winter Storm Fern Stress Test (January 2026)

The storm validated ERCOT's approach: Texas maintained adequate reserves, no emergency procedures were triggered, and real-time prices peaked around $300/MWh. PJM lost ~21 GW of generation capacity (15% of the capacity-market fleet) to frozen equipment. Real-time PJM prices averaged $700/MWh system-wide, with Virginia's datacenter-heavy Dominion zone spiking to $1,800/MWh. The DOE issued Section 202(c) emergency orders authorizing ~35 GW of backup datacenter generation — none of it eligible for the BRA — to supplement the grid. High capacity prices bought forecasted risk, not actual winterization.

## Supply Chain Winners (Paywalled Analysis)

The shifting bottlenecks create investment opportunities in onsite gas generation equipment (GE Vernova, Caterpillar, Bloom Energy), datacenter power infrastructure suppliers (Vertiv), and IPPs with PJM merchant exposure (Vistra, Constellation, Talen Energy). The report frames these as "winners" from a combination of structurally elevated capacity prices and the growing value of reliable, on-site backup generation that Winter Storm Fern elevated into an "untapped asset class."
