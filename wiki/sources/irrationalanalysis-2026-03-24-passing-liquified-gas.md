[[irrationalanalysis]] takes a detour from semiconductors to examine the liquefied natural gas (LNG) market amid escalating Middle East conflict, centering on a specific event: Iran retaliating against Israeli strikes on Iranian gas infrastructure by attacking Qatari LNG facilities, which knocked out roughly 17% of Qatar's LNG export capacity for an estimated 3–5 years (per QatarEnergy statements as of March 19, 2026). Trump, described as panicking, subsequently pressured Israel to stand down — a reaction the author reads as a tell for how significant the supply disruption is.

The post explains the structural features that make LNG uniquely sensitive to geopolitical shocks: it requires highly specialized liquefaction terminals (expensive, slow to build or repair), dedicated cryogenic tankers, and has a concentrated producer base compared to oil. Demand from Japan, South Korea, China, and a large portion of the EU is largely inelastic because LNG underpins heating and electricity generation. On top of the Qatar supply hit, European gas storage sits at historic lows heading into the period, leaving little buffer if further disruptions materialize.

The United States is the world's largest LNG exporter, and the author argues it is the natural beneficiary if Asian buyers must re-source supply away from Qatar. He highlights **Cheniere Energy (LNG)** as the pure-play US LNG exporter and frames it as a "war profiteering" trade: if the Strait of Hormuz remains disrupted or more Qatari infrastructure is destroyed, Asian buyers bidding for American LNG would spike prices dramatically, directly benefiting Cheniere's export volumes and contract premiums.

The author is candid that this is a "shitpost" written during a macro-driven drawdown (down ~$540K in 25 days on his "safe" account), and that he generally loses money when he leaves semiconductors. He frames the piece as a vacation from his usual semiconductor coverage while awaiting macro clarity.

Separately, the post teases two upcoming projects: a large OFC (Optical Fiber Conference) recap post expected by April 4–5, and a piece on AI hardware startups that is being held up by MatX, which the author criticizes for making vague public claims (weights in SRAM, KV cache in HBM, splittable systolic arrays) and refusing to provide substantive engineering detail.
