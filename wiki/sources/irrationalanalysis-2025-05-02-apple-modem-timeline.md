[[irrationalanalysis]] covers Qualcomm's Q2 FY25 earnings call with a focus on the accelerated Apple internal modem ramp and its implications for Qualcomm's business. The author entered the quarter with a large short position in QCOM, covered it on earnings day for a profit, and plans to re-short as any bear-market rally continues.

The key earnings call data point: September 2025 iPhone launches will use Apple's internal C1 modem in approximately 30% of units. Prior consensus expected only the iPhone Air to carry the C1; the new disclosure implies both the base iPhone and iPhone Air will ship with the Apple modem, while the Pro and Pro Max retain the Qualcomm modem. This is a faster-than-expected displacement timeline.

The author argues Qualcomm management got six years of extension (since signing the Apple supply deal in 2019) because Apple's modem program repeatedly stumbled. That runway has now expired. Diversification efforts into Windows on Arm and automotive have not progressed fast enough to offset the impending revenue hole — Windows on Arm is described as "a joke of a state," and auto success may be partially attributable to Mobileye's underperformance rather than Qualcomm's own strength.

A notable secondary signal: the author observes a wave of Qualcomm engineers departing for Apple, including several senior individuals. The timing — shortly after Qualcomm would have completed a detailed competitive teardown of the iPhone 16e C1 modem (estimated complete by end of March 2025) — is read as insiders recognizing the competitive situation is dire.

On the sell-side dynamics: Deutsche Bank's Ross Seymore asked whether Qualcomm would be reducing headcount given the faster modem ramp, which the author interprets as buy-side clients telegraphing concern about Qualcomm's cost structure through their sell-side proxy. Management's deflective, word-salad answer is treated as a confirmation that cuts are coming.

Beneficiaries identified: SiTime and CEVA both benefit from a faster Apple modem ramp (Apple is a customer for both timing and DSP IP). The author holds SiTime but is margin-constrained. CEVA is on the buy list but portfolio leverage prevents adding the position currently. Separately, the author holds a large NVDA call option position, though this is disclosed as a margin/portfolio management note rather than a new thesis.

The post closes with career advice to engineers at publicly traded companies to read earnings transcripts and never take internal all-hands communications at face value.
