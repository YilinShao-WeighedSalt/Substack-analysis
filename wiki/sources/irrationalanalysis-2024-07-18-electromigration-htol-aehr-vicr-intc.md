This post from [[irrationalanalysis]] uses semiconductor reliability engineering concepts — electromigration (EM) and High Temperature Operating Life (HTOL) testing — as the analytical lens for examining three connected investment situations: AEHR, VICR, and INTC.

**Electromigration & HTOL background.** Electromigration is the degradation mechanism in semiconductors driven by high current, voltage, and especially temperature. HTOL testing (80–240 chips at 125°C for 1,000 hours at full load) is the standard methodology to qualify chip reliability for a 10-year operating life. HTOL re-qualification is required when porting to a new process node, making non-trivial architectural changes, or entering new markets with different operating conditions. This framework underpins all three investment theses in the post.

**AEHR — bearish/short setup.** Aehr Test Systems (wafer-level burn-in for SiC, GaN, silicon-photonics) announced the acquisition of Incal, a packaged-part HTOL test equipment vendor. The author argues these two businesses are completely dissimilar — different engineering goals, different financials, zero synergy. Management's framing that AI chips will require high-volume production burn-in is called out as "the most gaslighting, nonsensical, misleading bullshit" the author has read. The thesis is that the acquisition exists to plug a ~$12M revenue gap that would have caused guidance to miss street expectations. Multiple participants on the earnings call reportedly called it a contrived revenue boost. The author flags this as a potential short with further diligence.

**VICR — speculative read.** Vicor (DC-DC and AC-DC power components; competes with MPWR, ADI, TXN) has recently seen unusual stock strength. The author's key insight: Vicor has reportedly alienated major customers due to reliability issues, and no sophisticated customer will run high-volume programs without an HTOL report for the specific part. The call-to-action is: find the HTOL report and you have the answer on whether the stock will collapse or re-rate upward.

**INTC — tail-risk warning.** Intel's 13th and 14th generation high-core-count Raptor Lake desktop CPUs are failing in the field as early as six months of operation, with early EM-induced failure localized to the ring bus. Datacenter providers are already raising warranty pricing. If the EM defect is confined to high-end desktop SKUs, the financial impact is manageable. If the defect is latent in laptop SKUs as well, Intel faces a potential multi-billion-dollar pre-tax charge.
