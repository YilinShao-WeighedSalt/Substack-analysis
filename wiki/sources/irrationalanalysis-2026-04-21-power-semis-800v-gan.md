[[irrationalanalysis]] argues that high-voltage GaN (650V+ continuous, 800V transient) represents a genuinely differentiated segment within the otherwise commoditized power semiconductors market. The catalyst is the shift to 800V DC and ±400V datacenter power architectures, which dramatically expands GaN's addressable market beyond the low-margin smartphone fast-charger space where it has languished for over a decade.

The key insight is fungibility, or rather the lack of it. In conventional power semi markets (e.g., SiC in EVs), mediocre parts can be compensated by adding more phases. In next-generation datacenter power delivery, board footprint is severely constrained, eliminating that workaround and creating a genuine performance moat for top-tier parts. The author identifies only six viable players at high voltage vs. 20+ at lower voltages, making the competitive landscape far more favorable.

A detailed technical comparison is conducted across Navitas/Infineon, Renesas, ROHM, Nexperia, Innoscience, and Texas Instruments on four dimensions: Rds_on (on-state resistance), power dissipation vs. temperature, safe operating area (including switching speed), and transistor characteristic curves. At a headline spec level, Navitas (NVTS) and TI are tied on Rds_on. On power dissipation (a more realistic metric at operating temperatures of 100°C+), Navitas and Innoscience lead. Infineon's part stands out for extremely fast 20 ns switching vs. the 1–10 µs range of peers.

The author concludes that Navitas and TI have a meaningful and defensible advantage in high-power GaN. Navitas's GaNSafe integrated safety and driver features — initially dismissed as marketing — are deemed genuinely valuable and differentiated, reducing external component count and simplifying system design. Infineon's integrated-driver GaN is rated second tier on performance. Renesas is rated poor due to high gate threshold voltage. ON Semi (ON) has vertical GaN technology in development but no shipping product or public datasheet — described as a "pretty brochure." The author bought NVTS and ON on the day of writing, citing the vertical GaN optionality at ON.
