# Molybdenum vs. Tungsten: The Metal Swap Rewiring the Memory Supply Chain

Sequel to the WF6 tungsten-gas piece. Subject: NAND makers replacing **tungsten** with **molybdenum** in 3D NAND **word-lines** (the horizontal conductive layers addressing each memory cell). All three major NAND makers now have molybdenum on production roadmaps; first mass-production lines convert this year.

**Why tungsten ran out of road (past ~300 layers):** (1) as word-lines thin each generation, tungsten resistance climbs sharply, and it needs a titanium-nitride **barrier layer** to adhere — a liner eating the shrinking conductor space; (2) tungsten is deposited via **WF6**, whose released **fluorine** corrodes surrounding oxide → voids/leakage. Manageable at 100 layers; a hard cap at 300+.

**What molybdenum fixes (both at once):** resistance drops >50% in 300+-layer structures; needs **no barrier layer** (whole cross-section conducts; thinner layers → lower stack height, less wafer warping); leading precursor **molybdenum dichloride dioxide (MoO2Cl2)** contains **no fluorine** → corrosion problem eliminated at source. Kioxia VLSI-2024 data: molybdenum word-lines cut leakage failures to **~1/25th** of tungsten while tolerating tighter pitch; faster program/erase/read. "Molybdenum is what lets NAND makers keep stacking, and stacking is the whole business model."

**Investable angle (paywalled):** upstream — **molybdenum precursor** suppliers, **delivery hardware**, and the **qualification moat** (once a fab certifies a chemistry supplier, switching is slow/risky → protects incumbent pricing). NAND makers themselves benefit only indirectly.

Contrast with WF6: that was a *time-limited supply squeeze* (China export controls, relief ~mid-2027); molybdenum is a *durable, physics-driven technology transition* (stickier qualification moat).
