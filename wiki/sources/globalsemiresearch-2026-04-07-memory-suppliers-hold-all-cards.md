[[globalsemiresearch]] challenges the prevailing bear thesis that Long-Term Agreements (LTAs) are a cycle-peak warning sign for memory suppliers, arguing instead that structural changes in demand and contract design have fundamentally inverted the historical power dynamic — now firmly in favor of suppliers like Micron and SanDisk.

**The conventional bear case.** Some experienced memory analysts treat LTA announcements as a sell signal, citing prior cycles in which customers routinely abandoned agreements when prices fell, exploiting fragmented supplier competition to renegotiate. Under this view, Micron's five-year LTA announcements and SanDisk's recent agreements merely signal supplier desperation at a market top.

**Why this cycle is structurally different.** The author identifies three breaks from history. First, the demand-side composition has shifted: consumer electronics, smartphones, and PCs — the historical buyers — have been displaced by data centers as the dominant customer class. Data center operators building AI infrastructure require massive, predictable capacity; supply security is existential to their business model rather than a procurement convenience. Second, modern LTAs have genuine teeth. Many now include upfront prepayments that lock both volume and price (with floor protections), converting what were once gentlemen's agreements into financial commitments with real walk-away costs for buyers. Third, and most contrarian: if AI-driven demand continues to outpace supply, LTAs become non-binding on suppliers too — who can simply reprioritize to higher-bidding customers, exactly as buyers did in prior downturns. The optionality is now asymmetric, favoring sellers.

**The asymmetry argument.** In a soft market, improved contract structures and prepayments protect supplier revenue. In a tight market, suppliers retain full pricing power and can de-prioritize contracted volumes in favor of spot buyers. Bears who focus on customer walk-away risk in downturns miss the mirror scenario: supplier walk-away in upturns. LTAs do not cause outcomes — supply/demand dynamics do. The agreements are symptoms documenting the power shift, not determinants of it.

**HBM supply scarcity.** The piece highlights high-bandwidth memory (HBM) as the focal point of structural tightness, given its centrality to AI accelerator stacks. Customer urgency to lock in HBM capacity via LTAs reflects an acknowledgment of scarcity that may ironically prove moot if suppliers can command better terms on the open market.

**Bottom line.** The real analytical question is whether AI infrastructure will consume memory capacity at projected rates. If yes, LTAs are artifacts of a buyer scramble — not constraints on supplier upside.
