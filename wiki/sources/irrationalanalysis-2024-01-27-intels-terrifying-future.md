[[irrationalanalysis]] uses Intel's disastrous Q4 FY2023 earnings — a $12.7B revenue guide vs. the $14.3B sell-side consensus — as the lens for a broader thesis: Intel is the last leading-edge IDM (integrated device manufacturer), and that model is now fatally strained.

The post opens with a history of AMD's own IDM crisis. Through the 2000s, AMD's internal fabs chronically underperformed Intel. The 2008 financial crisis forced a reckoning: AMD spun out GlobalFoundries in 2009, endured years of painful Wafer Supply Agreement constraints, and only truly recovered after GloFo abandoned leading-edge nodes in 2018, freeing AMD to consolidate on TSMC. Under Lisa Su, AMD's Zen and CDNA3 architectures have made it a formidable competitor in both CPU and GPU markets.

The author argues Intel faces the same structural trap AMD did, but hasn't yet pulled the trigger on a breakup. Earnings call analysis is scathing: management blamed FPGA/PSG inventory correction and "seasonality" for the guidance miss, while analysts from Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, and Deutsche Bank pushed back, noting the numbers imply ongoing CPU core market share losses. Pat Gelsinger's non-answers are called out as corporate deflection. The BoA analyst (Vivek Arya) explicitly frames the issue: nearly all datacenter capex growth is flowing to NVIDIA and AMD GPUs, leaving little room for Intel CPU growth.

On the client (PC/laptop) side, Intel's CCG faces intensifying pressure. Meteor Lake (Intel Core Ultra) launched late with firmware bugs, an underclocked ring bus, and IPC regressions vs. Raptor Lake Refresh. AMD's upcoming Strix and Qualcomm's Snapdragon X Elite are expected to outperform it in both performance and battery life. Qualcomm's WoA exclusivity with Microsoft reportedly expires in 2024, after which NVIDIA, Samsung, and MediaTek may also enter the PC market in 2025.

Intel's response — separating design and manufacturing P&L under an "internal foundry model" — is viewed as a positive accountability measure but potentially too late. The author anticipates the separate P&L will expose design underperformance relative to manufacturing (which has genuinely improved under Dr. Ann Kelleher), inviting activist investor pressure to force a full breakup. Management's "pipe cleaning" synergy argument (that internal design must stay unified with fabs for yield learning) is undermined by Apple and TSMC's successful external lead-customer relationship.

The conclusion is stark: Intel design likely cannot survive as a standalone company, and the unified IDM model is failing in real time.
