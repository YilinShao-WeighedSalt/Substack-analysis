[[irrationalanalysis]] opens with Intel's Q4 2025 earnings reaction — stock down ~12% after a large pre-earnings run-up — framing it as a self-inflicted catastrophe. The post first takes a detour to mock "The Information" for a viral tweet comparing Intel's valuation to TSMC using EBITDA, an inappropriate metric for capital-intensive fab businesses where depreciation is enormous. The author argues Intel's low price/book ratio reflects a "book on fire" from years of compounding engineering and strategic mistakes, not a cheap-vs-TSMC situation.

The core thesis is a two-layer failure at Intel. First, **18A yield** is genuinely bad — Intel investor relations had previously claimed defect density below 0.4, but management is now openly admitting yields are not where they need to be. Second, and more damning, is Intel's **chiplet fungibility failure**. AMD has for years designed CPU chiplets so that the same leading-edge die can be redeployed across consumer and server products, maximizing yield absorption and supply flexibility. Intel's Panther Lake (consumer) uses chiplets that cannot be reused in datacenter products. The author attributes this to internal politics — design teams maximizing headcount and design work rather than adopting the AMD-style maximize-reuse-and-fungibility approach.

The combination of these two failures explains a specific embarrassment from the call: CFO Zinsner admitted Intel is sitting on **$11.6B in inventory** yet cannot ship the datacenter server CPUs customers need. The agentic AI wave has driven an unexpected structural surge in server CPU demand (LLM inference requires many CPUs alongside GPUs), and Intel is unable to capture it because the right chiplets don't exist in quantity. Analyst Stacy Rasgon landed the kill shot: "You have your own factories. How does that happen?"

The author is bullish on AMD as the primary beneficiary. AMD's chiplet architecture is fungible, its execution has been superior for 7+ years (since 2nd-gen EPYC), and the datacenter CPU share gains ahead should be significant. The post also hints that AMD porting IP to Samsung SF2 could happen before Intel credibly defends its datacenter position. Micron is mentioned approvingly in passing — the company deleted its consumer Crucial brand to redirect all supply toward the high-margin datacenter channel, a sign of how tight datacenter memory demand is.
