[[semianalysis]] examines whether TSMC's overseas fab expansion — spanning Arizona, Japan (JASM), and Germany (ESMC) — can overcome the structural cost disadvantages that founder Morris Chang warned about, drawing on supply chain details, fab economics, and operational data from the three ventures.

**Morris Chang's skepticism and WaferTech precedent.** Chang called U.S. semiconductor subsidies "a very expensive exercise in futility," arguing overseas fabs cannot replicate Taiwan's integrated ecosystem. The 1996 WaferTech joint venture in Washington state confirmed his concern: despite producing identical products, U.S. margins ran 20–25% below Taiwan operations. The experience took four years to normalize and entrenched TSMC's internal skepticism about offshore manufacturing economics.

**Taiwan's irreplaceable cluster advantages.** TSMC's competitive moat rests on Hsinchu Science Park, home to 189 semiconductor companies — 95 IC design firms, 17 foundries, and 17 test/packaging houses — representing ~60% of Taiwan's semiconductor output. Executives call it the "one-hour semiconductor ecosystem": suppliers, partners, and fabs are all within 60 minutes. This proximity enables 24/7 troubleshooting and rapid process iteration that Arizona cannot replicate: over 90% of TSMC's 83,000+ employees are Taiwanese, and 88% of management-level positions are held by Taiwanese staff.

**Arizona supply chain vulnerabilities.** Most tier-one raw material suppliers have not established Arizona facilities, leaving the fab dependent on Asian sourcing. A contaminated-process-gas incident involving Linde caused a multi-million dollar scrap event that collapsed Q3 fab profit from $140M to $1.4M — illustrating the fragility of a supply chain not yet localized. Permitting timelines in the U.S. run roughly twice as long as in Taiwan, extending project schedules further.

**Operational upside.** Despite structural headwinds, Arizona's 4nm production achieved yields 4 percentage points above comparable Taiwan operations in initial production, demonstrating that TSMC can execute advanced-node manufacturing overseas. Apple and AMD were among the first customers on the Arizona 4nm node. TSMC has announced $165B in U.S. expansion plans, with rumors of up to 12 Arizona phases by 2032 — a trajectory that would require substantial local supply chain development and sustained government subsidization to be economically viable.

**Japan (JASM) and Germany (ESMC).** JASM, targeting 28nm/16nm with Sony as primary customer for CMOS image sensors and automotive applications, entered high-volume production in 2025 with a Phase 2 planned at 7nm-class with EUV. ESMC targets the same 28nm/16nm nodes for European automotive customers, with Bosch as the primary anchor.

**Bottom line.** Overseas expansion succeeds operationally but remains structurally more expensive than Taiwan. The fundamental cost disadvantage Chang identified appears unavoidable without sustained government subsidization. Taiwan retains 90% of global advanced-node capacity and 60% of total semiconductor output — a concentration that overseas investment dents only at the margin.
