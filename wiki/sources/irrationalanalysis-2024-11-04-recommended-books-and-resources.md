[[irrationalanalysis]] published this curated resource guide in response to requests from high-school and undergraduate students looking to break into semiconductor industry analysis and engineering. The post is organized into four categories: Substack newsletters, YouTube channels, bespoke/niche websites, and textbooks.

**Substack Newsletters:** The author recommends SemiAnalysis (Dylan Patel) and Fabricated Knowledge (Doug O'Laughlin) as essential reads, noting that while paid subscriptions are expensive, free tiers still offer meaningful content. Also highlighted are Chips and Cheese for deep hardware dives, Vik's Newsletter for communication technology engineering insights, and the Asianometry Newsletter (Jon Y) for broad semiconductor and Asia coverage.

**YouTube Channels:** 3blue1brown's linear algebra series is described as "life-changing" and essential mathematical grounding across engineering disciplines. Asianometry (Jon Y) is called the best general semiconductor channel for combining variety with depth. More than Moore / TechTechPotato (Dr. Ian Cutress) is praised particularly for its interviews with industry figures like Mike Clark and Dr. Ann Kelleher. The Signal Path is recommended for RF and electrical engineering, and Gamers Nexus for factory tours and technical hardware content.

**Bespoke/Niche Websites:** Recommendations include SemiEngineering for industry developments, a lighterra.com article on modern microprocessor architecture for CPU fundamentals, RealWorldTech (David Kanter) for deep technical analysis, and a Moore Elite die-yield calculator for practical fab estimation. AnandTech is noted as a former great resource now defunct.

**Textbooks:** The author covers linear algebra, probability/random processes, DSP (basic and advanced), analog/mixed-signal, digital design, and niche topics. Personal favorites include Oppenheim & Schafer's *Discrete-Time Signal Processing*, Gray et al.'s *Analysis and Design of Analog Integrated Circuits*, and Dally & Harting's *Digital Design: A Systems Approach*. A pointed warning is given against campus bookstores, noting that "unauthorized" Indian-edition imports available online are often the same content at 10–20% of list price.
