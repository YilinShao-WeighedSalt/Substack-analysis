[[semianalysis]] authors Malcolm Spittler and Dylan Patel argue that AI is generating real economic value that national accounting systems cannot capture — a phenomenon they term "Dark Output." The piece positions this as a measurement crisis analogous to the Solow paradox of the computer revolution, but potentially far larger in scale.

## Core Framework

The authors define two distinct categories of Dark Output. **Substitution Dark Output** occurs when AI automates existing tasks and prices collapse so dramatically that transactions vanish from statistics rather than register as productivity gains. Their example: AI compressed the cost of drafting a basic legal will from $150 to $0.50 in a single year — a 99%+ decline that causes the economic activity to disappear from datasets entirely. **New Dark Output** covers tasks that become economical for the first time (e.g., literature reviews dropping from $2,000 to $2), generating value with no prior baseline to measure displacement against.

## Why Services Are Hard to Measure

Manufacturing productivity is tractable because units are standardized. Services lack such units — there is no "metric ton of legal advice." The BLS legal services price index, tracked since 1987, has risen 4.6x as of September 2024, functioning effectively as an employment cost index with no adjustment for productivity. This structural gap in measurement infrastructure means AI-driven productivity gains in services will systematically understate in GDP statistics.

## Evidence Ladder and Market Signals

The authors propose a six-tier "Evidence Ladder" prioritizing real-world deployment evidence over academic benchmarks: from benchmark performance at the bottom up to court cases validating AI work and insurance underwriting AI risk at the top. Using this framework they identify roughly $1.5T in tasks that current AI could substantially augment or automate, describing this as "exposed labor" rather than confirmed displacement.

Employment in AI-exposed sectors is falling relative to the broader economy while wages in those same sectors are rising — consistent with junior staff displacement raising average wages without actual raises occurring. Meanwhile, 37% of token usage concentrates in computers and mathematics sectors, yet software investment contributions to GDP show no deviation from pre-AI trends, illustrating the measurement gap.

## Macro and Policy Stakes

Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh acknowledged in December 2025 that macro data are "backward looking" and will fail to capture non-inflationary AI-driven growth, forcing policymakers to "make a bet" without complete information. If AI costs (data centers, GPUs, electricity, inference spend) remain highly visible while output stays invisible, investors and policymakers risk mischaracterizing genuine productivity transformation as speculative bubble activity. The measurement problem is also distributionally skewed: administrative work is 72% female, meaning displacement effects may concentrate in high female-employment occupations while remaining statistically invisible.
