[[semianalysis]] delivers a comprehensive autopsy of Intel's decline, arguing the company faces an existential crisis rooted in board incompetence, cultural rot, and a product strategy that has lost its moats — while contending that Intel Foundry is worth saving at almost any cost.

**Board and Leadership Failures.** Seven of Intel's 11 board members lack semiconductor experience, with only Stacy J. Smith having relevant credentials. The decision to fire CEO Pat Gelsinger is framed as emblematic of a board that cannot evaluate technical talent. The rot began earlier: choosing Paul Otellini (non-engineer) over Gelsinger as CEO in 2005 dismantled Andy Grove's "constructive confrontation" engineering culture and replaced it with political fiefdoms. Brian Krzanich presided over the catastrophic 10nm delay and is labelled Intel's worst CEO. Bob Swan, the first truly non-technical leader, prioritized financial engineering — $36B in stock buybacks versus only $38B in capex during a critical competitive window — further hollowing out the manufacturing advantage.

**x86 Moat Erosion.** Apple's 2020 M-series launch, enabled by Rosetta 2 emulation, broke the software lock that kept x86 indispensable for PCs. Qualcomm, Nvidia, MediaTek, and AMD are all developing Arm-based PC CPUs, turning the client CPU market into a five-heavyweight fight. In the datacenter, AWS Graviton now accounts for over 50% of all new CPU capacity added in the last two years; Google, Microsoft, Alibaba, and Meta are deploying custom Arm silicon at scale. Nvidia's GB200 uses the Arm-based Grace CPU rather than Intel Xeon, a symbolic and practical displacement. Intel also wasted silicon area — using roughly 2x more die area than AMD, Nvidia, and Qualcomm in comparable products — reflecting process and design inefficiency.

**Intel Foundry as Strategic Asset.** The authors argue the foundry business is Intel's genuine strategic value. The 18A process (gate-all-around transistors plus backside power delivery) could become the best non-TSMC logic node available. National security concerns are concrete: the 2021 chip shortage cost the U.S. roughly 1% of GDP (~$240B). The required investment is steep — approximately $36.5B in equipment over three years plus $15-20B+ in facilities — and the GlobalFoundries spinoff precedent (AMD's fab lost $22.4B over a decade) shows the risks of undercapitalized foundry independence.

**Recommended Path.** The analysis calls for selling Intel's product divisions (Client x86, Mobileye/MBLY, Altera) to private equity or strategic buyers, spinning out Intel Foundry as an independent entity capitalized at roughly $50B, securing anchor manufacturing agreements with major U.S. semiconductor companies, and pursuing government support on national-security grounds.
