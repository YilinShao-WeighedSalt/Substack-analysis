[[semianalysis]] traces the Apple-TSMC foundry relationship across five phases from 2010 to the present, drawing on proprietary wafer demand and foundry industry models to quantify the power dynamics, node economics, and shifting competitive landscape as AI reshapes semiconductor manufacturing.

**The anchor-tenant model.** Apple's annual spend at TSMC grew from $2B in 2014 to $24B in 2025 (12x in 12 years), and Apple held >50% share of every major TSMC node launch since 20nm — in some cases near 100%. Apple's manufacturing purchase obligations scaled from $8.7B in 2010 to $71B in 2022. This anchor-tenant relationship enabled TSMC to fund $98B in capex from 2019–2022 alone, exceeding cumulative spend of the prior 14 years. Without the iPhone's 200M annual unit baseline, TSMC could not have sustained the R&D velocity that left Intel and Samsung Foundry behind.

**Platform shift to HPC.** TSMC's HPC revenue rose from 36% in 2020 to 58% in 2025; smartphone revenue fell from 46% to 29%. SemiAnalysis's model shows Nvidia will consume more N3 wafers than Apple by 4Q27, and Apple's N2 share drops to 48% — the first time in a decade Apple is not the dominant customer on a new node. However, the post argues this is not permanent displacement: TSMC's A16 (1.6nm) is HPC-centric and smartphones will skip it, while A14 (1.4nm) is designed for both mobile and HPC from the start. Apple is forecast to reclaim 67% node share on A14, its highest since N3.

**Dual anchor tenants.** TSMC now has two anchor customers instead of one: Apple provides predictable baseline demand that justifies fixed fab costs; Nvidia provides high-margin upside through CoWoS packaging (revenue hit $9.6B in 2025 vs. Apple's InFO at $3.5B+). This bifurcation shifts TSMC's capex between Moore's Law scaling (2nm for Apple) and packaging density (CoWoS-L for Nvidia).

**Apple silicon economics.** Mac gross margin expanded from ~29% to 39.5% after ditching Intel — an 11pp expansion. iPhone GM grew 5pp from A4 to A18. Annual chip savings exceed $7B across displaced Intel CPU, Qualcomm modem, and Broadcom connectivity revenues. Total Apple silicon revenue at TSMC was $23.5B in 2025 (A-series: $9.7B, M-series: $4.9B, others).

**Phase 5 — beyond TSMC.** Intel's 18A-P process (shipping late 2026) is discussed as the first theoretically viable alternative since 2016. A 20% base M-series shift to Intel 18A-P would imply ~$630M in foundry revenue for Intel. Apple has also re-engaged Samsung Foundry for CMOS image sensors at Austin, Texas, potentially capturing 20–30% of CIS volume by 2027 ($1–1.5B revenue for Samsung). Leading-edge A/M-series chips remain exclusive to TSMC for the foreseeable future; diversification targets are PMICs, display drivers, audio/connectivity, and image sensors. Apple's C1 modem (shipped iPhone 16e, 2025) is on track to fully displace Qualcomm across the iPhone lineup by 2027–2028.
