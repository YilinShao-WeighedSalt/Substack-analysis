[[irrationalanalysis]] covers a chaotic earnings season across five names — Lumentum, Coherent, SiTime, Qualcomm, and ARM — with pointed technical and fundamental commentary.

**Lumentum (LITE):** The author had been an OCS bear but acknowledges Lumentum executed well. Key points: 200G/lane EML at double ASP is margin-accretive; Lumentum's San Jose fab has a performance lead on high-power EML that the Japan fab cannot replicate; subsea applications demand narrow linewidth which LITE can deliver; they secured long-term agreements eliminating quarterly cost-down negotiations while simultaneously raising prices on incremental capacity — a structurally favorable dynamic. A "multiple hundreds of millions of dollars" OCS backlog was explicitly disclosed with quarterly ramp timing, which is viewed as highly credible.

**Coherent (COHR):** Author is skeptical and combative. COHR's CEO claims 200G/lane VCSEL will work and touts 6-inch InP yield, but simultaneously continues buying EML from Lumentum — a logical contradiction. The liquid-crystal OCS has high optical insertion loss vs. Lumentum's competing product. COHR reported 10 customer engagements for OCS but gave no revenue or orders, in contrast to LITE's hard backlog. The author calls COHR's 200G VCSEL unreliable and doubts their 6-in InP can yield EML at scale.

**SiTime (SITM):** Author holds a large position and was positively surprised. The key thesis: 200G-per-lane transceivers require a 312.5 MHz local oscillator, which is difficult for quartz but straightforward for SiTime due to its integrated fractional-N PLL. The Renesas distribution deal is strategic — it adds fracN PLL engineering talent, which is the remaining gap to fully obsolete quartz. Author is bullish and views quartz as a dying technology for high-speed comms.

**Qualcomm (QCOM):** Bearish conviction reinforced. The author had called the following problems ahead of time: over-reliance on smartphones and Apple; Apple's modem internalization before the March 2027 license expiry; China share loss to Huawei and MediaTek; DRAM price spike destroying smartphone demand and benefiting suppliers with long-term contracts (not QCOM). QCOM has the highest or near-highest China revenue exposure in the S&P 500, Huawei is self-sufficient on modems so QCOM has zero leverage, and a datacenter pivot is viewed as vaporware. Characterized as "out of time."

**ARM:** Author is nervous primarily because Masayoshi Son likely has large margin loans against his ARM stake — an existential financial risk that overshadows fundamentals. Notes that 15% smartphone unit volume decline may be too optimistic; 30–50% is possible. ARM recovered the day after earnings while QCOM did not, which the author finds ironic. Mentions cryptic "Izanagi and Ampere" activity but lacks sufficient detail to draw conclusions.

## Calls

- [[LITE]] — LONG — px@n/a — EML pricing power, hard OCS backlog, and structural margin tailwind from long-term customer agreements
- [[COHR]] — SHORT — px@n/a — 200G VCSEL reliability doubts, liquid-crystal OCS optical insertion loss, and credibility gap vs. LITE on actual orders
- [[SITM]] — LONG — px@n/a — FracN PLL advantage positions SiTime to capture 200G LO demand as quartz struggles with higher frequencies
- [[QCOM]] — SHORT — px@n/a — Apple modem cliff, China share loss, DRAM price shock hurting smartphones, and no credible datacenter offset
- [[ARM]] — NEUTRAL — px@n/a — Fundamentals decent but Masa's leveraged ARM position is an existential overhang that dominates the risk calculus
