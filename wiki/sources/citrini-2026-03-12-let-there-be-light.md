[[citrini]] revisits its July 2024 optical interconnect thesis — originally identifying AI connectivity as a bottleneck and optics as a major beneficiary before consensus caught on — and updates positioning now that the theme has "broadened and matured." The subtitle is "Revisiting the Optics & AI Connectivity Theme."

## Performance of Original Calls

Citrini's original optics/connectivity basket has more than tripled since initial publication, with a 35% year-to-date gain led by Lumentum (LITE). The original calls on Ciena (CIEN), Lumentum (LITE), Astera Labs (ALAB), and Credo (CRDO) are characterized as "early, uncomfortable, and very right." The piece draws an analogy to Ciena's earnings revision cycle as similar to Nvidia's 2023 trajectory — a comparison that has since played out in performance terms.

## Current Market Assessment

With the original optics names now "priced for perfection," Citrini recommends taking profits on the crowded consensus positions. The core argument is that AI infrastructure spending — estimated at $650B–$700B in 2026 hyperscaler capex — continues to underpin optical demand, but the easy money in the first-order names has been made. The "Memory Wall" (bandwidth constraints from memory latency vs. compute throughput) is cited as an ongoing structural driver that keeps optical interconnect demand durable.

## Emerging Opportunity Set

The piece pivots attention to names "further down the stack" that remain undervalued relative to the theme's maturity:
- **Photonic components**: component suppliers beneath the module/transceiver level
- **Substrates**: materials (notably indium phosphide) that underpin photonic chips
- **Capital equipment**: tooling required to manufacture photonic devices at scale
- **Co-packaged optics (CPO) enablers**: companies positioned for the next architecture transition where optics are integrated directly onto switch/processor packages

The recommendation is to avoid crowded consensus trades and rotate into these sub-themes where valuation has not yet caught up with the structural demand picture.
