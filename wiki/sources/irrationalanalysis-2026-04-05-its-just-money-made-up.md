[[irrationalanalysis]] wrote this post as a personal manifesto explaining why the newsletter remains free and why the author refuses all paid consulting, advertising, and expert-call arrangements. The essay is structured around a close reading of the 2011 film *Margin Call* and draws direct parallels to the author's own corporate career in the semiconductor industry.

The core argument is that money is an artificial social construct — "pieces of paper with pictures on them so we don't have to kill each other to get something to eat" — and that chasing it beyond sufficiency corrodes ethics and judgment. The film's character Sam Rogers serves as the cautionary archetype: a 34-year veteran who made tens of millions yet found himself trapped, unable to walk away from a morally bankrupt firm because he needed the money. The author identifies this as the defining failure mode of the Bay Area professional class, where wealthy people perpetually chase the next rung at the cost of their integrity.

From personal finance: the author entered the workforce at $85K/year, spending $40–50K/year, and has kept lifestyle inflation tightly controlled even as income grew significantly. Current annual spend is ~$80–90K (roughly half on rent for a single-bedroom in the Bay Area). A long-only portfolio provides 6–7 years of unemployment runway; a separate trading account has generated more income over 3.5 years than the cumulative earnings of the author's engineering career.

The policy conclusions are concrete: no paywall, no consulting fees, no private-company investments, no advisory arrangements. All holdings, average prices, account statements, and trade records are published publicly. The author frames this transparency as protection against the perception of financial bias — and as a structural guarantee that opinions cannot be bought.

The only stock mentioned is AAOI (Applied Optoelectronics), cited self-critically as an example of an irrational round-trip trade made despite a positive research view — used to illustrate that mistakes happen in a leveraged, high-conviction book.

The broader financial framing draws on the historical pattern of financial crises (1637 through 2008+), arguing that markets cycle endlessly and individual actors can only react, never control the system.
