[[irrationalanalysis]] argues that Astera Labs (ALAB) faces an imminent and severe revenue decline that is hiding in plain sight in publicly available technical documentation. The thesis rests on a single structural fact: more than 95% of ALAB's revenue comes from PCIe re-timer chips, and its two largest customers — Nvidia and Amazon — together account for the overwhelming majority of that revenue.

The critical data point is that the GB200 NVL (Grace-Blackwell) platform, which the author had previously called as Nvidia's dominant Blackwell configuration following GTC, contains **zero ALAB PCIe re-timers**. The author presents two exhibits: (A) the HGX Blackwell platform, which does use PCIe re-timers; and (B) the Grace-Blackwell platform, which does not — because the short, simple channel (single PCB, no backplane, no extra connectors or cables) does not require re-timing. Re-timers are only necessary for longer, lossier channels; the integrated Grace-Blackwell form factor eliminates that need entirely.

The implication is straightforward: as Nvidia's GB200 NVL systems ramp and displace the HGX architecture in AI data centers, ALAB's revenue from its largest customer is set to be cut roughly in half. Amazon has purchase-volume-based warrants that incentivize single-sourcing, but that dynamic cannot offset the platform-level content loss. Nvidia itself has no incentive to support ALAB given its desire not to prop up AI ASIC vendors like AVGO and MRVL.

The author rates ALAB an "imminent violent sinusoid" — expecting a sharp decline driven by Grace-Blackwell adoption, followed by a eventual recovery as AWS's Trainium and Inferentia programs (which presumably still use re-timers) ramp. The author considers ALAB an attractive short but states they cannot participate for ethical reasons. ARM is noted parenthetically as a beneficiary of GB200 NVL traction; the author expresses caution on ARM at $178/share after a ~50% run from the prior sub-$120 entry.
