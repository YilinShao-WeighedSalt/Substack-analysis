[[irrationalanalysis]] opens with a Dune allegory: HBM is the "Spice" of AI — the only substance that enables the next era of compute, available from a narrow oligopoly and thus extraordinarily valuable. The post provides a brief primer on DRAM fundamentals (capacitor/transistor grid, volatile refresh cycle, ~9-month wafer-to-finished-goods timeline) to explain why DRAM is structurally prone to brutal oversupply cycles and negative-gross-margin blow-ups.

**Industry structure:** The DRAM market is a three-player oligopoly — Samsung (~50% share), SK Hynix, and Micron — that survived decades of consolidation driven by burn-in testing requirements and punishing cycle dynamics. HBM has reshuffled the competitive stack dramatically.

**Micron's turnaround:** After severe production cuts in late 2022 and mid-2023 (CEO Sanjay Mehrotra publicly pleading for industry discipline in what the author calls "begging Samsung for mercy"), Micron reported a massive Q1 CY24 beat-and-raise. Key management disclosures from the March 2024 earnings transcript: HBM sold out for the next 21–24 months; HBM requires 3× the wafers of DDR due to larger die structures and stacking yield loss; Micron claims 30% lower power versus competition; supply cannibalization from HBM is expected to tighten and boost conventional DDR/LPDDR pricing.

**Samsung's HBM disaster:** Reuters confirmed that Samsung's HBM3 production yields are roughly 10–20%, versus SK Hynix's 60–70%. The author argues this has kneecapped AMD's MI300X ramp — a key customer for Samsung HBM — and that Samsung faces catastrophic loss of revenue share given its ~50% DRAM exposure. However, the author warns Samsung could eventually recover and flood the market, unilaterally destroying HBM gross margins.

**Bear case framing:** The post explicitly pushes back on a Rosenblatt analyst calling this the "biggest upcycle in history," calling such rhetoric "comically dangerous." The author's view is that the biggest upcycle historically precedes the biggest downcycle. Violently volatile commodity markets with a history of alleged price-fixing do not offer an attractive risk/reward. The sign-off is a warning: "Tread lightly, or the sand worms might eat you."
