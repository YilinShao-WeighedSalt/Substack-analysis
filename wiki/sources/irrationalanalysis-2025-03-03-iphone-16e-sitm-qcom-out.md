[[irrationalanalysis]] performed a hands-on teardown of the iPhone 16e — purchasing tools and spending a weekend examining the device under a microscope — to assess semiconductor content changes driven by Apple's first-generation C1 modem.

**SiTime (SITM) — Probable Return:** The author holds a significant SITM position and believes there is a 70% probability that SiTime's precision MEMS oscillator is back in the iPhone via the RF/Modem/Connectivity board. The logic is circumstantial but grounded in hardware observation: previous iPhones contain one Quartz timer on each of the two PCBs (application processor and connectivity). The author identified a Quartz timer on the apps PCB but found no corresponding Quartz timer on the connectivity board after hours of microscope analysis. The absence of Quartz on the connectivity board is treated as evidence that a SiTime MEMS oscillator — which would not be visually identifiable as Quartz — has replaced it.

**Qualcomm (QCOM) — Complete Exit:** Qualcomm has zero content in the iPhone 16e. All RFFE (Radio Frequency Front-End) components have been designed out, representing a content loss of $25–30 per Apple C1-enabled iPhone. Sell-side models widely assume 2025 Qualcomm share with Apple in the 75–89% range; the author argues this is wrong. Qualcomm itself has guided share could be as low as 20%. The Apple C1 modem, while currently impaired by RACH (Random Access Channel) processing issues, is expected to be improved via firmware updates in time for the September iPhone 17 launch — enabling Apple to ramp its own modem at scale and further displace Qualcomm.

**RF Front-End (QRVO / SWKS):** Qorvo has gained RF front-end share at the expense of Skyworks in the iPhone 16e.

**Apple Modem Quality:** The C1 modem is described as "surprisingly good" overall, with RACH processing as its primary weakness — a firmware-fixable issue the Apple modem team is actively addressing ahead of the iPhone 17 launch cycle.
