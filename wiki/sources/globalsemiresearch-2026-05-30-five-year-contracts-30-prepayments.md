[[globalsemiresearch]] argues that the balance of power in the memory market has definitively shifted toward suppliers — and that the evidence is visible in contract structures, inventory levels, relative fab margins, and delayed supply timelines.

**Contract structure as leverage signal.** Long-term agreement (LTA) durations have expanded from one year to three-to-five years, with customers — not suppliers — initiating the longer commitments. Buyers are prepaying 15–30% of contracted volume upfront; Microsoft is reportedly prepaying Samsung well over $10 billion. Pricing mechanisms have flipped: floors are now set off recent average selling prices with upward adjustment clauses, replacing the buyer-protective ceilings of prior cycles. The author reads these terms as evidence that hyperscalers are genuinely afraid of not securing supply. Micron has reportedly locked a five-year deal, and leading Korean makers are pushing the same structure across their books.

**Inventory is at cycle lows.** Industry checks put supplier inventory below four weeks for both DRAM and HBM, versus a healthy norm of eight to twelve weeks. This is a present-tense constraint, not a forecast.

**Supply response is late.** The oft-cited bear case — a Samsung advisor warning that global output could reach six million wafers per month by late 2027, coinciding with weakening Big Tech AI returns — is acknowledged but dismissed. The author's key point: if demand were rolling over in one to two quarters, customers would not be signing multi-year prepaid contracts. The duration of LTAs is itself a vote on supply tightness, cast by buyers with far better demand visibility than outside observers.

**Hyperscalers funding capex.** Buyers are proactively approaching leading memory suppliers to co-fund capacity expansion — a buyer de-risking the seller's capex is not a cycle-top pattern.

Overall, the author's stance on memory has "hardened," not softened, after industry checks, viewing the shortage as supply-structural rather than demand-speculative.
