[[semianalysis]] (authored by Kimbo Chen) argues that RL training system efficiency is fundamentally a **queue health problem**: the three actors — generator (LLM inference), RL environment (sandbox), and trainer (gradient updates) — must be throughput-matched to avoid one stage starving the others. The piece is a hands-on systems analysis of four real training runs, surfacing concrete bottlenecks and quantifying their costs.

**Core framework.** GRPO (Group-Relative Policy Optimization) is established as the open-source RL standard; it groups multiple completions per prompt and normalizes rewards within each group. Two paradigms are contrasted: synchronous training (trainer waits for full batches, simple but idle-heavy) vs. PipelineRL / asynchronous RL (in-flight weight updates overlap generation and training, but introduce **policy staleness** — samples generated by a mix of old and new policies). A staleness budget defines the acceptable gap between generator production and trainer consumption rates.

**Four case studies expose recurring failure modes.** Case 1 (Qwen3-235B, 64 H200 trainer + 192 generator GPUs): generation-bound with 60% rollout discard due to long chain-of-thought tail latency; trainer MFU only 10.5% and 30% wait time despite 3× generator compute. Case 2 (GLM-5, 128 H200 trainer + 128 generator with prefill-decode disaggregation): curriculum mismatch causes 55% of groups to have 100% solve rates, producing zero-advantage signals; trainer idles 74% of wall-clock time. Case 3 (Qwen3-235B on GB300): scaling concurrent rollouts from 96 to 960 exposed sandbox infrastructure limits — Modal sandbox initialization errors and 1-hour+ startup latencies at scale. Case 4 (Qwen3-235B with slime framework): partial-rollout technique saves straggler sequences to a buffer for later resumption, reducing tail latency but introducing **environment state-level staleness** — resumed rollouts continue from mid-task state created by an older policy, degrading training signal quality; high resume counts correlate with low task resolve rates.

**Market stakes.** Coding assistants represent a $30B+ ARR market on track to clear $100B by year-end, with RL-trained agents (Claude Opus 4.8 at 69.2% SWE-bench Pro) at the frontier. CEO Dario Amodei characterized RL as showing "log-linear scaling" analogous to pre-training — making it extremely expensive and system efficiency critical to competitive positioning.

**Open-source framework comparison.** Prime RL scores well on ergonomics and environment diversity but has container/dependency friction. slime offers minimal abstractions and a flexible hook system with sparse async documentation. Modal provides clear API docs but needs better concurrency observability at scale.

**Central insight.** Algorithmic choices (advantage filtering, oversampling, partial rollouts) and infrastructure choices (sandbox reliability, KV cache sizing, prefill-decode disaggregation) are deeply intertwined. Model behavior — response length, tool-use patterns, task difficulty distribution — acts as a dynamic system variable that shifts the generator/trainer balance throughout a training run.
