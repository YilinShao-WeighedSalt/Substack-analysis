Both Marvell (MRVL) and Broadcom (AVGO) reported earnings on the same day in March 2024, and both sold off after hours — but Marvell dropped far more sharply. [[irrationalanalysis]] argues the divergence is explained by a single variable: 5G exposure.

The author opens with a detailed case that 5G is an "economic hoax." Technologically, 5G (including mmWave, Massive MIMO, and beamforming) was designed to increase wireless capacity at a time when demand for that capacity never materialized. Shannon's capacity theorem is invoked to explain why bandwidth (scarce in wireless due to spectrum licensing) matters far more than SNR gains, and why the industry's push into difficult mmWave spectrum was a solution looking for a problem. The one narrow use-case that finally emerged — fixed-wireless home internet in India via CPE boxes — has already peaked and collapsed, as evidenced by Ericsson's revenue implosion.

Marvell has deep exposure to carrier/basestation infrastructure. Its Q1 guidance called for carrier revenue to decline ~50% sequentially and enterprise networking to fall ~40%, with recovery not expected until the second half of the fiscal year. The author flatly rejects CEO Matt Murphy's characterization of carrier as a "large and enduring" business that will return to over $1B annually, calling the CEO "delusional" and asserting that enterprise weakness reflects share losses to Broadcom's superior Ethernet switch portfolio.

Broadcom, by contrast, is positioned as the clear winner. Networking revenue of $3.3B grew 46% year-over-year in Q1, driven by custom AI accelerators for two hyperscale customers — identified as Google (TPU) and Meta. The Tomahawk 5 800G switch and associated optical/DSP components also saw strong demand in AI data center buildouts. Apple's RF business (WiFi, Bluetooth, cellular RF filters) was described as stable despite unit losses in China, with content gains offsetting volume declines. The VMware integration is praised: Hock Tan's software bookings are ramping sharply as Broadcom converts VMware customers to a virtual private cloud subscription model. Revenue upside at AVGO came from existing hyperscaler relationships, not new logos.

A separate thread addresses competitive speculation about MediaTek winning share in a future Google TPU chiplet design — Hock Tan dismissed it as "trash-talking" and pointed to Broadcom's deep multiyear relationships with its hyperscale customers.

The author explicitly states they own Broadcom shares and chose AVGO over MRVL precisely because of the toxic 5G/carrier exposure Marvell carries.
