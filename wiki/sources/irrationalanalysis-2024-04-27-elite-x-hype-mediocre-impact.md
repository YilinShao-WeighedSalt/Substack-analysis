[[irrationalanalysis]] walks through a COGS/BOM cost analysis of Qualcomm's Snapdragon X Elite (Hamoa) laptop chip and argues that, while the product is genuinely competitive, its financial impact on QCOM stock is already priced in — and the real story is what it means for Intel.

**Qualcomm's long-delayed PC ambitions.** Qualcomm has been attempting to break into the laptop/PC market since at least 2018 (when Broadcom's hostile takeover bid cited a ~$1B revenue opportunity). By FY2023, that opportunity had delivered effectively zero revenue, representing less than 0.1% of the ~250M unit/year laptop TAM. The new Snapdragon X Elite generation is materially better: Qualcomm finally invested in a real CPU microarchitecture (NUVIA Orion) rather than relying on stock ARM IP, and Microsoft now has a passable x86-64 emulator. The author projects QCOM will reach ~5% PC market share by CY2025.

**BOM and gross margin analysis.** Using a public die measurement from Semiaccurate and an online yield calculator, the author estimates ~286 good dies per TSMC N4 wafer. Assuming $20K/wafer (up from the ~$17K public 2020 price for N5, adjusted for inflation), and an ASP of $125, the base-case gross margin on the X Elite comes out to roughly 32%. Defective dies are partially salvaged via yield harvesting — approximately 40% of defective dies can be binned into the lower-tier Snapdragon X Plus (which disables two CPU cores). Parametric yield further segments the stack by clock speed. The monolithic die on a simple ABF substrate is notably cheaper to package than Intel's chiplet-based Meteor Lake, which roughly doubles Intel's packaging BOM cost.

**Stock impact: nothing.** Sell-side models already embed ~20% YoY IoT segment growth in the first two quarters of 2025, attributable to laptop/PC share gains. The margin profile (~32% GM) is technically accretive to QCT's ~30% average, but the upside is modest and consensus has already captured it. The author's blunt conclusion: the thesis is priced in.

**The real victim: Intel.** QCOM is entering PC at half Intel's cost basis and is willing to dump product at thin margins to claim diversification progress and gain share. Intel, already under severe competitive and financial pressure, is losing share in its core market to a desperate, cost-advantaged rival — described as "the last thing Intel needs."
