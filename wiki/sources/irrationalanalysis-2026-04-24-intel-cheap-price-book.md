[[irrationalanalysis]] opens with a portfolio risk-management move ahead of a potentially volatile weekend due to Middle East geopolitical risk: trimming exposure from ~$3.5M to ~$2.5M net long and buying ~$100K in SMH puts as a hedge, noting that his high-beta semiconductor holdings would see ~20% drawdown if SMH drops 10%.

The core "do not touch" positions are INTC, LITE, and TSEM. SMTC was removed from this list after strong gains and concern over an Edgewater note suggesting Mediatek TPU is dead and near-term volatility risk. BE (Bloom Energy) replaces SMTC on the protected list.

The central thesis of the post is that Intel is exceptionally cheap on a price-to-book basis. The author compares foundry-adjacent peers: TSMC at 10.6x P/B, Tower Semiconductor at 7.8x P/B, GlobalFoundries (described as "a flaming pile of dogshit") at 2.9x P/B — and Intel at only 3.6x P/B (book value of $22.9/share, trading at $81.4 after hours). The author argues Intel deserves at minimum 2x GloFo's P/B multiple (5.8x), implying a minimum price target of $133/share — and that this assumes Intel's products group is worth zero, which the author no longer believes.

The bullish case for Intel products has been upgraded: high-performance CPU is increasingly necessary for agentic AI workloads, and ARM's first-party microarchitectures (including Neoverse V3) are characterized as inferior to Intel, AMD, Apple, and Nuvacore. Qualcomm is dismissed due to key Nuvia talent departures to form Nuvacore.

On the Intel earnings call: 18A yields are finally improving with supporting data; gross margins came in above 40%, surprising the author who was previously bearish on near-term margins. External foundry demand is materializing — Intel has cleanroom capacity, advanced packaging (EMIB vs. TSMC's CoWoS-L are functionally equivalent for designers), and a supply-flex advantage over AMD and ARM vendors who are TSMC-constrained. Advanced packaging wins could have come 18 months earlier but for internal dysfunction now fixed under Lip-Bu Tan. TSMC does not want to expand CoWoS fast enough given margin priorities; Intel is happy to fill that gap.
