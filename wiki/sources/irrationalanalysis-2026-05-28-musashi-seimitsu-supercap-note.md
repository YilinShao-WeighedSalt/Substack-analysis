[[irrationalanalysis]] writes a quick (~1 hour) note on Musashi Seimitsu (7220.T), a Japanese manufacturer the author considers the dominant player in the supercapacitor market — a niche they separately researched across 10+ companies and concluded Musashi "vaporizes everyone else."

**The supercapacitor context.** Supercapacitors are large capacitors used primarily for rapid-response energy storage and power buffering, not traditional filtering/decoupling applications. The author distinguishes this from the conventional capacitor market, calling it an "exponentially growing niche." Key use cases include UPS-style backup power, smoothing load and power spikes, and acting as a dense energy buffer in systems that also include batteries.

**Musashi's competitive moat.** The central thesis rests on volumetric energy density — the critical spec for these components. Musashi's supercapacitors are described as far ahead of any competitor in this metric. They also carry a notably higher rated voltage per cell (3.8 V), which reduces the number of cells that must be stacked in series, simplifying control circuitry and system design. The author frames this as a technical/engineering moat that is difficult to replicate, calling it an "uber monopoly of a rapidly growing, mission-critical niche." The main drawback acknowledged is high equivalent series resistance (ESR), but the author argues ESR is secondary to density for this application because these circuits typically involve voltage step-down/step-up stages anyway, making efficiency via ESR a lesser concern.

**Circuit design context.** Using a TI application note and an ADI application note as references, the author explains how supercaps are integrated with batteries in backup/buffering circuits: a control circuit steps down input voltage to charge a bank of caps and batteries in parallel, and the caps can be stacked in series to raise the working voltage at the cost of capacitance. This reinforces why density dominates over ESR as the primary selection criterion.

**Position disclosure.** Author holds shares from roughly a year prior in a long-only account, was down 25% at the trough, and is now up ~57% even after adding to the position that week. Describes wanting to double the position but lacking free cash.
