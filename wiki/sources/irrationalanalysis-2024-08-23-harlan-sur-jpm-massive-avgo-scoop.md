[[irrationalanalysis]] reacts to a JPMorgan research note from analyst Harlan Sur on Broadcom's 3D SOIC modular reference platform, calling it "omega bullish" from an engineering standpoint and arguing that JPM itself underappreciated the significance of the find.

The central thesis is that Broadcom's adoption of TSMC InFO_SoW (integrated Fan-Out System-on-Wafer) represents a decisive engineering moat. Unlike CoWoS-based packaging, InFO_SoW uses no substrate, delivering two key physical advantages: (1) drastically reduced packaging-related impedance at 100 MHz, which improves power delivery quality and silicon clock stability, and (2) lower insertion loss across the signal spectrum (estimated 1–4 dB improvement), benefiting Ethernet, PCIe, and HBM/DDR memory PHYs. The author dismisses TSMC's headline "10–15% power savings" marketing claim as physically inconsistent but affirms that the real gains are meaningful nonetheless.

Broadcom has further leveraged InFO_SoW by porting and validating its existing best-in-class IP (Ethernet, HBM, DDR, PCIe, NoC) onto the platform, creating a modular chiplet reference design. Critically, Broadcom has developed a proprietary chip-to-chip interconnect called "MAX" that deliberately locks out UCIe-compatible third-party chiplets, forcing all chiplets in the ecosystem to be Broadcom-sourced and defending content share per customer.

Investment implications drawn from this analysis: (1) Google's efforts to shift TPU design to lower-cost vendors like MediaTek (2454.TW) or Marvell (MRVL) have become exponentially harder; (2) the recent addition of 1–2 new custom-silicon customers at Broadcom is just the start, with organic growth highly likely; (3) Broadcom is now better positioned to poach design wins from Marvell and Alchip (including AWS Trainium/Inferentia programs). The time-to-market advantage from the validated modular platform compounds R&D OpEx efficiency and raises the barrier for any rival to replicate.
