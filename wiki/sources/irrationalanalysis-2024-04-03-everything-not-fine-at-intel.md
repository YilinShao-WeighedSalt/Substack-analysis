[[irrationalanalysis]] reacts to Intel's new segment reporting webinar (April 3, 2024), framing the event in one word: "concerning." The post dissects management's slides and transcript with a skeptical eye, concluding that Intel's next 3–4 years will be "hell."

**Margins are not "healthy."** CFO David Zinsner described product gross and operating margins as healthy, but the author argues Intel is kitchen-sinking by artificially setting internal wafer transfer costs low to flatter the Intel Products segment. True margin deterioration is coming as AMD and ARM gain share in datacenter, and as AMD leads a "feeding frenzy" in client CPUs joined by Qualcomm and Nvidia.

**Lunar Lake is almost entirely on TSMC.** Intel admitted that "Intel Foundry lost Intel products to external suppliers due to process deficits." The client CPU, GPU, I/O, and SoC tiles of Lunar Lake are all on non-Intel nodes — a striking concession for an IDM.

**Outsourcing peak is still ahead.** Management acknowledged 30% of wafers are currently sourced externally and guided for that to decline toward 20% over the plan period. The author expects the real peak — above 30% — will occur in 2026/2027, not now, meaning the external-foundry drag on Intel Foundry profitability will worsen before it improves, likely not recovering until 2027/2028.

**Intel Foundry's economics are Intel Products' hostage.** The 8-K makes clear that foundry profitability is tied almost entirely to Intel Products revenue — which is declining as AMD wins datacenter and ARM gains foothold. The engineering turnaround led by Dr. Ann Kelleher is real, but competitively "too late" for the near term.

**The patient vs. the drug company.** Using a medical metaphor, the author frames Intel as a chronically ill patient — decades of antibiotic misuse (partial fixes that left stronger competitive bacteria alive) have produced a multidrug-resistant problem. Recovery requires backside power delivery, gate-all-around transistors, High-NA EUV, and advanced packaging — a 4–6 year timeline. Applied Materials is singled out as the "sole-source" supplier of critical process medications. The investment question posed: would you rather own the patient or the drug company?
