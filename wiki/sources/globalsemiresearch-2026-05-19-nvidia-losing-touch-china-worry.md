[[globalsemiresearch]] argues that Nvidia's persistent campaign to maintain a China presence — three Beijing trips by Jensen Huang in 2025, lobbying for H200 export approval, and direct involvement in U.S.-China summits — is fundamentally not about revenue but about market intelligence. China's share of Nvidia revenue collapsed from ~26% in FY2022 to ~5% by Q3 FY2025 (ending late 2025), so the commercial logic of fighting so hard for access is otherwise incoherent. The real thesis: physical presence in China is what keeps Nvidia wired into where AI infrastructure is actually heading.

The strategic risk the post surfaces is that Nvidia's China team is failing at that exact mission. Through industry checks, the author finds that Nvidia's China staff are widely described as detached "scientists" — uninterested in clients, giving irrelevant academic talks at partner events, and coasting on a decade of product that sold itself. That era is over: Huawei's Ascend ecosystem is maturing, DeepSeek's V4 optimized for Ascend (with Huawei engineers involved pre-launch, while Nvidia and AMD were excluded), and ByteDance, Tencent, and Alibaba are placing serious Ascend 950 orders.

Two product failures illustrate the disconnect. First, the RTX 6000D — a China-specific Blackwell workstation GPU launched Q3 2025 and internally codenamed "L20 Next" — was aggressively promoted but sold poorly: partners bought thousands of units and moved almost none, far below Nvidia's internal target of ~1 million units by year-end. The chip was cut down significantly from the global RTX Pro 6000 (no NVLink, no HBM, GDDR7 only), and partners felt misled about its positioning. Second, the DGX Spark ($3,999, GB10 Superchip, 128 GB unified memory) was marketed in ways that let Chinese buyers believe they could cluster many units into inference servers; the reality was a two-unit hard cap, causing inventory pileups at partners who had bought ~1,000 units each. Both cases reflect Nvidia pricing and pitching China as if the market would absorb whatever was shipped — an assumption that was true for years but no longer is.

The post concludes that Nvidia's deeper problem in China is not Washington export policy but operational: if the purpose of being in China is intelligence-gathering, then a team that misreads the market is failing at the one job that matters.
