SemiAnalysis

Stop Saying Half of 2026 US Datacenter Capacity Is Canceled
June 18, 2026 • Reyk Knuhtsen, Maya Barkin, Jeremie Eliahou Ontiveros + 2 others • ~5,200 words

Summary

SemiAnalysis directly refutes the widespread claim—originating from Bloomberg's April 1, 2026 article and amplified by TechRadar, Tom's Hardware, and others—that half of 2026 US datacenter capacity has been delayed or canceled. The team argues this narrative is built on fatally flawed data and methodology.

The denominator problem: The "50% canceled" figure traces to Sightline Climate, which estimates ~12 GW expected and only ~5 GW under construction. SemiAnalysis says this is off by multiples: the top two hyperscalers alone exceed 5 GW of self-build under construction, before counting third-party developers. Sightline's dataset tracks only large, publicly announced projects—the very slice most prone to slipping—creating survivorship bias in the numerator.

SemiAnalysis's own model: Over the last 6 months, their YE2026 NA Hyperscaler self-build forecast moved by only ~1%, and NA colocation by <5%. They attribute the discrepancy to "vibecoded" (Claude-generated) datacenter models that take press releases at face value without critical judgment about construction timelines, equipment procurement, or grid interconnection realities.

Three categories of "delay":

  • Aggressive announcements by new entrants: Data City/Energy Abundance (5 GW campus, 300 MW by 2026—no physical progress on satellite), APR Energy (400 MW Texas, no customer, no news), Cloudburst (1.2 GW announced, 50 MW phase progressing but shell construction not started). These projects had unrealistic timelines from inception.
  • Advanced projects with optimistic timelines: Detailed case studies of Nebius NJ (DataOne built shell fast but MEP/commissioning far exceeded timeline—25 MW by Oct 2025, 50 MW by ~Feb 2026, versus original targets of July and September 2025) and Core Scientific Denton (weather, transformer explosion, GB200 redesign delays). SemiAnalysis predicted CoreWeave's Q3 earnings delays and successfully forecast 1.7 GW active power by YE2026.
  • Permitting and NIMBY: Oracle/STACK New Mexico detailed as the most consequential real delay. Two behind-the-meter gas microgrids squeezed under the 250 tpy NOx major-source threshold; NMED ordered public hearings; 7,155 public comments filed; Oracle withdrew turbine applications and switched to Bloom Energy fuel cells; but the binding constraint is a 400,000 Dth/d gas pipeline. Transwestern's Green Chile Pipeline hit NM State Land Office right-of-way denial, FERC Section 7 review, and no SHPO clearance. SemiAnalysis delays this site to 2029.

Moratoriums are noise: 12+ states have filed moratorium bills, Indiana has four county-level enactments, and Maine's governor vetoed a statewide datacenter ban—but Maine has <5 MW planned. These measures land in areas with minimal existing or planned datacenter activity.

ERCOT reality check: 410 GW of large-load interconnection requests (87% from datacenters) versus Texas's 85 GW all-time peak. SemiAnalysis identifies 311 GW as "phantom datacenter demand." Texas SB 6 (site control documentation, $100K study fees, mandatory curtailment) is filtering out speculative filings as designed.

Equipment suppliers are fine: Canceled projects never placed equipment orders. Prepayments of 10–15% are standard for queue position. Reinhausen tap-changer bushings are quoted 3–5 years; most critical SKUs 1+ year; GE Vernova, Hitachi Energy, Mitsubishi Electric booked 3–4 years. Queue depth means reallocated slots, not lost orders. Chinese OEMs (xAI likely uses Sieyuan transformers) are quietly alleviating bottlenecks. Modular/prefabricated builds compress supply-chain complexity and labor requirements.

Leading operators' playbooks: Hyperscalers use political playbooks (land-use attorneys, utility relationships, community investment like Microsoft's $31M Quincy water reuse) and physical playbooks (behind-the-meter generation, powered land deals, long-lead procurement during scoping, prefab builds). When opposition kills a project, operators withdraw quietly and relocate.

"The cancellations and delays cluster in a layer of the pipeline we already treat as structurally oversupplied. The veritable 2026 projects continue to progress on schedule."

Critical Analysis

Logic assessment: The argument is rigorous and well-evidenced. The distinction between announcement-stage projects (structurally oversupplied, never going to deliver in 2026) and execution-stage projects (site control, equipment orders, vertical construction) is the critical insight. The specific case studies with satellite imagery timestamps, FERC filings, and NMED documents give the claim far more credibility than the Bloomberg/Sightline methodology.

Self-serving bias: This article is simultaneously a market analysis and a sales pitch for SemiAnalysis's Datacenter Industry Model, Energy Model, and Industrials Model. Every section ends with "check our model for details" or "contact us for more." The piece benefits commercially from debunking the Bloomberg narrative because it positions SemiAnalysis as the only reliable source. That said, the underlying argument doesn't require accepting their model as authoritative—the critique of Sightline's denominator is independently verifiable.

What would need to be true for this to be WRONG: (1) If real, capitalized, equipment-ordered projects begin failing at rates similar to speculative ones—this would mean the supply chain constraints are binding even for well-prepared operators. (2) If the Chinese OEM workaround (Sieyuan transformers) gets shut down by tariffs or executive order. (3) If behind-the-meter generation faces broader regulatory pushback beyond the Oracle/STACK NM case. (4) If hyperscaler capex actually pulls back in H2 2026 (June 17 Fed hawkishness is a risk).

Cherry-picking risk: The article focuses on speculative projects failing to prove delays are overblown, but the Oracle/STACK NM case—a well-capitalized, real project delayed to 2029—is buried as a third category. How many more Oracle-type delays are in the pipeline that haven't surfaced yet?

Equipment supplier thesis: The VRT/Vertiv bull case (backlogs secure because canceled projects never placed orders) is logically sound but ignores margin risk from slowing order growth rates, even if existing backlogs are safe. A stock at 75x P/E needs growth acceleration, not just backlog protection.

Memory check (wiki): SemiAnalysis has covered datacenter infrastructure extensively. Their April 2025 note correctly identified that Wall Street was misreading lease cancellations as VRT orderbook misses. Their October 2024 datacenter electrical systems piece, followed by the May 2025 datacenter freeze analysis, showed evolving and self-correcting analysis. They correctly predicted CoreWeave delays ahead of CRWV Q3 earnings. Track record on datacenter-specific calls: STRONG.

Confidence: HIGH on the core thesis that headline "50% delayed" is misleading. MEDIUM on the equipment supplier bull case (VRT valuation already bakes in structural demand).

Daily Update — June 18, 2026
June 18, 2026 • Austin Lyons & Vik Sekar • ~1,250 words

Summary

1. ASML CEO on Terafab: Christophe Fouquet told Bloomberg that Elon Musk's proposed "Terafab" semiconductor project would be an extreme test for industry supply lines. ASML is the sole EUV scanner supplier, and a fab targeting wafer volumes an order of magnitude above today's largest facilities would strain tool output, materials, and skilled labor. Vik notes: "If Terafab comes online, we ain't seen nothin' yet regarding supply chain shortages. Big boost for semicap names."

2. NOR Flash / SLC NAND prices surge 100%+ in 1H26: TrendForce reports contract prices increased over 100% in the first half of 2026 as major memory suppliers prioritize HBM and advanced-layer 3D NAND, constraining mature-node capacity. TrendForce projects continued increases in 2H26: high-density NOR Flash potentially +60–65%, SLC NAND +70–75%. Vik notes Kioxia phased out SLC NAND in favor of TLC/QLC, but SLC still has demand for KV cache applications due to lower latency.

3. SK Hynix ships 12-layer HBM4E samples: Samples reach maximum speed of 16 Gbps per pin using Advanced MR-MUF packaging with 17% reduced heat resistance. Built on TSMC 3nm base die. Vik highlights 3nm for base die "really kicks up pin speeds a notch." Austin questions how much speed gain is node-dependent vs. architecture—if 3nm is the major contributor, competitors will follow.

4. Samsung gains chip orders as TSMC capacity strains: BYD, Google, and AMD increasingly seeking Samsung for contract chipmaking as AI infrastructure demand strains TSMC's advanced node capacity. Vik notes TSMC's $56B capex may not be enough given years-long capacity build timelines; "TSMC's conservatism might be their own Achilles heel."

5. Aehr Test secures follow-on order: FOX-XP wafer-level burn-in system for silicon photonics-based transceivers, configured to test 9 wafers in parallel, delivery within 6 months. Customer described as "global leader in networking products." Vik identifies customer as likely Broadcom. This is the second order after the April $41M initial order.

6. Broadcom AI revenue could triple by FY28: Wolfe Research states AVGO's AI XPU volume could reach 15 GW by FY28, driving $250–300B in FY28 XPU revenue if Apollo/Blackstone XPV financing vehicle for Anthropic's FY26 Broadcom purchases is fully funded. JPMorgan confirmed no TPU delays or cancellations. Vik adds TPU v9 is not delayed but Google's CoT may be 18 months behind.

7. Quick Takes: CoreWeave first to bring up Vera Rubin NVL72. China highlights 5 AI models trained on domestic chips. Infineon Malaysia draws 1,000+ applicants for production jobs.

Critical Analysis

NOR/SLC pricing surge: This is a genuine supply-side constraint worth monitoring. When memory makers reallocate capacity to HBM and advanced NAND, the tail wags—legacy products in automotive, industrial, and KV cache applications face structurally tight supply. The 100%+ increase is not demand-driven euphoria; it's rational pricing in a capacity-constrained market. However, 2H26 projections of +60–75% additional increases seem aggressive—at some point, OEMs redesign around these components or secondary suppliers enter.

HBM4E 16 Gbps/pin: Impressive but expected. SK Hynix has been the consistent leader in HBM generations and this tracks with the wiki's 18-call history of bullish calls on 000660.KS. The 3nm base die question Austin raises is sharp—if the speed gains are primarily node-dependent, Samsung's foundry overflow orders could enable faster competitive catch-up than expected.

AVGO $250–300B FY28 revenue: This would represent a near-tripling from current levels. The Wolfe Research framing explicitly conditions this on Anthropic financing coming together, making it a scenario rather than a base case. The market is already pricing AVGO at $1.94T (~65x TTM P/E), so meaningful upside requires this scenario materializing AND no customer concentration risk from Google/Anthropic/Apple dependency. The "TPU v9 not delayed" JPM note removes a near-term risk.

AEHR Broadcom speculation: Vik's identification of Broadcom as the "global leader in networking products" customer is plausible given Broadcom's silicon photonics transceiver push for CPO. Two orders in ~3 months signals production commitment, not just evaluation. At $103 and unprofitable, AEHR remains speculative but the wafer-level burn-in thesis for AI optics is gaining real traction—a meaningful upgrade from the wiki's March 2026 "exploratory/no conclusion" status.

Samsung absorbing TSMC overflow: This is a structural story that validates the thesis multiple publications have been building: TSMC capacity constraints are real and persistent. Samsung's participation as an overflow valve introduces yield quality questions—historically Samsung foundry has lagged TSMC on advanced node yields. If BYD/Google/AMD are going to Samsung, it's out of necessity, not preference.

Confidence: MEDIUM — Semi Doped daily updates aggregate and contextualize news rather than building primary theses; the analysis quality is in the practitioner commentary (Vik and Austin), not in original research.

Daily Update — June 16, 2026
June 16, 2026 • Austin Lyons & Vik Sekar • ~1,450 words

Summary

1. PicoJool introduces 200G VCSELs for scale-up AI data centers: Palo Alto startup announces 200G VCSEL products with >37 GHz bandwidth, chip samples next quarter, high-volume production early 2027. Roadmap to 800G, 1.6T, 3.2T. Built on gallium arsenide (GaAs) supply chain via WIN Semiconductor partnership (shipped 1B+ VCSEL chips for 3D sensing). Pat Gelsinger (Playground Global) endorsed the combination of bandwidth and production scale. Vik emphasizes: "VCSELs are based on GaAs, not InP. They are not subject to the same supply chain constraints. If this has reach, data rate, and capacity, the tech is IN." Austin notes the DSP can be eliminated with slow-and-wide approach (32x 50G NRZ instead of 8x 200G PAM4).

2. AMD acquires MEXT: MEXT developed AI-powered predictive memory technology that makes flash behave like DRAM, expanding usable memory capacity while maintaining performance. Austin explains the core loop: classify hot/cold DRAM pages, offload cold to flash (20x cheaper), predict which flash pages will be needed next, prefetch before the application asks. Software-only, no OS or app changes required. Vik: "Flash is becoming increasingly important as DRAM's price and lack of capacity fundamentally limits long-running agentic AI workloads."

3. TSMC partners with Ibiden, Innolux on glass substrates: Chip-on-panel-on-substrate (CoPoS) packaging using glass substrates. Rectangular panels rather than round wafers. Glass offers improved dimensional stability for larger packages. Vik: "This is a 2028/29 story at earliest before production, but suppliers to this tech will start showing profits earlier."

4. Qualcomm in talks to acquire Tenstorrent: RISC-V-based AI processor startup led by Jim Keller. Vik questions: "Why buy Tenstorrent when you're building your own accelerator?" Austin counters: "Wouldn't you want to acquihire Jim Keller?"

5. TechInsights confirms SMIC N+3 in Kirin 9030: SMIC's third-generation advancement of 7nm-class node, narrowing gap with TSMC's 5nm despite no EUV access. Powers Huawei's latest smartphone lineup. Vik wonders what tau folding can do for transistor density going forward.

6. Applied Materials launches 3D chip tools: Centris Spectral SiN ALD (microwave plasma for 3D logic/memory) and Producer Selectra Mo Etch (selective molybdenum removal). Targeting vertically stacked architectures.

7. TSMC CoWoS shortfall halving: Supply-demand gap projected to narrow from ~20% to ~10% by end-2026 per TrendForce. TSMC ramping output for Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom. CoWoS has been a persistent bottleneck since 2023. Vik: "Good to have CoWoS expand capacity when people are looking to Intel EMIB due to shortages."

8. Stock movers June 15: WDC +16.1%, MRAM +14.1%, AAOI +13.3%, NBIS +11.9%, POET +11.2%.

9. Quick Hits: IQE + Tower Semiconductor multi-year InP epiwafer agreement for silicon photonics. Samsung Electro-Mechanics in talks with big tech for silicon capacitors for AI servers. AWS launches Graviton5-powered EC2 instances. Ericsson defends purpose-built 5G chip strategy after Nvidia's radio unit chip plans.

Critical Analysis

VCSELs for AI scale-up: This is a genuinely important development to watch. The GaAs supply chain advantage is real—InP capacity constraints are well-documented across the wiki's silicon-photonics theme, with multiple publications flagging InP wafer supply as a bottleneck. If VCSELs can achieve the data rates and reach needed for scale-up interconnects while avoiding InP supply constraints, this reprices the optical interconnect value chain. However: 200G per lane is behind the 200G per lane InP lasers already shipping, VCSELs have historically been limited to short-reach (<100m), and the company is pre-revenue. Watch for hyperscaler qualification data, not just specs.

AMD MEXT acquisition: This is smart strategically but won't move the needle financially for a $834B company. The flash-as-DRAM concept directly addresses the memory wall for agentic AI workloads (long-running sessions that accumulate context far beyond DRAM capacity). This aligns with the Semi Doped May 4 piece on "CapEx is just Memory Tax Now." However, it's a software acquisition—the value depends on whether MEXT's page prediction actually works at production scale with real workloads, not just benchmarks. AMD's GPU bear thesis in the wiki (zero production inference customers per SemiAnalysis) is more material than this acquisition.

Qualcomm / Tenstorrent: The wiki's 51-call history on QCOM shows a sustained HIGH-conviction SHORT (current stance). QCOM acquiring Tenstorrent would be a desperation move confirming the bear thesis: QCOM can't build its own AI accelerator fast enough and is paying a premium for Jim Keller's talent and RISC-V IP. This doesn't fix the $1.2B Apple modem cliff. Vik's skepticism is warranted; Austin's "acquihire" argument has merit but the acquisition price likely makes this value-destructive for shareholders.

SMIC N+3: The confirmed narrowing of the gap with TSMC N5 is geopolitically significant but not directly tradeable for our universe. Combined with the Huawei tau scaling discussion from the wiki, SMIC's multi-patterning approach is squeezing more from DUV than most expected—but at a significant cost-per-transistor penalty that limits commercial competitiveness outside Huawei.

Confidence: MEDIUM — daily update with solid practitioner commentary; the VCSEL and MEXT items warrant deeper investigation.
