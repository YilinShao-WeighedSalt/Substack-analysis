Irrational Analysis

Coherent Lite Transceivers + AMD CPU Opportunity
Published June 13, 2026  |  ~1,800 words (public content)
MRVL TSEM COHR AMD NOK SMTC MACOM

Full Summary

This post is a two-part analysis. The first and larger section responds to FundaAI's article on coherent lite transceivers, correcting key technical errors while broadly affirming FundaAI's investment conclusions. The second section is a brief but punchy case for AMD's datacenter CPU opportunity tied to incremental TSMC N3P wafer allocation.

Part 1 — Coherent Lite Transceivers: The author begins with a crash course on coherent optics fundamentals. Coherent optics encodes data on two axes (I and Q), similar to wireless communication. The benefit is dramatically more bits per symbol (optics uses 16QAM). The critical drawback is phase noise management — laser linewidth must be <0.2 MHz, and if the symmetry between arms and photodetectors is not precisely matched, "everything becomes garbage and is unfixable." RIN (relative intensity noise) kills you; linewidth kills you harder.

The author then breaks down the Google OFC 2026 tutorial on coherent lite architecture. The key insight is polarization-folding — a clever trick that eliminates polarization handling from the transmitter side entirely. The transmitter output has random polarization, so the receiver uses a structure that folds all light onto a single polarization mode (likely TE), and the DSP backs out a simple 1+Z⁻¹ transfer function. This dramatically reduces complexity and cost on both Tx and Rx sides.

Crucially, the author identifies that FundaAI's InP intensity table is "completely wrong." The errors stem from treating photonic chip area as rectangular when InP devices are actually narrow and long — you need a wafer map calculator, not simple area calculations. The author spent time running sanity checks and concluded that all numbers in FundaAI's table are incorrect, though the directional investment conclusions remain valid.

"A MZI modulator can easily hit 5 mm long. Using InP for coherent lite Tx PIC would obliterate the industry. Also would obliterate cost."

Key technical correction: The transmit side of coherent lite does NOT need InP. Nothing in the Tx block is polarization-sensitive. SiPho (silicon photonics) will work fine for the MZI modulators, which run at 168 Gbps per modulator — actually easier than the 224 Gbps line rate SiPho already handles. The receive side does use monolithic InP PIC, but making both Tx and Rx monolithic InP would be "crazy town" given that InP supply is already "an unmitigated disaster."

The author describes InP yield as a "satanic nightmare" — 50% yield is considered normal for discrete devices. Devices fail at wrong wavelengths (too far to thermally tune), fail to hit target power at temperature, or exhibit mode hopping. InP is also extremely brittle, so many die are lost during packaging and wirebonding.

The author's theoretical optimal coherent lite design:

  • Tx: DFB laser (no ITLA), Tower Semiconductor SiPho with MZI modulators (168 Gbps each), Marvell or Semtech drivers
  • Rx: Monolithic InP PIC (Coherent Corp or Nokia), TIA from Marvell or Semtech
  • DSP: Marvell — 8× 336G PAM6 SerDes receive from host, 16× 168G PAM4 SerDes to PIC

Winners and Losers:

  • Marvell (MRVL): "Biggest winner by far." Will make "so much money" on DSP and has driver/TIA bundling opportunities.
  • Tower Semi (TSEM): Wins because of more SiPho demand, and "GloFo is actually incompetent."
  • Semtech (SMTC): Wins, but "maybe not as much as I hope" — Marvell might take most transceiver sockets.
  • MACOM: Loses because photodiodes integrate into the InP PIC, eliminating MACOM's discrete photodiode revenue.
  • Coherent Corp (COHR) & Nokia (NOK): Win with 6-inch InP process for monolithic InP PIC. Other InP players "won't want to burn their 4in capacity on this."

Part 2 — AMD CPU Opportunity: The author argues that if AMD secures incremental N3P wafers from TSMC (hinted at during a Raymond James bus tour), all of that capacity would go to datacenter CPUs — AMD's "hottest and by far highest gross margin product line." AMD Turin uses N3P for CPU core chiplets and N6 for the I/O die, which the author calls "by far the best implementation of chiplets." The numbers are described as "dirty" in a positive sense — implying very high margins. The author claims contacts don't fully appreciate "the money tsunami Dr. Lisa Su is capable of unleashing IF AND ONLY IF SHE GOT MORE N3P WAFERS."

Critical Analysis

Logic Assessment: The coherent lite technical analysis is exceptionally strong. The author demonstrates first-principles engineering knowledge — the correction of FundaAI's InP intensity table, the phase noise crash course, and the polarization-folding explanation all reflect deep domain expertise. The identification that Tx does not need InP is a crucial insight that FundaAI missed. The author's claim to have "numbers pulled from my anus" while simultaneously winking about industry knowledge is consistent with someone who works in the semiconductor industry and cannot directly cite proprietary data.

Unstated Assumptions:

  • Coherent lite adoption timeline is assumed to be imminent, but Google is the primary driver and hyperscaler adoption cycles are notoriously slow. The OFC 2026 tutorial is a research presentation, not a product announcement.
  • The "SiPho for Tx, InP for Rx" architecture is the author's "best guess" — the design space is acknowledged as "pretty open," which means actual implementations could differ significantly.
  • The AMD N3P wafer argument is entirely conditional on TSMC allocation decisions that are not public. "Assume this but don't think about it" is literally the author's framing.

What Would Make This Wrong:

  • If coherent lite adoption is delayed 2+ years, the near-term thesis evaporates. Coherent optics adoption has historically moved slower than predictions.
  • If Broadcom develops competitive DSP for coherent lite (they already dominate traditional coherent DSP), Marvell's "biggest winner" status could be contested.
  • MACOM's photodiode business could be more defensible than assumed if discrete solutions maintain cost or performance advantages at certain reach profiles.
  • AMD's N3P wafer allocation may not materialize — TSMC capacity is under extreme pressure from Apple, Nvidia, and others.

Bias/Conflict: The author explicitly states they are "heavily invested in the semiconductor industry" and regularly shares trading snapshots. The coherent lite piece is framed around correcting another newsletter's errors (FundaAI), which creates a "I'm smarter" dynamic that could bias toward more confident conclusions. The AMD section relies on non-public "contacts" and a bus tour hint — this is classic industry-insider alpha that is hard to verify externally.

Confidence: HIGH on the coherent lite technical analysis and MRVL/TSEM positioning. MEDIUM on the AMD N3P wafer thesis (conditional and unverifiable). LOW on MACOM as a loser (contradicted by consensus).

SemiAnalysis

Is SMIC N+3's Metal Pitch Smaller than Intel 18A's?
Published June 14, 2026  |  ~8,100 words  |  Paid (partial paywall)  |  STEEL Team, Afzal Ahmad, Andrew Wagner +5 others
INTC TSM

Full Summary

This is the inaugural public report from SemiAnalysis's new Teardown Engineering & Evaluation Lab (STEEL) — a state-of-the-art facility in Oregon for reverse-engineering advanced chips. SemiAnalysis claims to now exceed TechInsights in revenue and frames this as competition for the decades-long TechInsights monopoly in chip teardowns. The report is a comprehensive teardown of the HiSilicon Kirin 9030 Pro SoC fabricated on SMIC N+3, compared with a MediaTek Helio G99 on TSMC N6.

The headline finding: SMIC N+3 uses a minimum metal pitch (M0) of 32.5 nm, which is ~10% tighter than the 36 nm M0 pitch shipping in Intel's Panther Lake on 18A. However, SemiAnalysis immediately contextualizes this: "The headline is true, but an incomplete cherry picked metric." M0 is a local intra-cell routing layer — its usefulness depends on the full interconnect stack. Intel 18A's looser M0 pitch is partly due to PowerVia (backside power delivery), which reduces front-side congestion.

Process Technology Details:

  • Transistor density: SMIC N+3 achieves 113.4 MTr/mm² vs TSMC N6 at 107.7 MTr/mm² — slightly above N6 class, achieved without EUV through aggressive DUV multi-patterning (SAQP for M0) and DTCO
  • Fin profile: N+3 fins are taller, narrower (aspect ratio ~9.5:1 vs N6's 7.8:1), with less top rounding — technically impressive single-digit-nm engineering
  • Cell height: 228 nm across all CPU cores (5% smaller than N6, 9.5% smaller than N+2)
  • CGP: 57 nm (matching TSMC N6, 9.5% shrink from N+2)
  • SRAM: 8T bitcell at 0.0463 µm², with estimated 6T HDC at 0.0260 µm² — near Samsung 7LPP/5LPP levels

Architecture & Performance (Kirin 9030 vs 9020):

  • Prime core: +10% frequency (2.5→2.75 GHz), 2× L2 cache, core size -7.6% (excl. L2: -21%)
  • Middle cores: increased from 3 to 4, each shrinks ~22%, +17% integer IPC
  • GPU: Maleoon 935 with 6 CUs (from 4), adds ray tracing, +70–79% in 3DMark benchmarks — reaches Snapdragon 8+ Gen 1 territory but trails current flagships by 2.4–3.2×
  • NPU: added second Tiny core, significant layout changes

The Performance Gap: The most striking comparison is against Apple. Apple's efficiency cores deliver 20% higher integer performance at 1W vs Huawei's prime core at 4.5W. Apple M5 P-core is 60% higher per-clock and 2.7× faster in absolute performance. The current process gap (N+3 matches N6, a several-generation-old node) means Huawei cannot match the voltage-frequency curves or transistor budgets available to Apple, Qualcomm, and MediaTek on TSMC N4/N3P.

Memory & Packaging: Samsung LPDDR5X-9600 (1a node, 12GB in Pro), with CXMT DRAM found in 16GB Pro Max variant. All-organic iPoP packaging — no silicon interposer needed.

Future Roadmap:

  • N+4 (theoretical): Could reach ~138 MTr/mm² (TSMC N5/Samsung SF4 class) via cell height reduction to 198nm, CGP to 54nm — but cumulative difficulty is high
  • N+5 (theoretical): Would require backside contacts, reaching ~164 MTr/mm² (Intel 18A HP class) — but "much more expensive route"
  • Huawei's τ Scaling & LogicFolding: Huawei's answer to lack of EUV — 3D stacking of active logic, not just cache. Prime core targets ~5 GHz by 2031, with "14A-equivalent density" through stacking. But this inflates density by counting multiple active layers per package footprint — not directly comparable to foundry Bohr density

Export Control Assessment: Export controls forced a different path but didn't freeze progress. SMIC is licensing N+2/N+3 to HLMC/Hua Hong, spreading manufacturing knowledge. Cambricon (expected ByteDance supplier) and Alibaba T-Head could benefit. "Sanctions aimed at SMIC alone become less effective once the manufacturing knowledge has spread."

Critical Analysis

Logic Assessment: This is first-rate technical journalism from a team with a real lab and TEM cross-sections. The analysis is methodical, comparison-based, and appropriately hedged. SemiAnalysis correctly contextualizes the clickbait-worthy "SMIC beats Intel 18A" headline — a single metal pitch metric means little in isolation. The teardown methodology (fin-cut + gate-cut TEM, EDS, die annotation) is rigorous.

Investment Implications: The direct investment implications are limited because SMIC, Huawei, and HiSilicon are not publicly traded on Western exchanges in a way most readers can access. The indirect reads are:

  • INTC (neutral): Intel 18A's M0 pitch being "beaten" by SMIC is headline risk, but the analysis shows 18A's architecture (PowerVia, HP libraries) is deliberately optimized differently. Not a real competitive threat to Intel in datacenter or PC.
  • TSM (neutral): TSMC N6 comparison shows SMIC has caught up to a mature node, not the leading edge. TSMC's moat (N3P, N2, EUV) remains wide.
  • The LogicFolding / τ scaling analysis is important for anyone assessing the long-term efficacy of export controls.

What's Missing: Cost-per-transistor comparison — SMIC's SAQP approach for M0 requires more masks and tighter overlay control than TSMC's equivalent, making cost significantly higher. Also missing: yield data comparison (mentioned qualitatively but not quantified).

Self-Promotion Note: The report doubles as a marketing piece for STEEL lab, with multiple calls to contact sales@semianalysis.com. The TechInsights competitive framing ("virtually no credible competition for decades") is aggressive but factual.

Confidence: HIGH on technical accuracy. MEDIUM on geopolitical/sanctions implications (prediction, not engineering).

TIL: The Calculator Wars And the Revenge That Took a Decade
Published June 13, 2026  |  ~855 words  |  Historical essay

Summary

A well-written historical essay about the 1970s "Calculator Wars" and how Texas Instruments weaponized its vertical integration against customers like Bowmar and Commodore — then how Commodore's Jack Tramiel bought MOS Technology (maker of the 6502 CPU), waited a decade, and used TI's own playbook to destroy TI's home computer business in 1983.

"The rhythm keeps recurring: a chipmaker enables a brand-new class of device, integrates downward to squeeze out the assemblers buying its parts, then provokes a wave of defensive vertical integration from whoever survives."

The essay draws an explicit parallel to today: Nvidia moving into full systems, Apple/Amazon/hyperscalers rolling their own accelerators. The implication is that "the next Tramiel" — someone who integrates backward into silicon to compete with Nvidia — is out there. No specific investment thesis, but frames the strategic landscape for AI hardware competition.
