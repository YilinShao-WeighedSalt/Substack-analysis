AVGO Post-Earnings −12%: AVGO Didn't Miss — The Bar Did

Published: June 4, 2026 | Tickers: AVGO

Summary

KOVA's analysis of Broadcom's after-hours crash centers on a critical distinction: this was not an earnings miss but an expectations reset. After market close, AVGO delivered Q2 FY2026 revenue of $22.2 billion (+48% YoY), AI semiconductor income of $10.8 billion (+143% YoY), non-GAAP EPS beat, and free cash flow of $10.26 billion. Despite these objectively strong numbers, the stock fell over 13% in after-hours trading.

The author argues this is "more dangerous and more common" than a genuine earnings blowup. The key disappointment was that management reiterated rather than raised its FY2027 AI revenue target of $100B+, and guided Q3 AI chip revenue to $16 billion versus the $17.2 billion Street estimate. At a ~$2 trillion market cap with a PE around 94x, "good" wasn't good enough — the stock needed to deliver beyond what anyone could imagine.

KOVA promised to cover historical parallels of similar post-earnings reactions and planned to address portfolio positioning in a member livestream the following morning.

"This is not a blowup. This is an expectations reset. Separating it from a real earnings disaster is the most important thing to understand tonight."

Critical Analysis

Logic assessment: The "expectations reset vs. blowup" framing is sound and well-articulated. The distinction between fundamental quality and valuation-driven reactions is the correct lens for this event. The Q2 numbers were genuinely strong by any historical standard.

What's missing: The author doesn't address the structural concern Hock Tan raised on the call — that Google is diversifying away from Broadcom for custom silicon (Customer-Owned Tooling). This isn't a temporary expectations gap; it's a potential permanent revenue risk for one of Broadcom's biggest AI customers. Semi Doped flagged this; KOVA did not.

What would prove this wrong: If Google's CoT shift is accelerating, the FY27 $100B+ target may not be conservative — it may be aspirational. If more hyperscalers follow Google's lead in owning their chip design process, Broadcom's custom silicon moat narrows significantly.

Cherry-picking risk: The article focuses heavily on one quarter's strength while glossing over the forward guidance miss. At 94x trailing PE, the market isn't pricing Q2 — it's pricing FY28-FY30. The author's bullish undertone doesn't match the recommendation to hold steady.

Confidence: MEDIUM — The framing is correct for a single-day reaction, but the CoT risk and structurally elevated valuation create real downside tail risk that isn't addressed.

TIL: The Man Who Invented the Future, Then Starved to Death in It

Published: June 6, 2026 | Tickers: None

Summary

A historical narrative about Oleg Losev (1903–1942), a Soviet technician who at age 18 built what we now call an LED in 1922 — 25 years before the rest of the world caught up. Working with carborundum crystal detectors, Losev identified the phenomenon as the inverse of the photoelectric effect, patented it, and predicted it would replace incandescent bulbs. He also discovered negative resistance in zincite crystals, building fully functional solid-state radios by 1924 (Hugo Gernsback named them "Crystodyne").

His class background as the son of a retired Tsarist Army captain blocked academic advancement. He received a doctorate from the Ioffe Physical-Technical Institute in 1938 only because the institution waived the thesis requirement entirely — acknowledging that his 43 papers and 16 author's certificates made it moot. He died of starvation during the Siege of Leningrad on January 22, 1942, at age 38. Shortly before his death, he mailed a manuscript describing a three-electrode semiconductor device to Physical Review. The paper was lost in the wartime Atlantic. Five years later, Shockley, Bardeen, and Brattain invented the transistor at Bell Labs independently.

"The semiconductor industry has always had this quality: the difference between a pioneer and a founder is often just access to materials, capital, and time."

Critical Analysis

Investment relevance: None — this is a historical essay with no tickers, theses, or market implications. It's well-written and provides cultural context for the semiconductor industry's relationship with timing and access. Skip for trading purposes; read for intellectual enrichment.

Confidence: HIGH — Factual historical content. Not applicable to investment decisions.

Deep: Glasswing/Mythos — AI-Native Cyber Reshapes the Cybersecurity Value Chain

Published: June 4, 2026 | Tickers: PANW, CRWD, MSFT, GOOGL, CYBR (Long) | QLYS, TENB, RPD (Cautious)

Summary

FundaAI's deep analysis examines the implications of Anthropic expanding Project Glasswing to ~200 partners (from initial ~50) across 15+ countries. The expansion covers critical infrastructure: power, water, healthcare, communications, hardware. FundaAI frames this as not a simple model pilot but a fundamental shift in cybersecurity's value chain.

Three reasons this matters: (1) Shift from capability validation to controlled real-world deployment — technical barriers to broader Mythos rollout are declining; (2) Proactive dual-use risk management — Anthropic chose vetted partner network over public GA; (3) Governance/productization rehearsal for Mythos-class models ahead of commercialization.

Mythos capabilities beyond traditional scanning (5 areas):

(1) Vulnerability Discovery: Stronger contextual understanding and cross-file reasoning vs. rule-based scanners. Channel feedback: Mythos surfaces "vulnerabilities that traditional tools have failed to find for years." Direct substitution risk for DAST/SAST players like Veracode and Snyk.

(2) Exploitability Analysis: Deeper contextual analysis to assess which vulnerabilities are real, externally triggerable, and actually exploitable. Shifts center of gravity from "listing CVEs" to "identifying real attack risk."

(3) Exploit Chain Construction: Key breakthrough — ability to chain multiple individually low-severity issues into high-risk exploit chains. Enterprises need to shift from "do I have this vulnerability?" to "can an adversary weaponize this?" Channel contacts believe SIEM and MDR could be materially disrupted within 12-18 months by agentic SOC tools like 7AI and Dropzone AI.

(4) Proof Generation: Generates evidence to validate findings, reducing triage burden and improving security-to-engineering handoff efficiency. Addresses the root cause of scanner findings failing to enter remediation loops: high false-positive rates.

(5) Remediation/Patch Suggestion: Anthropic's Claude Security (public beta) extends beyond discovery into remediation workflows, CI/CD integration, Jira/GitHub workflow completion, fix validation, and compliance records.

Investment implications: Long PANW, MSFT, CRWD, GOOGL, CYBR — platform vendors with breadth, cross-domain telemetry, remediation loops, and SOC control points. Cautious QLYS, TENB, RPD — business models dependent on traditional vulnerability scanning face "dual pressure of functional commoditization and multiple compression."

"Models such as Claude Mythos Preview are likely to push the marginal cost of vulnerability discovery another step lower, while shifting the center of gravity in cybersecurity from 'finding vulnerabilities' toward later-stage, more closed-loop workflows."

Critical Analysis

Thesis quality: This is the most substantive and original analysis in this scan cycle. The framework — AI-native cyber capability shifts value from discovery (commoditized) to validation/remediation (scarce) — is logically rigorous and has clear investment implications. The 5-capability taxonomy is well-structured and maps cleanly to competitive positioning.

Key risk to thesis: The analysis assumes Anthropic will maintain the vetted-partner model rather than broadly releasing Mythos capabilities. If Mythos goes GA (or if OpenAI/Google build comparable security models and release them broadly), then the "platform advantage" thesis weakens — everyone gets the capability, and the differentiator becomes integration, not AI access. FundaAI's PANW/CRWD bull case depends on platform vendors having preferential access to frontier cyber models, which is not guaranteed.

12-18 month SIEM/MDR disruption timeline: This is an aggressive call based on "channel contacts." AI security tools replacing established SIEM/MDR workflows in production environments typically takes 3-5 years due to compliance requirements, enterprise procurement cycles, and integration complexity. The 12-18 month timeline may reflect pilot excitement rather than deployment reality.

Cherry-picking risk: The analysis mentions "channel feedback" multiple times but doesn't identify sources. Unverifiable industry checks are the weakest form of evidence. Channel checks can be biased by respondent selection — Anthropic partners are inherently bullish on Anthropic products.

What would prove this wrong: (1) Mythos-class models go open-source or GA, democratizing the capability and removing platform advantage. (2) QLYS/TENB successfully integrate AI-native capabilities into their existing platforms, defending their positions. (3) Enterprise security buyers treat AI scanning as complementary to, not substitutive for, existing tools — expanding the market rather than redistributing share.

Confidence: MEDIUM — Excellent framework, but the timeline is aggressive and the evidence is channel-check dependent. The long-term direction is probably right; the speed of disruption is probably overstated.
