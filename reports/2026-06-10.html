DeepSeekV4 1.6T Day 0 to Day 43 Performance Over Time

NVDA AMD Huawei

Full Summary

This is SemiAnalysis's InferenceX team's comprehensive benchmark analysis of DeepSeek V4 (1.6 trillion parameter model) inference performance across every major hardware platform — NVIDIA GB300 NVL72, B200, B300, AMD MI355X, and Huawei Ascend 950DT — tracking improvements from Day 0 through Day 43 post-release.

Key finding #1: NVIDIA GB300 NVL72 dominates. The GB300 rack-scale architecture is "unbeatable across all interactivity levels" with MTP (Multi-Token Prediction) enabled. The cost per million output tokens reaches $0.156 at 50 tok/s/user (8k input, 1k output). The rack's 72-GPU NVLink domain enables expert parallelism wide enough to keep DeepSeek V4's MoE dispatch/combine entirely on NVLink, avoiding slower scale-out fabric. CoreWeave provided the compute for these benchmarks after SemiAnalysis's own GB300 cluster was down at launch.

Key finding #2: AMD MI355X achieved a 100x performance improvement in 26 days. On Day 0, the MI355X was "unusable" — delivering only 1-2 tokens per user per second, far below human reading speed. The AMD SGLang team led by HaiShaw systematically replaced PyTorch-native fallback paths with real AITER, Triton, TileLang, and FlyDSL kernels, eventually achieving pareto-frontier performance that exceeded H200 at lower interactivity levels. Key improvements: AITER mHC kernels, SWA-prepare Triton implementation, FlashMLA migration from TileLang to Triton, AITER FlyDSL FP4 MoE kernel, and fused hash-topk.

Key finding #3: CUDA ecosystem moat is real but narrowing. Both vLLM and SGLang supported native DeepSeek V4 on CUDA the moment the model was released. Distributed inferencing works on Day 0 for CUDA. By contrast, AMD's ROCm stack didn't work on Day 0, and TensorRT-LLM had a critical hidden-size bug (hardcoded FHC_HIDDEN = 4096 when V4 Pro uses 7168). SemiAnalysis had to fix NVIDIA's own open-source mHC kernel code. However, AMD's ROCm vLLM distributed inference for non-DeepSeekV4 models has finally been enabled.

Key finding #4: Huawei Ascend 950DT achieved Day 0 support. This is the first major open model with first-class Huawei support on Day 0 — only the second stack (along with CUDA) to achieve this. DeepSeek's official API has been partly served on Huawei since Day 0. CANN (Compute Architecture for Neural Networks) delivered optimized inference with MC² compute-communication fusion, independent AIC/AIV core scheduling, and on-device AI CPU for metadata operations. The 950 chip (codename "David") comes in two flavors: 950PR (Prefill/Recommendation, lower cost) and 950DT (Decode/Training, higher bandwidth).

Key finding #5: B200 power efficiency improved 1.7x purely through software. Token throughput per all-in provisioned utility megawatt went from ~300K tok/s/MW on Day 0 to ~500K tok/s/MW by June 5th for 50 tok/s/user interactivity. Since B200's all-in power envelope is fixed at ~2.17 kW/GPU, this is a pure software gain from MegaMoE grouped-FP4 GEMMs, wider EP, FP4 weight path, and scheduler tuning.

Key finding #6: DeepSeek V4 architectural innovations. The model introduces Compressed Sparse Attention (CSA) and Heavily Compressed Attention (HCA), replacing MLA. This achieves 50x KV cache reduction at 1M context length. MegaMoE splits experts into waves for finer-grained overlapping, claiming 1.92x theoretical speedup. DeepSeek went "all in" on deterministic computation for RL training stability, writing custom batch-invariant kernels for all operations.

Key finding #7: Open-source inference ecosystem dynamics. Both vLLM (now Inferact) and SGLang (now RadixArk) have raised hundreds of millions each to continue fueling their open-source inference engines. Chinese labs continue dominating the open model landscape, with Kimi K2.6 still beating NVIDIA's Nemotron 3 Ultra on coding. AMD is criticized for re-focusing on ATOM (which has "zero production customers") instead of native vLLM that major customers actually use.

Critical Analysis

Logic assessment: The technical analysis is exceptionally rigorous — this is first-party benchmark data from SemiAnalysis's own InferenceX initiative, making it highly credible. The throughput-interactivity curves are real measurements, not estimates. However, the article is fundamentally promotional of the InferenceX platform and its supporters (CoreWeave, NVIDIA, Oracle, Microsoft, etc.), which creates an inherent bias toward showcasing the value of these partnerships.

Bias/conflict of interest: SemiAnalysis has a commercial relationship with CoreWeave (who provided GB300 compute), and InferenceX is supported by NVIDIA, Oracle, Microsoft, and others. The article prominently credits these supporters. The critique of AMD's ATOM engine ("zero production customers") and TensorRT-LLM bugs serves SemiAnalysis's narrative that open-source inference (vLLM/SGLang) is superior — which aligns with InferenceX's mission.

What would invalidate this thesis? If AMD's ATOM or a proprietary inference engine achieved meaningfully better production performance than vLLM/SGLang, the open-source dominance narrative weakens. If Huawei's Day 0 support is only viable for Chinese-designed models (not Western architectures), the "David vs. Goliath" framing overstates the threat. If GB300 NVL72 supply remains severely constrained, its theoretical superiority may be academic for most deployments.

Key gap: The cost-per-token analysis is paywalled. The article shows performance but doesn't provide the TCO comparison that would make this actionable for investors deciding between NVDA and AMD exposure. Also, the AMD 100x improvement narrative could be misleading — going from "completely broken" to "functional" is very different from delivering competitive production performance.

Confidence: HIGH — First-party benchmark data with transparent methodology. The NVDA inference dominance thesis is well-supported. The AMD software improvement narrative is real but overstated in its investment implications.

China's Unitree Will Dominate Global Robotics

Unitree (pre-IPO) TSLA BYDDY

Full Summary

SemiAnalysis presents a comprehensive bullish investment case for Unitree Robotics ahead of its highly anticipated IPO on the Shanghai Stock Exchange. The central thesis: Unitree is executing the "DJI Strategy" — own the critical bottleneck component (actuators, 50-70% of humanoid BoM), bootstrap a willing niche audience (researchers/hobbyists), ride the Chinese manufacturing ecosystem, and let each hardware generation unlock the next market. The piece draws extensive parallels to BYD (battery cells → EVs → global automotive dominance) and DJI (flight controllers → consumer drones → 70% global market share).

Financial profile: Unitree is tripling revenues YoY on 60% gross margin product lines. They plan ~$300M in AI R&D spend. The G1 humanoid's BoM has been engineered down to $8,976 with 67% gross margins at a $27.3K price point. SemiAnalysis has "already heard pricing well under $20K in some deals." Pre-tax pricing has been slashed from $50K+ to $27.3K over 12-18 months, and BoM is set to plummet further as manufacturing scales.

The QDD actuator bet: Unlike competitors using expensive HarmonicDrive strainwave gearboxes (~13-step manufacturing process, decades to perfect), Unitree chose quasi-direct-drive (QDD) actuators — simpler, cheaper (up to 80% cheaper), higher efficiency (95-98% vs 85-90% for strainwaves). Early critics were right that QDDs caused overheating — the original G1 could only hold 2kg for a few seconds before needing 30-minute cooldown. But after two years of iteration (reduced cogging torque, reshaped magnets, denser copper windings, active cooling around pelvis), the G1 now sustains 5kg for 10-15 minutes with arms bent — a 2x payload increase and 5x duration improvement. A new QDD redesign becomes a sample actuator within weeks at Unitree vs. 3+ months for Western humanoid companies.

Deployment viability: SemiAnalysis estimates ~250 Unitree humanoids are deployed in productive industry settings, with one company running 30 G1s and multiple companies with 5-6 each. The primary use case is lightweight materials handling — carrying 2-5kg totes from A to B in e-commerce settings, similar to Agility Robotics' GXO deployment. Even with fully conservative assumptions (100% teleoperation, 15% service contract, 2-year useful life, zero residual, two shifts), Unitree's economics are "currently passing below the $30/hour labor cost of a human." The robot runs 10-15 minutes of work followed by 5-10 minutes of cooling, yielding 50-67% utilization.

Competitive landscape: Tesla "first unveiled a humanoid in 2022, and while it and other Western players are now producing early humanoids that remain works in progress, we hear Unitree may ship its 10,000th in the coming weeks." NVIDIA, Apple, and Meta have all purchased hundreds of G1 units for AI research. Western startups (Sunday Robotics, Dyna, XDOF) already run hardware teams out of China. Even Tesla Optimus sources from the Chinese supply chain.

Manufacturing advantage: Unitree self-develops BLDC motors, planetary gearboxes, LiDARs, and depth cameras. Their IPO filing shows quadruped margins improved from 42.36% to 55.49% while costs dropped nearly in half. Within China, ~200 humanoid companies now exist, all contributing to and benefiting from the ecosystem Unitree helped create.

Critical Analysis

Logic assessment: The BYD/DJI analogy is compelling but risks selection bias — for every BYD there are dozens of Chinese hardware companies that failed to achieve ecosystem dominance. The key assumptions (QDD reliability improvement trajectory continues, deployment economics scale linearly, Western competitors can't close the cost gap) each carry meaningful risk.

Unstated assumptions: (1) That teleop-to-autonomy transition will happen smoothly — current economics assume full teleoperation, but the thesis implicitly requires AI-driven autonomy to unlock larger TAMs. (2) That Western governments won't impose tariffs or restrictions on Chinese humanoid robots the way they have on EVs (US already imposed 100% tariffs on Chinese EVs). (3) That the IPO valuation will be reasonable relative to current revenues.

Cherry-picked data? The 250 deployed units and $30/hour viability calculation uses Agility Robotics' task profile (tote handoff) which is specifically optimized for Unitree's current limitations. Real-world deployments involving heavier payloads, dexterous manipulation, or longer duty cycles would tell a very different story. The "10,000th humanoid shipping" claim is sourced as "we hear" — unverified.

What would need to be true for this to be WRONG? (1) QDD actuators hit a thermal ceiling that prevents meaningful payload improvement beyond 5-10kg. (2) Western companies (Tesla, Figure, Agility) achieve cost parity through their own supply chain deals. (3) Geopolitical restrictions limit Unitree's addressable market. (4) The IPO prices in the growth story at excessive multiples.

Author bias: SemiAnalysis has established relationships with Unitree (they received detailed BoM data, and referenced prior coverage from their quadrupeds piece). The piece reads as fundamentally promotional of the Unitree thesis, with limited engagement on bear cases beyond acknowledging early QDD weaknesses.

Confidence: MEDIUM — Unitree's manufacturing advantage is real and well-documented, but the investment case depends on unproven deployment scaling and an uncertain IPO valuation. The BYD/DJI parallels are instructive but not predictive.

State of the Themes: June 2026

GOOGL AI Infra broadly NVDA MU

Full Summary

Citrini publishes their semi-annual thematic universe update, covering performance attribution, forward-looking positioning, and a major new macro risk narrative they call "Token Panic." The Citrindex model portfolio's four of six baskets have outperformed the US market YTD, with AI and Robotics leading.

Track record highlights: In August 2025, Citrini called Google (GOOG) "the most asymmetric large cap AI winner" — it doubled over 9 months. They published a bullish Korea thesis — KOSPI tripled since. Called for Taiwan to outperform the US — TWSE outperformed QQQ by 60%+. They acknowledge being "too cautious at points — like cutting memory exposure early in the year."

The Token Panic thesis (most important section): In weeks, the narrative has shifted from "tokenmaxxing" to "tokenpanic." Enterprise AI costs are exploding: Uber burned through its entire AI budget in four months. An anonymous $500M budget overrun was reported. Anthropic's ARR reached $45B in May (5x increase since start of year per The Economist). Sam Altman confirmed: "My company spent my entire 2026 budget in Q1. Can you make this more efficient? That went from an issue that never came up to all of a sudden a huge issue." Microsoft's AI Chief cancelled Claude Code licenses in May, saying: "Anthropic is extremely expensive, and I think many people are urgently looking for alternatives."

Pricing shifts driving panic: All major providers have shifted to usage-based pricing in near-lockstep: OpenAI changed Codex pricing (April 2), Google changed Gemini to "compute-used" model (May 19), Microsoft GitHub Copilot moved to usage-based billing (June 1). Claude Opus 4.7/4.8 uses a "new tokenizer" that may use up to 35% more tokens for the same text — effectively a hidden price increase.

Chinese alternatives: DeepSeek V4 and Qwen 3.7 are 10-25x cheaper than Opus 4.8 and GPT 5.5. DeepSeek has shot past Anthropic to the top of OpenRouter in tokens processed. Cursor's new model is post-trained on xAI compute using a Moonshot base model — "comparable to 4.7 and 5.5 for 10x lower cost per task." The trend of application-layer companies post-training on open-source base models for specialized workflows is accelerating.

Implications for the AI trade: Citrini emphasizes this isn't the end of AI spending — "revenues for labs and hyperscalers are going to grow" and "this won't fix near-term compute constraints." But cost and efficiency become more important. Themes to watch: local inference, miniaturization, smart routing, observability, price competition, efficient model architecture. The full article (paywalled) covers Edge AI, DRAM optimization, agentic utilities, AI losers, robotics, EU Chips Act, Colombia, oil capex, Shopify, Hyperliquid, healthcare, and "The Girlfriend Index" (GOP loses the House).

"Free-AI is ending. Tokenomics is beginning. What happens when underlying costs of compute become more transparent and directly traceable to outcomes? The ROI debate is about to be answered in real time, across millions of users and use cases."

Critical Analysis

Logic assessment: The Token Panic thesis is the most important macro narrative in this scan. The evidence is compelling and multi-sourced (Uber, anonymous reports, Altman direct quotes, Microsoft AI Chief). The shift from subsidized AI to usage-based monetization is real and well-documented. The pricing data (four providers moving to usage-based in near-lockstep) is particularly strong evidence of coordination.

However, this narrative has limits: (1) Enterprise AI cost blowouts are partially a demand signal — companies are using AI so much they're burning budgets, which is bullish for infrastructure. (2) The "switch to Chinese alternatives" framing overstates the practical barrier to adoption for large US enterprises dealing with compliance, security, and data sovereignty requirements. (3) Citrini admits they were "too cautious" cutting memory exposure earlier — they may be pattern-matching to their prior mistake.

What would invalidate this thesis? If token costs continue falling fast enough (via efficiency gains, competition, model distillation) that total AI spend grows modestly even as token consumption explodes, the "panic" resolves without damaging the infrastructure trade. If enterprises simply increase AI budgets rather than cut back, the revenue growth narrative holds.

Cherry-picked data? The Uber budget blowout and $500M anonymous report are anecdotes, not systematic evidence. Citrini doesn't present data on what percentage of enterprises are experiencing budget overruns vs. those operating within budget. The Claude tokenizer price increase claim ("up to 35% more tokens") needs verification — this could be an architectural choice rather than deliberate price extraction.

Conflict of interest: Citrini manages the Citrindex model portfolio, so their thematic calls directly affect their positioning and trading. The strong track record cited (GOOG doubled, KOSPI tripled) creates survivorship bias in credibility. They acknowledge underperformance areas (cutting memory too early) which adds credibility.

Confidence: HIGH — The Token Panic narrative is well-evidenced and represents a genuine risk to the AI infrastructure trade's most aggressive valuations. The resolution is likely in the middle: AI budgets grow but more slowly, efficiency becomes paramount, and Chinese alternatives gain share at the margin.
