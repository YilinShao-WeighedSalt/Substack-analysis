Global Semi Research

Co-Packaged Optics Is Not Delayed. SemiAnalysis Is Just Wrong
Jun 10, 2026 • Paid • ~3,500+ words (truncated at paywall)
LITE Long COHR Long AAOI Long GLW Long MRVL Long

Summary

Global Semi Research (GSR) fires a direct broadside at SemiAnalysis's June 9 report "Powered Down, Lights Off," which argued co-packaged optics (CPO) shipments are being revised down through 2027 with scale-up pushed to 2029. GSR says this thesis is categorically wrong and is "a positioning call wearing a supply-chain costume."

The 19% yield argument is bogus. SemiAnalysis's core claim rests on a single calculation: assume 95% optical-engine attach yield, compound it across all 32 COUPEs per Spectrum-6 ASIC, and 0.95^32 = ~19% system yield. GSR calls this "a magic trick" that freezes yield at one pessimistic snapshot and raises it to the 32nd power "as though yield never improves, as though there is no screening, no binning, no redundancy." GSR notes SemiAnalysis itself contradicts this by observing that Nvidia's Quantum-X with just 3 COUPEs per module is in "comparatively better shape" because of module screening — which is an admission that the 0.95^32 framing is an artifact of module granularity, not a physics constraint. Furthermore, Spectrum-X carries redundant engines (32 active plus spares), so a single bad coupling doesn't kill the switch.

GSR concedes the real concerns: soldered switch substrates have no rework path, Spectrum-6 reportedly showed >3.5 dB insertion loss in system-level testing (enough to eat the optical channel budget), and integration at 32 engines is genuinely hard. Neither Nvidia nor TSMC had pinned the root cause at the time of writing.

The supply chain tells a different story. GSR then pivots to the question SemiAnalysis "never answers": if CPO is sliding to 2029, why is the upstream supply chain in its most violent shortage ever?

  • Laser orders: Nvidia's guidance to Coherent and Lumentum for high-power CW lasers climbed from ~40M units in January to ~100M units by April/May — a 2.5x jump that likely pulls some 2027-2028 demand forward.
  • Translation: That implies 10M+ ELSFP external-laser units, which back-solves to CPO shipments in the hundreds of thousands: 200K-300K switches in 2027 scaling to 600K-800K in 2028. This squares with TSMC's COUPE production plan.
  • Lumentum has announced its largest ELSFP purchase order ever. Both Lumentum and Coherent state they expect to ship into scale-up hardware by 2027.
  • TSMC COUPE ramp: Per GSR's own checks, the ramp is running ahead of expectations, not behind. TSMC has thrown "enormous resources" at it.

Market impact: Within hours of the SemiAnalysis note, Lumentum, Coherent, Applied Optoelectronics, Corning, and Marvell all sold off. GSR argues this is a buying opportunity created by misanalysis.

"Nvidia does not scale high-power laser orders from 40 million to 100 million units, and Lumentum does not book out an entire FA production line, for a technology that is supposedly delivering 'meaningfully below prior expectations.'"

Critical Analysis

Where GSR is compelling: The supply-chain evidence is GSR's strongest card. Order volumes are observable and hard to fake. A 2.5x ramp in laser orders from Jan to Apr/May is a concrete, verifiable data point that is very hard to reconcile with a "CPO is delayed" thesis. Lumentum booking its largest ELSFP order ever is a corporate commitment, not a forecast.

Where GSR stretches: The article dismisses the 3.5 dB insertion loss finding quickly but this is a genuine technical risk. Even if yield improves (and it will), system-level optical loss in the channel budget is a harder problem than manufacturing yield — it's a physics and design constraint. GSR's "TSMC is running ahead of expectations" is based on anonymous supply chain checks with no verification possible by the reader.

Bias/conflict: GSR has historically been bullish on the optics supply chain and likely has positions in LITE/COHR. This is a direct public feud with SemiAnalysis, which raises the temperature of the claims. Both sides have skin in the game.

What would make this thesis WRONG: If the 3.5 dB insertion loss proves to be a fundamental design flaw requiring a generation-level fix (not just a yield improvement), then the laser orders could end up as inventory write-downs rather than deployed CPO switches. Also, "demand pull-forward" cuts both ways: if orders were accelerated from 2027-2028 into 2026, future quarters could disappoint even if CPO ultimately works.

Verdict: MEDIUM-HIGH Confidence — Supply chain evidence strongly supports GSR's position, but the insertion-loss issue is underexplored and could be the real bottleneck.

DeepSeekV4 1.6T Day 0 to Day 43 Performance Over Time
Jun 9, 2026 • Paid • ~6,300 words (free portion)
NVDA Long AMD Neutral Huawei Watch

Summary

SemiAnalysis's InferenceX team provides the most comprehensive open-source benchmarking of DeepSeek V4 Pro (1.6T parameter MoE model) inference performance across every major hardware platform, tracking improvements from Day 0 through Day 43 post-release. This is a first-of-its-kind longitudinal study of inference optimization.

Key Day 0 findings:

  • CUDA (SGLang/vLLM): Both supported native DeepSeek V4 Pro out of the box on Day 0. Multi-node disaggregated prefill on GB200 NVL72 delivered up to 5x better results vs B200 runs using Dynamo vLLM recipe. MTP speculative decoding arrived Day 3 from SGLang, substantially improving throughput at higher interactivities.
  • AMD MI355X: Day 0 was a disaster — only FP8 worked (no native FP4+FP8), delivering 1-2 tokens/user/second, "an unusable experience far below average user reading speeds." Distributed inference still doesn't work on DeepSeek V4 Pro.
  • TensorRT-LLM: Didn't support DeepSeek V4 out of the box due to hardcoded FHC_HIDDEN=4096 (V4 Pro uses 7168). Nvidia engineers "fixed" this by simply removing the guard rather than adding proper support. SemiAnalysis had to fix and submit a PR. The bug caused hidden state corruption and invalid generations for over a week.
  • Huawei Ascend 950DT: First major open model with Day 0 support on Ascend, with DeepSeek's own API partly served on Huawei from Day 0 — a significant milestone vs last year when only CUDA worked on Day 0.

Performance Over Time (the headline story):

  • AMD MI355X: 100x improvement in 26 days. The AMD team led by HaiShaw replaced PyTorch-native fallback paths with real AITER, Triton, TileLang, and FlyDSL kernels. Gains came from: FP4 weight MoE, TileLang FlashMLA kernels, AITER mHC kernels (enabling MI355X to exceed H200 for first time at lower interactivity), and ultimately retiring all remaining fallbacks. By Week 4, MTP was working across all frameworks.
  • B300: 3x improvement in under a week from MegaMoE grouped FP4 GEMM and switching from EP8 to EP4.
  • GB300 NVL72: Dramatic improvement from W4A4 MegaMoE and decode topology rewrite widening to EP=16, scaling prefill workers, pushing concurrency to 21,504. GB300 "dominates and mogs all other inference systems."
  • Cost per million output tokens on GB300: $0.156 at 50 tok/s/user (8k in, 1k out). This is the new inference TCO benchmark.
  • B200 power efficiency: 300K tok/s/MW on Day 0 improved to 500K tok/s/MW by June 5 — a pure ~1.7x software gain with no hardware changes.

ROCm vLLM remains disappointing: Performance lags far behind CUDA vLLM. SemiAnalysis notes AMD is "re-focusing on ATOM (an inference engine that serves 0 production tokens) instead of focusing on native vLLM (an inference engine that lots of their major customers use)."

DeepSeek V4 Architecture: Features Compressed Sparse Attention (CSA) and Heavily Compressed Attention (HCA), achieving 50x KV cache reduction at 1M context. New MegaMoE kernel achieves theoretical 1.92x speedup by splitting experts into waves for finer-grained compute-comms overlap.

Huawei 950DT deep dive: Detailed trace analysis reveals CANN's MC2 (merged compute-communication) operators, dual-master AIC/AIV architecture, on-device AI CPU for metadata planning (vs host round-trips), and Day 0 optimization showing Huawei can now ship kernels on Day 0 for Chinese model releases. Internal codename for Ascend 950 is "David" (as in David vs Goliath/Nvidia).

"The biblical story that gave the Ascend 950 its internal codename ends with the giant face-down. But the Goliath in that story stood still and let David sling stones at him, whereas Nvidia's Goliath is constantly in motion, ships a new architecture every year and improves existing architectures."

Critical Analysis

Exceptional technical depth. This is genuinely first-rate systems engineering analysis. The longitudinal performance tracking is unique — no one else tracks inference optimization trajectories this systematically. The InferenceX GitHub repo is open-source and verifiable.

The CUDA moat thesis is reinforced: Day 0 support on CUDA vs weeks of painful bootstrapping on ROCm is the clearest evidence yet for Nvidia's software ecosystem advantage. The TensorRT-LLM embarrassment actually cuts both ways: it shows Nvidia's proprietary stack is brittle, but the open CUDA ecosystem (vLLM/SGLang) compensates.

AMD's 100x improvement is real but misleading. Starting from an "unusable" baseline means any progress looks exponential. The MI355X still can't run distributed inference for DeepSeek V4 Pro, which is a fundamental limitation for production deployment. The progress is impressive but the starting point was terrible.

Huawei as the second Day-0 platform: This is geopolitically significant. Two stacks now have Day 0 support for frontier Chinese models (CUDA + CANN), vs only CUDA a year ago. This matters for China's AI compute sovereignty.

What would make this thesis WRONG: If AMD's ROCm team delivers distributed inference and competitive Day 0 support in the next major model release, the "CUDA moat" narrative weakens. Also, the GB300 NVL72 results are impressive but depend on CoreWeave-donated compute — production deployments with diverse workloads may not replicate benchmark conditions.

Verdict: HIGH Confidence — Technically rigorous, open-source verifiable, and the conclusions follow from the data. The NVDA bullish implication is well-supported.

China's Unitree Will Dominate Global Robotics
Jun 8, 2026 • Paid • ~5,700 words (free portion)
Unitree IPO Western Humanoids

Summary

SemiAnalysis makes a sweeping, well-researched case that Unitree, the Chinese robotics company preparing for its IPO, will dominate the global humanoid robotics market by running the "DJI/BYD playbook": own the critical component, bootstrap through a willing niche audience, ride ecosystem cost curves, and let each hardware generation unlock the next market.

The BYD/DJI parallel: BYD owned the battery cell (30-40% of EV BoM), scaled from niche to global #1 EV maker, created the modern affordable EV market with Blade Battery, now in-houses ~75% of vehicle production. DJI owned the flight controller, turned a $679 Phantom 1 into global consumer drone dominance (70% market share by 2016-17), crushing 3DR, GoPro Karma, Parrot. Unitree owns the actuator (50-70% of humanoid BoM).

Unitree's trajectory:

  • Founded by Wang Xingxing (ex-DJI), started as quadruped company. Prices fell 94-96% in six years: Laikago $45K (2018) → Go2 $1,600-$2,800 (2025).
  • May ship its 10,000th humanoid soon. Tripling revenues YoY on 60% gross margins. Planning ~$300M AI R&D spend.
  • G1 humanoid: $27.3K list price, estimated 67% gross margin, with pricing "well under $20K in some deals." BoM estimated at $8,976.
  • QDD (quasi-direct-drive) actuator: cheaper (up to 80% vs strainwave), simpler, higher efficiency (95-98% vs 85-90%), but historically prone to overheating.

The QDD overheating fix: Unitree iterated on the core problem. Improvements include: reshaped/curved magnets and skewed slots for smoother torque (less cogging, less wasted current), denser copper windings ("Low Copper Consumption Coil"), active cooling around pelvis (Oct 2025 update). Result: G1 now sustains 10-15 minutes carrying 5kg with arms bent (2x payload, 5x duration improvement). A new QDD redesign can become a sample actuator within weeks vs 3+ months for Western competitors.

Deployment economics: Using Agility Robotics' tote-handoff task as baseline (2-4 kg loads, 66 totes/hour), Unitree G1 matches Digit's throughput with conservative assumptions: full teleoperation, 15% service contract, 2-year life, zero residual value, two shifts. Result: Unitree is currently passing below the $30/hour labor cost threshold. ~250 Unitrees deployed in labor settings, one company with 30 G1s deployed.

Manufacturing advantage: Unitree self-develops BLDC motors, planetary gearboxes, LiDARs, depth cameras. Their motors run 30-40% of equivalent Western cost. Quadruped margins improved from 42.36% to 55.49% while costs dropped nearly in half. Even within China's competitive ecosystem (~200 humanoid companies), Unitree is exceptional on verticalization.

"No humanoid is at mass-scale production/deployments yet, all are still iterating on the technology... but if Unitree unlocks even a slice of the warehouse TAM, progress can accelerate at breakneck speed."

Critical Analysis

The BYD/DJI analogy is powerful but imperfect. Both BYD and DJI operated in markets where the core task (driving, flying) was well-defined. Humanoid robotics is solving a much harder problem: general-purpose dexterous manipulation in unstructured environments. The comparison understates the software/AI gap. Unitree's current deployments are essentially "carry boxes A to B" — an AMR/conveyor could do this more cheaply.

The $30/hour crossover is cherry-picked. The calculation uses Agility's most favorable task (light tote handoff, low throughput, forgiving retries) and generous throughput assumptions for Unitree. Most warehouse tasks require >5 kg payloads, longer sustained operation, and dexterity the G1 doesn't have. The 50-67% utilization rate (15 min work, 5-10 min cooldown) is still terrible for a production environment.

Tesla/Figure are dismissed too quickly. SemiAnalysis acknowledges Tesla hasn't shipped Optimus externally, but Tesla's manufacturing scale, AI compute resources, and Dojo training infrastructure could close the gap rapidly if they commit. The report focuses on current state but the race is dynamic.

Unitree IPO timing raises a flag. This deeply bullish piece drops just as Unitree prepares its IPO. SemiAnalysis has no stated conflict, but the alignment of interests (SemiAnalysis gaining access/relationships, Unitree getting favorable pre-IPO coverage) deserves skepticism.

What would make this thesis WRONG: If humanoid robotics remains software-limited (AI capabilities plateau), hardware cost doesn't matter — cheap robots that can't do useful tasks are still useless. Also, if US/EU impose tariffs or restrictions on Chinese humanoids (as they did on EVs with 100% tariffs), Unitree's cost advantage doesn't translate to Western market share.

Verdict: MEDIUM Confidence — Excellent hardware/manufacturing analysis but the "dominance" thesis requires AI software breakthroughs and open trade that aren't guaranteed.

State of the Themes: June 2026
Jun 8, 2026 • Paid • ~1,800 words (free portion)
AI Infra Cautious GOOG Long Edge AI Long Open Source Watch

Summary

Citrini's semi-annual thematic review updates their "Citrindex" portfolio across six thematic baskets. The free portion focuses heavily on what they call "Token Panic" — the narrative shift from tokenmaxxing to tokenpanic in just weeks.

Track record claimed: Since last State of the Themes (Aug 2025): called Google the most asymmetric large-cap AI winner (GOOG doubled), published bullish Korea thesis (KOSPI tripled), called Taiwan outperformance (TWSE outperformed QQQ by 60%+). Four of six thematic baskets outperformed broader US market YTD.

Token Panic — the core thesis:

  • In March, explosive token consumption growth drove semiconductor industry valuations to double in 2 months. Now the goldilocks narrative hits a wall: explosive usage = explosive customer costs.
  • Key data points: Uber burned through its entire AI budget in 4 months. Anonymous report of a $500M "oopsie." Anthropic's ARR hit $45B in May (5x since start of year, per The Economist). Sam Altman confirmed cost is "all of a sudden, a huge issue" for customers.
  • Microsoft's AI Chief cancelled Claude Code licenses in May, saying "Anthropic is extremely expensive, and I think many people are urgently looking for alternatives."
  • Pricing shift: OpenAI (April: Codex to API token pricing), Google (May: Gemini to compute-used model), Microsoft (June: GitHub Copilot to usage-based billing). Frontier models using new tokenizers that consume up to 35% more tokens for same text.
  • Chinese alternatives: Qwen 3.7 and DeepSeek V4 are 10-25x cheaper than Opus 4.8/GPT 5.5 while behind on benchmarks. DeepSeek has passed Anthropic on OpenRouter token volume. Cursor's new model (Chinese open source base, post-trained on xAI compute) is comparable to Opus 4.7/GPT 5.5 at 10x lower cost.

What this means for the AI trade:

  • Lab and hyperscaler revenues will still grow. Near-term compute constraints won't ease.
  • But cost and efficiency become more important. Local inference, miniaturization, smart routing, observability, price competition, and efficient model architecture will grow.
  • "Free-AI is ending. Tokenomics is beginning."
  • "In many (most) situations, good enough will do."

Thematic updates teased (behind paywall): Edge AI/inference on-device, ways past the DRAM tax, agentic utilities update, AI losers, robotics, custom silicon, EU Chips Act, Colombia, oil capex cycle, Shopify, Hyperliquid, healthcare cyclical inflection, "The Girlfriend Index," GOP loses the House, oncology.

"We've gone from tokenmaxxing to tokenpanic. The corollary of explosive token usage is explosive cost to customers, which is coming just as the US labs and hyperscalers are turning up the dial on monetization."
"Why rent a Ferrari when a Vespa does the trick?"

Critical Analysis

Citrini's Token Panic thesis is the most important macro narrative in this scan cycle. The data points are concrete: Anthropic's $45B ARR, Uber's budget blowout, Microsoft cancelling Claude Code licenses, the synchronized shift to usage-based pricing. This isn't speculation — it's observable corporate behavior change.

The implication for the AI hardware trade is nuanced. Citrini explicitly says this won't fix near-term compute constraints and lab revenues will grow. But the mix shift matters: if "good enough" models (open source, Chinese alternatives at 10-25x lower cost) capture the majority of token volume, the premium pricing power of frontier labs erodes, and the downstream demand signal to chip companies becomes more price-sensitive.

The Chinese model threat is real. DeepSeek passing Anthropic on OpenRouter tokens processed, and Cursor (one of the most popular coding tools) choosing a Chinese base model over Western frontier models, are leading indicators that the cost gap is already driving switching behavior.

What would make this thesis WRONG: If frontier models develop capabilities so far ahead of open source that cost becomes secondary (e.g., models that can reliably replace entire job functions). Or if US policy restricts Chinese model deployment in enterprise settings. Also, Citrini's past track record claims should be taken with a grain of salt — they highlight winners (GOOG doubled!) but don't mention misses.

Verdict: HIGH Confidence — The Token Panic narrative is well-supported by multiple independent data points and has direct, observable corporate behavior changes backing it.
