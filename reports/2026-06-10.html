Co-Packaged Optics Is Not Delayed. SemiAnalysis Is Just Wrong

June 10, 2026  |  Paid post  |  Original

LITE COHR MRVL AAOI GLW

Summary

This is a forceful, point-by-point rebuttal of SemiAnalysis's June 9 report "Powered Down, Lights Off," which claimed co-packaged optics (CPO) would be delayed — with scale-out CPO shipments revised down through 2027 and scale-up pushed to 2029 — and that the photonics trade was overcrowded and due for a "violent unwind." The SemiAnalysis note triggered immediate selloffs in Lumentum, Coherent, Applied Optoelectronics, Corning, and Marvell.

Global Semi Research (GSR) calls the delay thesis "a positioning call wearing a supply-chain costume" and systematically dismantles it:

The "19% system yield" argument is a magic trick. SemiAnalysis's core CPO-is-late case rests on a single arithmetic flourish: assume 95% optical-engine attach yield, compound it across 32 COUPEs per Spectrum-6 ASIC, and 0.95^32 lands at roughly 19% system yield. GSR acknowledges the underlying concern is real — soldered switch substrates have no rework path, Spectrum 6 showed more than 3.5 dB of insertion loss in system-level testing (enough to eat the entire optical channel budget), and integration at 32 engines is genuinely hard. However, GSR argues this calculation "freezes yield at one pessimistic snapshot and raises it to the 32nd power" as though yield never improves, ignoring screening, binning, and redundancy. SemiAnalysis's own report contradicts itself by noting Quantum-X (with 3 COUPEs) is "in comparatively better shape" because you can screen modules — an admission that module granularity, not physics, drives the scary number. Spectrum-X carries redundant engines (32 active plus spares), so a single bad coupling doesn't brick the switch. TSMC has thrown "enormous resources" at COUPE, and per GSR's own checks, "the ramp is running ahead of expectations, not behind."

The supply chain tells the opposite story. GSR poses the devastating question SemiAnalysis never answers: "If CPO is sliding to 2029, why is the entire upstream supply chain in the most violent shortage it has ever seen, right now?"

  • NVIDIA's guidance to Coherent and Lumentum for high-power CW (continuous-wave) lasers climbed from roughly 40 million units in January to about 100 million units by April-May — a 2.5x jump that "very likely pulls some 2027-2028 demand forward."
  • This translates to approximately 10 million+ ELSFP external-laser units, back-solving to CPO shipments in the hundreds of thousands — 200,000–300,000 switches in 2027 scaling toward 600,000–800,000 in 2028.
  • Lumentum has announced its largest ELSFP purchase order ever. Both Lumentum and Coherent state they expect to ship into scale-up hardware by 2027.
  • Lumentum has booked out an entire FA (final assembly) production line for the technology.
  • This all "squares with TSMC's COUPE production plan."
"Nvidia does not scale high-power laser orders from 40 million to 100 million units, and Lumentum does not book out an entire FA production line, for a technology that is supposedly delivering 'meaningfully below prior expectations.'"

The post is paywalled beyond this point, but the free section alone delivers the core argument: CPO delays are not supported by supply chain evidence, and the 0.95^32 yield math is misleading.

Critical Analysis

Strength of argument: GSR's rebuttal is unusually forceful and data-driven for a Substack post. The supply chain evidence — laser orders jumping 2.5x, booking out entire production lines, record purchase orders — is concrete and verifiable through earnings calls and supplier disclosures. The logical critique of the 0.95^32 yield math is sound: compounding a point-in-time yield without accounting for learning curves, binning, and redundancy is indeed misleading.

Unstated assumptions and weaknesses: GSR assumes laser orders translate directly to CPO deployments. However, laser orders could also be pulled forward for NPO (near-packaged optics) or pluggable deployments, not necessarily CPO. Semi Doped's June 8 update notes the Rubin Ultra NVL576 platform is shifting to NPO, which would still consume optical components without requiring full CPO integration. GSR also doesn't address the specific 3.5 dB insertion loss problem in detail — they acknowledge it but wave it away. If insertion loss eats the optical channel budget, no amount of laser ordering fixes the fundamental physics problem.

Potential bias: GSR has been consistently bullish on optics and CPO, with their readership likely skewing heavily toward photonics investors. Their tone ("let us not hedge, because the moment calls for none") is more advocacy than analysis. A truly balanced piece would acknowledge scenarios where SemiAnalysis could be partially right about CPO timelines without the demand thesis changing.

What would make this thesis wrong: If the insertion loss problem proves structural rather than a yield learning-curve issue, and if NVIDIA shifts meaningfully toward NPO as a permanent architecture rather than a bridge, then laser orders could be robust while CPO-specific deployments still slip. The Semi Doped coverage of NPO being the "goldilocks zone" suggests this is a plausible middle ground that neither GSR nor SemiAnalysis fully addresses.

Confidence: HIGH — The supply chain data is strong and independently verifiable. However, the bull case may be "right for the wrong reasons" if NPO rather than CPO absorbs most of the demand.

DeepSeekV4 1.6T Day 0 to Day 43 Performance Over Time

June 9, 2026  |  Paid post  |  Original

NVDA AMD Huawei (Private)

Summary

This is an exhaustive, 6,300-word technical benchmarking piece from SemiAnalysis's InferenceX initiative, tracking DeepSeek V4 Pro (1.6 trillion parameter MoE model) inference performance from Day 0 through Day 43 across all major hardware platforms. It is the most comprehensive public analysis of multi-platform inference optimization trajectories available. Key findings:

GB300 NVL72 dominates everything. The rack-scale advantage is fundamentally a scale-up domain story: 72 GPUs in a single NVLink domain keeps DeepSeek V4's MoE dispatch/combine all-to-all entirely on NVLink instead of spilling onto slower scale-out fabric, while amortizing expert-weight loads across far more ranks. Cost per million output tokens for GB300 reaches $0.156 at 50 tok/s/user (assuming 8k input, 1k output). Multi-node disaggregated prefill on GB200 NVL72 delivered up to 5x better results than B200 standalone runs. MTP (Multi-Token Prediction) speculative decoding, first supported on Day 3 by SGLang, substantially improved throughput at higher interactivities.

AMD MI355X: 100x improvement in 26 days, but started from dismal Day 0. Day 0 was "confusing" — the MI355X could only run FP8, delivered 1-2 tokens per user per second (far below reading speed), and native FP4+FP8 checkpointing didn't work. However, AMD's SGLang team under HaiShaw's technical leadership achieved a 100x improvement by Day 26 through a systematic rewrite: replacing PyTorch fallback paths with AITER, Triton, TileLang, and FlyDSL kernels; getting FP4 weight MoE working; implementing AITER mHC kernels; retiring FlashMLA fallbacks; and enabling fused hash-topk, radix attention, and other optimizations. By Week 4, MTP was working for AMD. At some data points, MI355X now exceeds H200 performance at lower interactivity levels. However, AMD's ATOM inference engine still has "zero production customers" and distributed inference for DeepSeekV4 Pro still doesn't work on AMD.

NVIDIA TensorRT-LLM had a serious bug. SemiAnalysis discovered that TensorRT-LLM hardcoded FHC_HIDDEN = 4096 for the mHC kernel, which didn't support DeepSeek V4 Pro's 7168 hidden size. An NVIDIA engineer's "fix" simply removed the guard check rather than adding proper support, causing silent data corruption during inference — "the engine ends up corrupting hidden states and producing invalid generations." SemiAnalysis authored the actual fix PR, and noted it took over a week for the problem to be noticed and several more days for the PR to be approved. This "proves the strength of the open native SGLang and vLLM engine ecosystems" where Day 0 support comes first.

B200/B300 performance. B300 SGLang showed 3x improvement in under a week thanks to grouped FP4 MoE GEMM (MegaMoE). B200 tokens per megawatt improved from ~300K tok/s/MW on Day 0 to nearly 500K tok/s/MW by June 5 — a 1.7x pure software gain with fixed power envelope.

Huawei Ascend 950DT analysis. This is the first public analysis of DeepSeek V4 inference on Huawei's Ascend 950DT chip. Huawei achieved Day 0 support — only the second stack (after CUDA) to do so, a significant improvement from last year when only CUDA worked on Day 0. The analysis details Huawei's CANN stack architecture, MC² (merged compute-communication) operators, and the split AI Core (AIC matrix/AIV vector) architecture. Huawei's internal codename for the 950 is "David" (vs. NVIDIA's "Goliath").

DeepSeek V4 architecture innovations. The model introduces Compressed Sparse Attention (CSA) and Heavily Compressed Attention (HCA), achieving 50x KV cache reduction at 1M context length. MegaMoE achieves a theoretical 1.92x speedup over naive MoE kernels by splitting experts into waves for finer-grained compute-communication overlap. DeepSeek also achieved fully deterministic computation for RL training stability through custom batch-invariant kernels.

Open source ecosystem winners: vLLM (Inferact), SGLang (RadixArk) — both have raised hundreds of millions. Supporters include OpenAI, Oracle, Microsoft, Weka, PyTorch Foundation, CoreWeave.

Critical Analysis

Strength of argument: This is genuinely best-in-class technical analysis with reproducible benchmarks, open-source code, and transparent methodology. The InferenceX initiative's credibility is bolstered by its supporter list (OpenAI, Microsoft, CoreWeave). The AMD 100x improvement narrative is well-documented with commit-by-commit tracking. The TensorRT-LLM bug discovery demonstrates real technical depth and access.

Unstated assumptions and investment implications: The piece is implicitly a massive bull case for NVIDIA (GB300 dominance) and a cautious/wait-and-see on AMD. However, SemiAnalysis has a complex relationship with NVIDIA — they receive compute access and collaborate with NVIDIA engineers, while simultaneously finding and reporting bugs. The AMD narrative (100x Day 0 to Day 26 improvement) could be read as either "AMD software is finally catching up" or "AMD software was so broken it needed 100x improvement to become functional." SemiAnalysis's framing clearly leans toward the latter.

What's missing: Cost comparisons. The piece provides GB300 cost-per-token ($0.156/M tokens at 50 tok/s), but doesn't provide equivalent AMD MI355X cost-per-token to enable direct ROI comparison. Given AMD's significantly lower hardware costs, the TCO picture could look different than raw performance suggests. Also, the Huawei analysis is tantalizing but explicitly incomplete — no apples-to-apples comparison is provided yet.

Risk to thesis: If AMD's ROCm stack continues improving at 100x/month rates, the CUDA moat argument weakens considerably. AMD's MI355X claiming "40% more tokens than B200 for the same cost" (from AMD's own benchmarks) contradicts SemiAnalysis's framing. The truth likely lies between the two claims depending on workload and optimization maturity.

Confidence: HIGH on technical accuracy, MEDIUM on investment framing given the NVIDIA-centric perspective.

China's Unitree Will Dominate Global Robotics

June 8, 2026  |  Paid post (188 likes)  |  Original

Unitree (Pre-IPO) TSLA (Optimus comparison) BYD (Analogy)

Summary

SemiAnalysis makes a bold, extensively researched case that China's Unitree Robotics will dominate the global humanoid robotics market, drawing parallels to BYD's conquest of EVs and DJI's dominance in drones. At 5,600+ words with original Bill of Materials (BoM) analysis, this is one of the most comprehensive publicly available analyses of Unitree's business. Key arguments:

The "DJI Strategy" playbook. SemiAnalysis frames Unitree through a "Chinese hardware giant" template: (1) Own the most expensive, technically challenging component (BYD = batteries, DJI = flight controllers, Unitree = actuators at 50-70% of humanoid BoM); (2) Bootstrap through niche researcher/hobbyist market; (3) Ride the Shenzhen ecosystem for cost reduction; (4) Let each hardware generation unlock the next market. Unitree has already executed steps 1-3 and is crossing the threshold into step 4.

Cost dominance is structural and widening. SemiAnalysis developed detailed BoMs "by going through a full design examination of Unitree's robot, speaking with manufacturers of every component, and verifying items with multiple supply chain buyers/sellers." Key findings:

  • G1 pre-tax pricing has fallen from $50K+ to $27.3K over 12-18 months
  • At $27.3K, SemiAnalysis estimates 67% gross margins on the G1
  • BoM cost is approximately $8,976
  • Pricing under $20K in some deals already heard
  • IPO filing confirms quadruped margins improved from 42.36% to 55.49% while costs dropped nearly in half

QDD actuators are defying the skeptics. Unitree chose quasi-direct-drive (QDD) actuators — cheaper and simpler than strainwave/HarmonicDrive alternatives, but initially plagued by overheating. The original G1 could only hold 2kg for a few seconds before mandatory 30-minute cooldown. After years of iteration (motor torque smoothing, copper fill optimization, cooling improvements), the G1 now sustains 5kg for 10-15 minutes with arms bent (2x payload, 5x duration improvement). Key improvements: reduced cogging torque via reshaped magnets, "Low Copper Consumption Coil" (denser wire packing), active cooling around pelvis added October 2025.

Crossing the deployment viability threshold now. SemiAnalysis estimates approximately 250 Unitrees deployed in productive industry settings in 2025, with one company running 30 G1s and multiple companies at 5-6 units. Using Agility Robotics' tote-handoff task as a baseline (66 totes/hour, 2-4kg payloads), the economics work: even with extremely conservative assumptions (full teleoperation, 15% service contract, 2-year life, zero residual value, two shifts), Unitree is currently passing below the $30/hour human labor cost benchmark.

IPO and scale trajectory. Unitree has cleared Shanghai Stock Exchange approval for IPO, targeting ~$6.2B valuation and ~$610M raise. 2025 net profit was 600M yuan ($90M), up 674.3% from 2024. They may have shipped up to 10,000 humanoids. Unitree self-develops BLDC motors, planetary gearboxes, LiDARs, and depth cameras — unusual verticalization even within China. Plans to bring further in-house: "tooth-profile design, simulation optimization, material validation, and high-precision machining."

"We hear Unitree may ship its 10,000th in the coming weeks. Tesla first unveiled a humanoid in 2022, and while it and other Western players are now producing early humanoids that remain works in progress..."

Critical Analysis

Strength of argument: This is an unusually well-sourced analysis with original BoM research, supply chain verification, and deployment economics modeling. The BYD/DJI parallel is intellectually compelling and historically grounded. The cost advantage data ($8,976 BoM at 67% gross margins) is the kind of granular detail that's hard to fake.

Cherry-picked framing: The BYD and DJI analogies are flattering but survivorship-biased — for every Chinese hardware giant that executed this playbook successfully, multiple failed (Faraday Future, dozens of drone startups, etc.). The "DJI Strategy" is a post-hoc narrative, not a predictive framework. SemiAnalysis doesn't seriously engage with the failure cases.

What's underweighted: Geopolitical risk is barely mentioned. US tariffs on Chinese EVs hit 100% — similar restrictions on Chinese humanoid robots in Western markets are not just possible but likely if Unitree succeeds. The EU Chips Act and growing techno-nationalism could create regulatory barriers that BYD is already facing. Unitree's path to Western market dominance faces hurdles BYD never did in its early scaling phase.

Reliability concerns undersold: The piece acknowledges overheating problems but frames the improvement trajectory as inexorable. However, the current "15 minutes of work, 5-10 minutes cooling" duty cycle is still far from industrial-grade reliability. No cycle test data is provided. The jump from "passing below $30/hour" to "dominate global robotics" requires enormous leaps in reliability, autonomy, and regulatory acceptance that aren't quantified.

Investment implications: Unitree is pre-IPO (STAR Market), so direct investment access for Western investors is limited. The implicit bear case is against Tesla Optimus (TSLA), which SemiAnalysis notes "still isn't shipping externally." However, Tesla's advantages (brand, US manufacturing, regulatory acceptance, massive capital base) are not adequately weighed. The piece reads more like a technology analysis than an investment thesis.

Confidence: MEDIUM — The technology and cost analysis is excellent, but the investment thesis is underdeveloped for a company most Western investors can't directly buy, and geopolitical risks are severely underweighted.
