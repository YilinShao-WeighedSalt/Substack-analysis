① Background — the state of the book

Before the new posts, here is the world these newsletters live in. Five ideas do most of the work; learn them once and the rest of the report is readable.

Chip / semiconductor. A tiny slab of silicon with billions of microscopic switches (transistors) etched into it. It does the actual computing inside every phone, car, and data-center server.

Foundry. A factory that manufactures chips designed by other companies. TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company) is the dominant one — most advanced chips in the world are physically built by TSMC even though they carry Nvidia's or Apple's name. A fab is the building itself.

Process node ("18A", "N3", "A14", "1.4nm"). A generation of manufacturing technology. Smaller numbers = newer, denser, faster, more efficient. It is roughly the chip industry's version of model years.

DRAM & HBM (memory). Memory is the chip that temporarily holds the data a processor is working on. DRAM is the ordinary kind. HBM ("High-Bandwidth Memory") is a premium version where memory chips are stacked into towers and glued right next to the processor so data moves faster — it is the single most supply-constrained ingredient in AI hardware. The three companies that make it are SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron.

Advanced packaging (CoWoS) & co-packaged optics (CPO). Packaging is how separate chips are wired together into one module. CoWoS is TSMC's method for bolting HBM towers next to a processor; it is a notorious bottleneck. CPO means putting the laser/optical parts that shuttle data between chips right onto the chip package instead of in separate plug-in cables — faster, but hard to build.

Where the major theses stand (and whether they're working):

  • MEMORY The loudest theme in the wiki all year has been a memory super-cycle: AI needs colossal amounts of DRAM and HBM, supply is tight, and prices are soaring. This week that thesis burst into the mainstream — Apple raised the price of Macs and iPads and openly blamed memory costs, and South Korea pledged ~$880 billion (about 5% of its entire annual economic output) to build two new memory fabs. Global Semi Research and Semi Doped have been early and correct on this tightness; the buyers panicking to lock up supply is exactly what they predicted.
  • HBM DEBATE Irrational Analysis is the lone contrarian: it argues HBM is a clever short-term fix but the wrong long-term architecture, and prefers Samsung (ticker 005930.KS) as its memory play over Micron because Samsung also owns a foundry and optical-chip capability. That structural call is unresolved.
  • OPTICS A long-running feud: SemiAnalysis published a note in June saying co-packaged optics (CPO) is running late; Global Semi Research fired back with order data showing it is on track. Irrational Analysis is emphatically bullish on Lumentum (LITE), the laser maker at the center of it, and this week doubled down.
  • INTEL Intel (INTC) spent two years as the industry's punching bag (failing foundry, weak chips). Sentiment has flipped hard: the stock is up ~256% year-to-date and just hit a new high, and it is now handing test kits for its 14A node to SpaceX and Apple. Multiple newsletters have moved from deep skepticism to cautious optimism.
  • QUALCOMM The one genuine cross-publication fight this week (details below): Irrational Analysis has been a persistent short-seller of Qualcomm; Semi Doped came away from Qualcomm's investor day cautiously impressed. Both cannot be right.

With that scaffolding in place, here is what actually got published in the last three days.

Semi Doped
2026-06-29 · podcast transcript · ~9,300 words
"Qualcomm's HBC Memory, Alphawave, Modular, and more" — Austin Lyons & Vik Sekar break down Qualcomm's Investor Day

Semi Doped is a two-host technical podcast (Austin Lyons of Chipstrat and Vik Sekar). Here they dissect the investor day of Qualcomm (QCOM) — historically the chip company inside most Android phones. Qualcomm's message: phones are a maturing business, and it is betting its future on data-center and automotive chips. Its stated goal is for two-thirds of revenue to come from non-phone businesses by fiscal 2029.

The headline technology — "High Bandwidth Compute" (HBC). Recall that today's AI chips keep their memory (HBM) in towers beside the processor. The hosts use a kitchen analogy: it's like keeping your fridge out in the garage and walking back and forth for every ingredient — slow and power-hungry. Qualcomm's idea is to stack the memory directly on top of the processing logic, so data travels a tiny vertical distance through potentially 100,000 connections instead of ~2,000 out the side. And it uses cheaper, higher-capacity LPDDR memory (the low-power kind in laptops/phones) instead of expensive HBM. If it works, you get far more memory bandwidth without TSMC's bottlenecked CoWoS packaging.

Why the hosts are intrigued but skeptical. They note this is very similar to what a startup called d-Matrix already does ("in-memory compute"), so the innovation may be less novel than billed. And the hard problems don't vanish — they move. Stacking memory on a blazing-hot, dinner-plate-sized processor creates severe thermal (heat) and planarity (keeping the layers perfectly flat so the microscopic connections don't rip apart) challenges. As Vik put it, Qualcomm claims to have "solved packaging," but really "they moved it to a different place… from next to the [processor] to on top of it, which is even harder."

The rest of the strategy:

  • Accelerator roadmap: the AI200 chip samples in 2026 (no HBC yet); the AI250 in 2027 is the first with HBC; the AI300 in 2028.
  • "Disaggregated inference" is Qualcomm's opening. AI answering (called inference) is increasingly split into stages, so you can slot in a specialized rack for just one stage rather than buying all-Nvidia everything. That lets a latecomer get "a foot in the door."
  • Alphawave (recently acquired) is described as "Qualcomm's Mellanox" — the networking/interconnect piece (the plumbing that moves data between chips) that turns a chip into a full rack-scale system. It also brought two unnamed hyperscaler custom-chip customers.
  • Modular (also acquired) is the software layer — its "Mojo" language is pitched as an open alternative to Nvidia's CUDA (the software moat that locks developers into Nvidia). Modular's founder Chris Lattner is a genuine legend (created Swift, LLVM, Clang).
  • C1000 is a new data-center CPU (general-purpose processor) with Meta (Facebook's parent) named as a customer.
  • Edge AI & robotics — running AI locally in cars, phones, and eventually robots — is framed as Qualcomm's real long-term prize (a claimed "$1 trillion opportunity… in 2040").
"We're late, we've got a new approach, we think it can ultimately be competitive… but they do eventually need to follow up with technical details, especially if stuff's not shipping for a few years." — the hosts' summary of Qualcomm's pitch

What this means (context from memory)

This matters because it is a bull-ish (or at least fair) hearing for Qualcomm from technical people — and that collides directly with the wiki's dominant view. Irrational Analysis has been short Qualcomm for nearly two years (the thesis: Apple is building its own modem chip and will strip Qualcomm of ~$1.2 billion in revenue, and Qualcomm's data-center ambitions are vapor). Semi Doped's hosts land somewhere gentler: the data-center entry is genuinely late and unproven, but the underlying edge-AI/memory technology is real and Qualcomm has the engineering pedigree, IP, and cash to matter eventually. The HBC concept also quietly reinforces the memory super-cycle — the hosts' punchline is "MOAR memory": even Qualcomm's HBM-lite approach needs more DRAM wafers, not fewer, so "don't let anyone DeepSeek you and tell you HBM's dead."

Track record (for what it's worth)

Semi Doped is a young voice in the wiki (first tracked April 2026) with calls still open. Irrational Analysis's Qualcomm short, by contrast, is one of the few graded calls in the wiki — and it has been wrong: entries at $163 (2024) and $177 (April 2026) are both underwater now that QCOM sits at ~$192 after popping ~23% on this very investor day.

The skeptical read

The hosts are appropriately skeptical of Qualcomm: HBC is a slide, not shipping silicon, and stacking hot logic under memory is an unsolved manufacturing problem they repeatedly flag. The Alphawave CEO's claim that Qualcomm's 2028 CPU "is still going to be the best" is called out by the hosts themselves as un-credible cheerleading. Net: this is a "credible long-term optionality, unproven near-term" read — which is why our ticker call below is NEUTRAL/cautious, not a buy. The genuine tension with Irrational Analysis is logged as an open contradiction. Confidence: MEDIUM.

SemiAnalysis
2026-06-30 · research note · ~2,100 words · Crystal Huang, Joey Brookhart, Dylan Patel
"TokenBudgeting: Our Conversations with Enterprises on Token Spend — Was Widespread TokenMaxxing Ever Really Here?"

SemiAnalysis is the most influential research shop in the sector, known for granular supply-chain data. This note tackles a demand question rather than a chip: are companies pulling back on AI spending? First, two terms. A token is the unit AI models bill by — roughly a fraction of a word; every AI query consumes tokens, and companies pay per token. "Tokenmaxxing" was the early-2026 fad where firms told employees to burn as many tokens as possible for productivity; "token budgeting" is the newer trend of capping that spend.

The team interviewed 50+ enterprises (by Slack, phone, and at the Databricks AI Summit). Their conclusion: the "budget blowout" headlines are overblown, and AI spending keeps growing. Key findings:

  • The viral stories (Meta employees burning 60 trillion tokens/month with a "Claudeconomics" leaderboard; Uber blowing its annual budget in four months and imposing a $1,500/employee/month cap) came from poor incentives and lax oversight, not from AI failing to deliver value. The team didn't find that pattern elsewhere.
  • Even Meta, spending ~$50,000/year per employee on AI, is only a "3–5% customer" for Anthropic (the maker of the Claude AI models). The revenue is concentrated: top-1% enterprise customers spend ~$90,000/employee/year while the median tech-forward company spends ~$136 — meaning there is enormous room to grow as normal companies catch up.
  • Budgets are now normal but wildly inconsistent — from $250/month (an aerospace & defense firm) to $2,000/month (Workday, Stripe) — and most employees never hit their cap.
  • Coding is the killer app: SemiAnalysis estimates 70%+ of Anthropic's and OpenAI's revenue comes from coding use cases today, with cybersecurity and general office work expected to repeat that pattern.
  • Cost-saving tricks abound: companies downgrade the default model (one travel-tech firm with 800 engineers switched everyone from the pricey "Opus" model to the cheaper "Sonnet"; an aerospace firm turned off "Opus 4.8" and Fast-Mode), and employees game their free Microsoft Copilot allowance to avoid burning metered tokens.
"There is not a material risk present to 2H26 AI budgets and we expect Anthropic and OpenAI's API business to continue to grow at their current net new rates."

What this means (context from memory)

Why does a note about software budgets belong in a chip report? Because AI token demand is the ultimate source of demand for every chip in this report. The bear case that haunts the whole sector — tracked in the wiki's "AI models & economics" theme since the January-2025 DeepSeek shock — is that AI spending is a bubble that will pop, taking GPU, memory, and optics demand down with it. SemiAnalysis is planting a flag against that fear with primary data: enterprise usage is early on its growth curve, not late. If they are right, the memory super-cycle and the AI-infrastructure build-out have more room to run. This is bullish, indirectly, for essentially every long in this report — and directly supportive of Amazon (AMZN), whose AWS cloud rents out this compute (SemiAnalysis says AWS's AI-hosting business is running "well above street" estimates).

Track record (for what it's worth)

SemiAnalysis's structural calls (TSMC packaging bottleneck, HBM absorbing DRAM capacity, Nvidia's dominance) have tracked well over 2024–2026 per the wiki. Its demand-side reads are harder to grade but have generally leaned correct-and-early.

The skeptical read

The evidence is qualitative and self-selected — 50 conversations, many at an AI conference (Databricks) where attendees are AI enthusiasts, plus data from Ramp (a fintech whose customers skew tech-forward, which the authors admit). "Headlines are overblown" is also a conveniently bullish conclusion for a firm that sells an AI-forecasting model (the "Tokenomics Model" it advertises throughout). The falsifier to watch: if a hyperscaler's next earnings call shows AI-cloud revenue decelerating, this thesis breaks. For now it is a credible, well-sourced counter-narrative, not proof. Confidence: MEDIUM.

Global Semi Research
2026-06-29 · deep-dive (partly paywalled) · ~950 words visible
"Tungsten Hexafluoride: The Semiconductor Gas Nobody Can Get Enough Of"

Global Semi Research does supply-chain deep dives, often with a contrarian, Asia-focused angle. This one is about an obscure but critical industrial gas. Some terms: tungsten hexafluoride (WF6) is a toxic gas used to deposit ultra-thin layers of the metal tungsten inside chips — the tiny "wires" (called word-lines and contacts) that carry electricity between transistor layers. It is used in essentially every advanced memory and logic chip. There is no substitute at the required scale.

The dislocation: WF6 prices have risen five-fold since January 2026, and supply is short by roughly a quarter of global demand. Three things collided:

  • China's export controls. China controls 80%+ of the world's raw tungsten. Aiming mainly at Japan's military industry (tungsten is used in armor-piercing munitions), Beijing throttled tungsten exports to Japan — and Japanese gas makers, who have almost no domestic tungsten, lost their raw material overnight. Japan's tungsten imports from China fell 50% in April 2026.
  • A factory fire. Japan's Kanto Denka Kogyo had a 2025 fire damaging a related production line.
  • The result: Japan's two big WF6 makers (Kanto Denka, ~1,200 tons/year; Central Glass, ~1,000 tons/year) told customers — Samsung, SK Hynix, TSMC — that WF6 production would permanently stop from July 1, 2026. That erases ~2,200 tons, nearly a quarter of global supply, and it cannot be quickly rebuilt outside China.

The killer economic point: WF6 is less than 1% of a memory chip's manufacturing cost. A factory earning 70%+ profit margins on HBM will happily pay almost any price rather than scrap an entire wafer for lack of one gas. The article's blunt analogy: "specialty gas is to a fab what oxygen is to a human." That combination — utterly essential, negligible cost — gives WF6 pricing "almost no ceiling" while supply is short. New (mostly Chinese) capacity is expected to close the gap by mid-2027. The specific stock thesis was behind a paywall, but the clear beneficiaries are the Chinese producers consolidating the market; the losers are the exiting Japanese suppliers.

What this means (context from memory)

This is a fresh instance of a pattern the wiki has tracked all year under "geopolitics & export controls": China weaponizing its dominance of upstream materials. The West spent years restricting China's access to chip tools (like ASML's lithography machines); China's counter is to squeeze the raw inputs — rare earths, and now tungsten and fluorine chemistry — that everyone else depends on. The article notes related gases (hydrofluoric acid, specialty fluorocarbons) are also up 2–3×. It's the same story as the krypton/xenon spike during the Russia-Ukraine war: a tiny, ignored input suddenly gates the whole industry. It reinforces why memory chips keep getting more expensive (feeding directly into this week's Apple price-hike news).

Track record (for what it's worth)

Global Semi Research's signature style is exactly this — sizing a niche supply shock from upstream capacity data — and its memory-tightness and InP-substrate scarcity calls have been directionally early per the wiki. No priced ticker call is attached here (the names are behind the paywall), so nothing to grade.

The skeptical read

The five-fold price move is real, but the investable conclusion is weak on the visible side: the named companies are the losers (exiting Japanese firms), and the winners are largely unlisted or hard-to-access Chinese producers — plus new capacity is explicitly expected to relieve the shortage by mid-2027, making this a time-limited monopoly, not a durable one. A ~1% cost input, even up 5×, is also not by itself a needle-mover for the big chipmakers' margins. Useful as macro color on why memory inflation is broad-based; thin as a standalone trade. Confidence: MEDIUM (as a supply-chain observation), LOW (as an actionable equity idea from what's visible).
