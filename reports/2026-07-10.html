Background — the state of the book (read this first)

The semiconductor industry is the business of making the tiny electronic "brains" — chips — inside every phone, car, and computer. Right now the whole sector is being reshaped by one force: the buildout of artificial intelligence. To understand this week's posts you need five running storylines that this wiki has tracked for months. Here they are in plain language.

1. Training vs. inference — where the money actually goes now

Building an AI model happens in two phases. Training is teaching the model — a huge, one-time, painfully expensive computing project. Inference is running the finished model to answer each user's question. For years the market obsessed over training ("who has the biggest computer?"). The quiet shift of 2026 is that inference — the perpetual electricity-and-hardware bill that grows with every user and every chat — is now the dominant cost. This one fact drives the biggest story in today's scan.

2. Nvidia's GPU vs. "custom silicon"

A GPU (graphics processing unit) is a general-purpose parallel-computing chip; Nvidia makes the ones that run almost all AI and is, as of mid-2026, the most valuable company on earth. The alternative is a custom ASIC (application-specific integrated circuit) — a chip hard-wired for exactly one job, which makes it cheaper and more power-efficient than a flexible GPU for that one job. Big tech companies increasingly design their own ASICs, usually with Broadcom (ticker AVGO) or Marvell as the engineering partner. SemiAnalysis and Global Semi Research have tracked this "merchant GPU vs. custom chip" contest as the central question in the sector since 2024. Today it takes a dramatic new turn: the AI model companies themselves are now designing chips.

3. The memory supercycle

Chips need memory to hold data. Two kinds matter: DRAM (fast working memory, like a desk you spread papers on) and NAND flash (slower long-term storage, like a filing cabinet). A special stacked form of DRAM called HBM (high-bandwidth memory) sits right next to an AI chip and feeds it data at enormous speed. AI has created a memory shortage so severe that the three big makers — America's Micron (MU), and South Korea's SK Hynix and Samsung — are sold out through 2027 at record profits. This wiki's sources are almost uniformly bullish memory near-term, with a nagging worry about oversupply arriving in late 2027.

4. The AI data-center capex & debt boom — and the one open argument

Running all this requires data centers: warehouses of chips that draw as much power as a small city (measured in gigawatts, GW — one GW is roughly a million homes' worth). Spending on them is now so large it is being funded with record borrowing. The single live disagreement in this wiki is "buildout vs. monetization": SemiAnalysis argues the spending is rational and demand is real and early; Global Semi Research has turned more cautious, warning the market may stop rewarding raw buildout and start asking whether it earns money. Both, notably, are bullish on Meta itself — they disagree only on the read-through to Meta's suppliers.

5. Materials & geopolitics — the squeeze from below

Making a chip needs dozens of ultra-pure gases and metals that investors never hear about, most refined in China. The West restricts China's access to chip-making tools; China retaliates by squeezing the raw inputs (rare earths, tungsten, fluorine). Last month this wiki logged a 5× spike in tungsten hexafluoride, a gas no memory fab can run without, after Chinese export controls. Today's molybdenum post is the sequel to that story.

This week's macro weather: the tape was risk-off. Per the 2026-07-08 market recap, renewed US strikes on Iran sent oil up ~6%, pulling forward interest-rate-hike fears; Amazon's giant AI bond sale drew soft demand, and Korean chip stocks sold off before rebounding. A jumpy backdrop for a sector that lives on cheap capital.

Post 1 · 2026-07-09 · "Why the Model Makers Are Coming for Their Own Chips"

Tickers: NVDA (neutral) · AVGO (long, implied)

Full summary

On July 7, Reuters reported that DeepSeek — a Chinese AI lab, backed by the quant hedge fund High-Flyer, famous for building strong models cheaply — is developing its own AI inference chip to cut its reliance on both Nvidia and China's national champion Huawei. It was the third such signal in months: on June 24, OpenAI (maker of ChatGPT) and Broadcom unveiled "Jalapeno," which OpenAI calls its first "Intelligence Processor," a chip purpose-built to run large language models, due to deploy in late 2026; and in April, Reuters reported Anthropic (maker of Claude) was exploring its own chip designs.

Three frontier AI labs, three announcements, one direction. The author's core claim: the reflexive market read — "the model companies are coming to dethrone Nvidia, so short Nvidia" — is wrong, or at least premature. The real story is that the cost structure of the AI business has flipped, and the companies that understand their own workloads best are starting to bake that knowledge directly into hardware.

"Training is a capex event. Inference is the electricity bill."

The logic: training is a bounded, one-time investment; inference is a perpetual operating cost that scales with revenue. Every chat message, code completion, and agent step forces the model to re-read its context and generate tokens, each consuming compute, memory, interconnect, and power. Cruelly, success makes it worse — the more capable a model is, the longer the documents and codebases users trust it with, and the more each request costs to serve. For OpenAI, Anthropic, and DeepSeek, the chip bill now sits directly under gross margin, latency, and pricing power. That is the kind of cost a serious company eventually refuses to leave entirely in a supplier's hands.

Why inference chips specifically, and why now? The author uses DeepSeek's own trajectory. In December 2024, DeepSeek's V3 model trained on 2,048 Nvidia H800 GPUs (the export-compliant chip Nvidia was allowed to sell into China). By April 2026, DeepSeek's newer V4 had been adapted to run on Huawei's Ascend chips. The key technical point is about the KV cache — think of it as the model's short-term memory of everything said so far in a conversation. DeepSeek's latest V4-Pro model carries 1.6 trillion "parameters" (its learned settings) and supports a one-million-token context window (roughly a 1,500-page document held in mind at once). At that scale the bottleneck is no longer raw arithmetic — it is moving enormous amounts of data in and out of memory. V4-Pro's technical report claims it needs only ~27% of the per-token computing and just 10% of the memory cache of the prior version. Once a lab knows exactly which operations dominate and which context lengths its users actually send, a flexible, general-purpose GPU starts to look like paying for versatility you no longer need. A hard-wired chip can drop the rest.

The same logic underlies OpenAI's Jalapeno, whose design emphasizes memory movement and serving patterns over headline compute. The strategic inversion: for a decade, model builders adapted their software to whatever the GPU offered; now the frontier labs are defining what the chip should optimize. The piece stops (paywall) before its "three companies, three levels of commitment" ranking, but frames DeepSeek, OpenAI, and Anthropic as very different degrees of real hardware investment, not one uniform threat.

Analysis — what it means & whether to believe it

Context from the wiki (teach-first). This is the "custom silicon" theme reaching its logical endpoint. For two years the story was hyperscalers (Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta) building ASICs with Broadcom/Marvell to reduce Nvidia dependence — Google's TPU, Amazon's Trainium, Meta's MTIA. The new wrinkle is that the pure-play model labs — who don't own cloud businesses — are now doing it too, because inference has become their single largest variable cost. Note how this dovetails with SemiAnalysis's parallel framing that inference is "the electricity bill": both publications, from different angles, are telling you the center of gravity in AI economics has moved from training to serving. That is the meta-story of this entire scan.

Why Nvidia is not simply "shorted" here. A custom inference chip does not replace the GPU for training, for research, or for any workload whose shape keeps changing — and AI workloads change constantly (the author notes a good coding task today involves orchestrating sub-agents, which "didn't exist 7 months ago"). Custom silicon wins where the workload is stable and huge; the GPU keeps the volatile frontier. That is exactly why the wiki's SemiAnalysis dossier still models Nvidia holding ~75% of the data-center chip market even as ASICs scale.

Track record (secondary). Global Semi Research is the wiki's most committed custom-silicon voice (deep dives on Broadcom's >60% ASIC share and Marvell as "the next chip giant"). Its calls here are recent and unresolved, but its earlier, non-consensus INTC "CPU shortage" call was directionally right within months, which lends some credibility to its habit of spotting demand-side shifts early. It is not a reflexive Nvidia bear — this piece actively defends Nvidia against the lazy short.

Critique & falsifier. The soft spot is timing and magnitude. "Model makers designing chips" and "model makers successfully deploying chips at volume that dents Nvidia orders" are years apart — designing silicon is brutally hard, and none of these labs has disclosed a working chip at scale. DeepSeek and Zhipu face the added handicap of US export controls limiting their access to leading-edge manufacturing. The thesis would be falsified if, say, Jalapeno slips badly or underperforms, showing the labs can't actually out-design a merchant GPU vendor for their own workloads. Confidence: MEDIUM — the directional read (inference cost is the new battleground; Nvidia is defended, ASIC enablers benefit) is well-argued and cross-confirmed; the investable timing is fuzzy and paywalled.

Post 2 · 2026-07-07 · "Molybdenum vs. Tungsten: The Metal Swap Rewiring the Memory Supply Chain"

Tickers: 285A.T / MU / 005930.KS / 000660.KS (NAND makers — thematic)

Full summary

This is the sequel to last month's tungsten-gas shock. The subject is a quiet materials change inside 3D NAND — the flash-memory chips in every SSD and phone. To pack more storage into the same silicon area, NAND makers stack memory cells vertically, and the industry has now pushed past 300 layers. Running through every layer are horizontal wires called word lines that electrically address each memory cell.

For twenty years those wires were made of tungsten, a metal. At 300-plus layers, tungsten hits a wall for two compounding reasons. First, as the wires get thinner each generation, tungsten's electrical resistance climbs sharply, and it needs a "barrier layer" (titanium nitride) to stick to its surroundings — a liner that eats the shrinking space left for the actual conductor. Second, tungsten is deposited using a gas (WF6) that releases fluorine, which corrodes the surrounding insulation and creates leakage paths. Manageable at 100 layers; a hard cap at 300-plus.

Molybdenum, another metal, fixes both at once: in ultra-thin dimensions it conducts far better (resistance drops >50% at 300-plus layers), it needs no barrier layer (so the whole cross-section conducts and each layer can be thinner, reducing stack height and wafer warping), and its leading feedstock chemical (molybdenum dichloride dioxide) contains no fluorine at all, eliminating the corrosion problem at the source. Engineering data from Kioxia (the Japanese NAND maker, ticker 285A.T) at a 2024 industry symposium showed molybdenum word lines cutting leakage failures to ~1/25th of tungsten's, while tolerating tighter shrink. It also programs and reads faster — increasingly valuable as AI prices NAND on performance.

"Molybdenum is what lets NAND makers keep stacking, and stacking is the whole business model."

All three major NAND makers have now put molybdenum on their production roadmaps, with the first mass-production lines converting this year. The author's investable angle (paywalled before the details) sits upstream: not the memory makers themselves, but the suppliers of the molybdenum precursors (the specialty feedstock chemicals), the delivery hardware that pipes those chemicals into the deposition tools, and — crucially — the "qualification moat" around them (once a fab certifies a specific supplier's chemistry, switching is slow and risky, which protects incumbents' pricing).

Analysis — what it means & whether to believe it

Context from the wiki (teach-first). This is the third entry in the wiki's young "specialty gases & materials supply shocks" theme, and it rhymes with the WF6 story: a cheap-per-wafer, non-substitutable input whose economics are outsized because a >70%-margin memory fab will pay almost anything rather than idle a line. But note the important difference from the tungsten shock. WF6 was a supply squeeze (China cut off feedstock, prices spiked 5×) — a time-limited monopoly that new capacity relieves by mid-2027. Molybdenum is a deliberate technology transition — a durable, multi-year design win driven by physics, not politics. The moat here is qualification lock-in, which is stickier than a temporary shortage.

Track record (secondary). Global Semi's signature move is "size a niche shock from upstream capacity data," and its WF6 call from two weeks ago is the direct predecessor. Those calls are too new to grade, but the analytical method — following the money to the un-glamorous chemical/equipment layer rather than the famous chip names — is consistent and differentiated.

Critique & falsifier. The frustrating part for a reader is that the actual beneficiaries are paywalled and, in practice, tend to be hard-to-trade: molybdenum precursors are dominated by a handful of specialty-chemical firms (often divisions of larger European/US/Japanese conglomerates or private players), so there may be no clean listed pure-play. The NAND makers named here — Kioxia, Micron, Samsung, SK Hynix — are beneficiaries only indirectly (molybdenum lets them keep scaling), so this is not a fresh equity call on them; it is a supply-chain map. The thesis would weaken if molybdenum adoption stalls on yield problems at volume, or if the precursor supply base turns out to be more competitive (less moaty) than claimed. Confidence: MEDIUM on the technology transition (well-sourced, industry-wide, physics-driven); LOW on there being an easily investable public name for a non-expert to act on.

Post 1 · 2026-07-09 · "The Future of Meta Superintelligence: A 1-Year Progress Update"

Tickers: META (long) · GOOGL (bearish) · NVDA (compute substrate)

Full summary

A year ago, a disastrous launch of Meta's "Llama 4" open-source model pushed CEO Mark Zuckerberg to tear down and rebuild Meta's entire AI organization into MSL (Meta Superintelligence Labs). The rebuild was extravagant: a $14.3B "investment" in the data-labeling startup Scale AI, structured largely to poach its founder Alexandr Wang and top staff; pay packages of hundreds of millions (sometimes $1B+) for individual researchers; and a new fast-to-build "Tent" data-center design. SemiAnalysis's verdict one year on: MSL's first public model, "Muse Spark," was underwhelming (it lagged open-source models from China's DeepSeek and Kimi), but that misses the point. What matters is the slope, not the intercept — the trajectory, not today's snapshot. The interesting question is where MSL will be in six months.

The argument rests on the three ingredients of a frontier model — data, talent, and compute — and the claim that Meta is the only company on track to be world-class at all three, giving it the best shot at catching the two current leaders, OpenAI and Anthropic. (Google, the piece argues, has faded: its models trail on real-world coding despite a brief moment of buzz.)

  • Data. The overlooked advantage. Today's most important training method is reinforcement learning (RL) — instead of just predicting the next word, you teach the model to complete whole tasks (e.g. fix a bug) inside an "environment," with "verifiers" that grade the result. A whole supply chain of "human data" startups (Mercor, Surge, Handshake) has sprung up at $1B+ in annual revenue to produce these RL tasks. The prize everyone wants is real screen recordings of white-collar work, because tasks built from them are realistic by definition. Meta's twist: it has started tracking its own employees' screens, keyboards, and mouse movements — "quite literally some of the most valuable data in the world" — and reassigned ~3,000 engineers to make RL tasks full-time. SemiAnalysis calls this Meta effectively spawning a top-tier RL-environment company in-house, an underappreciated edge.
  • Compute. Meta has a hyperscaler's balance sheet, no cloud-rental business pulling chips away, and a CEO willing to run cash-flow-negative. SemiAnalysis's new "Tokenomics Model" projects Meta will have more AI computing power than either OpenAI or Anthropic by end of 2026. It is building five 1-gigawatt-plus "titan" clusters simultaneously (named Prometheus in Ohio, Hyperion in Louisiana, plus unnamed sites in El Paso, Iowa, Indiana) — including the world's largest single buildings at 400MW each. "Never in history" have two full-GW campuses been under construction at once; Meta has that now.
  • Networking ("scale-across"). Because you can't cool or power a single giant building indefinitely, Meta spreads a cluster across many data centers and links them so they act as one. Its "AI-Backbone" design pushes ~22 petabits/second of bandwidth across the Prometheus constellation (27 data centers over 6 campuses). The catch is latency: sites 100km apart can't communicate faster than ~500 microseconds (limited by the speed of light in fiber), forcing "asynchronous" training tricks. Future titans will connect campuses up to 2,000km apart.
  • Talent. A recruiting blitz: ex-OpenAI, ex-Anthropic, ex-Google researchers, Thinking Machines co-founders, plus a finance/infrastructure leadership team poached from OpenAI's compute org.

SemiAnalysis is "overall bullish" but stresses Meta is "basically at step 1." It flags concrete falsifiers: if Meta signs a long-term deal to sell its compute with no clawback, disbands the RL-task org, or lets top researchers walk, "we would be materially less bullish" — any one could be "a death sentence for MSL." The just-released Muse Spark 1.1 is judged roughly on par with a mid-tier Anthropic/Chinese model — improving, but SemiAnalysis won't move its own usage onto it yet.

Analysis — what it means & whether to believe it

Context from the wiki (teach-first). SemiAnalysis has run this MSL thesis since July 2025, and the wiki's META dossier has been LONG throughout on three pillars: compute scale, proprietary data, cashflow self-funding. This update deepens the "data" pillar (the screen-tracking / RL-environment insight is genuinely new) and hard-quantifies the "compute" pillar (five titans; more compute than OpenAI/Anthropic by year-end). It also connects to a second wiki thread: SemiAnalysis's June-30 "TokenBudgeting" note argued enterprise AI demand is early, not peaking — the demand-side foundation under this supply-side buildout.

Track record (secondary). This is SemiAnalysis's strongest franchise: it tracks data-center construction via satellite imagery months before it's public, and the wiki notes its META LONGs from July 2–3 at $584.88 are already +2.8% (last mark $601, July 6). Its custom-silicon and CoWoS-bottleneck calls have a strong prior hit history. When SemiAnalysis makes a granular, building-level infrastructure claim, it has earned the benefit of the doubt on the facts.

Critique & falsifier. Two honest weaknesses. (1) The thesis is about slope, which is unfalsifiable in the short run — "wait six months" can always be repeated, and the actual product (Muse Spark) is still admittedly behind. (2) Compute ≠ frontier models. Having the most GPUs and the best data pipeline has not, historically, guaranteed the best model — organizational focus and research luck matter, and SemiAnalysis itself concedes Meta "is still a big tech company with lots of competing opinions." The screen-tracking edge also carries real regulatory/PR/employee-morale risk the piece waves off as "minor." Falsifier: watch Meta's Q3/Q4 actions against SemiAnalysis's own list — any compute-selling deal, org disbandment, or researcher exodus flips the thesis. Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH on the infrastructure facts (SA's core competency); MEDIUM on the leap from "world-class inputs" to "catches Anthropic/OpenAI."

China, Nvidia, and the H200

The panel split: Vik thinks it may be "too little, too late" since China is already building domestic compute (and worries the Chinese model GLM 5.2 is capable enough to break cybersecurity defenses); Austin thinks momentum shifts back to Nvidia because it's easier to buy the best than to bootstrap. Korea's memory giants go to Wall Street

SK Hynix (the world's HBM memory leader, ticker 000660.KS in Seoul) launched a $28B American Depositary Receipt (ADR) offering — a mechanism that lets US investors buy a foreign stock directly on US markets — and it was oversubscribed (more demand than shares). Marquee funds Coatue and "Situational Awareness Partners" indicated interest in up to $7B combined. The motive: Seoul's market allegedly under-values SK Hynix, so a US listing seeks a valuation premium. The panel's caution: recent oversubscribed IPOs (Cerebras, SpaceX) later disappointed — "never underestimate the power of get-rich-quick schemes."

The "off switch" scare — Bloom Energy

Bloom Energy (BE) makes fuel cells that generate electricity on-site ("behind the meter"), bypassing the slow, congested power grid — a pitch aimed squarely at power-starved AI data centers. Activist short-seller Hunterbrook published an investigation alleging that a critical input for Bloom's cells, the rare metal scandium, is sourced from China and requires Chinese-government approval per shipment — meaning "China has an OFF switch to US datacenters" — while Bloom's CEO has repeatedly denied any China supply chain. The panel's balanced caveat: Hunterbrook is short BE (it profits if the stock falls), Bloom says the cells "supplement grid power" rather than run data centers off-grid, and most of those data centers aren't built yet. "Take it all with a grain of salt."

Power-chip lawsuit; other notable moves

  • Wolfspeed (WOLF) sued Navitas (NVTS) for patent infringement across Navitas's entire silicon-carbide (SiC) and gallium-nitride (GaN) portfolio — these are "wide-bandgap" power semiconductors that convert electricity far more efficiently than ordinary silicon, increasingly vital as AI data centers move to high-voltage power delivery. Wolfspeed, a leading SiC maker, seeks damages and a sales injunction. Vik: going after the "entire Navitas portfolio… not a good look" for Navitas.
  • TeraWulf (WULF), a former Bitcoin miner turned AI-data-center landlord ("neocloud"), signed a 20-year, ~$19B lease with Anthropic for a 401MW Kentucky campus (live H2 2027) — a huge demand commitment.
  • Amazon (AMZN) raised $25B in bonds — one of the largest US corporate debt sales ever — to fund AI infrastructure (demand was reportedly soft, per the macro recap).
  • Apple (AAPL) is testing DRAM from China's blacklisted memory maker CXMT for China-market devices — a sign Chinese memory is gaining ground on the Samsung/SK Hynix/Micron trio, at least in consumer (not cutting-edge AI) applications.
  • Analog Devices (ADI) closed its acquisition of Empower Semiconductor, extending into "vertical power delivery" — feeding power straight up into the chip, a rising need as Nvidia's chips pull ~11× the current of three generations ago.
  • TSMC (the world's dominant contract chip manufacturer) is building a resilient "second fleet" of localized suppliers to protect its CoWoS advanced-packaging supply chain — the technique that stitches a GPU to its HBM memory and remains the key bottleneck on AI chip output.
  • Samsung Electro-Mechanics (009150.KS) is forming a glass-core-substrate joint venture (a next-gen chip-packaging base material) with a Sumitomo unit, targeting 2H27 production.
  • BE Semiconductor / Besi (BESI) — the equipment leader in "hybrid bonding" (a way to stack chips with direct copper-to-copper bonds) — faces intensifying competition crowding into its once-uncontested market.

Nearly every item above is a facet of two wiki themes already in motion: custom silicon (DeepSeek, Zhipu, Apple–Broadcom, TeraWulf-for-Anthropic) and the AI capex/debt boom (Amazon's $25B bonds, SK Hynix's $28B ADR, TeraWulf's $19B lease). Track record & stance (secondary). Semi Doped is a technically fluent digest, not a high-conviction call machine; most items here are MENTION-grade context rather than fresh theses. Critique. A digest inevitably amplifies whatever is loud; the Bloom "off switch" item is the cautionary example — it's a short-seller's framing, and the panel rightly flags the conflict, the "supplements grid power" rebuttal, and that the data centers aren't even built. Bottom line

One theme dominated all three publications this run: the AI model makers are moving down into their own hardware. DeepSeek, OpenAI (Jalapeno/Broadcom), Anthropic (exploring, Broadcom), Zhipu, and Apple (Broadcom RF) all appeared in the same 72 hours — because inference, the cost of running models, has overtaken training as the dominant AI expense, and whoever controls that cost controls their own margins. The correct read, per Global Semi, is not "short Nvidia" — GPUs keep the shifting frontier — but "custom-silicon enablers (Broadcom) win, and Nvidia's moat is now partly financial."

Two supporting stories: (1) Meta is making a credible, fully-resourced run at the AI frontier (SemiAnalysis LONG, already +2.8%), and Anthropic is emerging as the profitable AI leader heading toward an IPO — validating that inference can be a real business (AMZN read-through). (2) The memory supercycle is hard-confirmed near-term (Micron's record margins), and molybdenum-for-tungsten is a fresh, physics-driven reason NAND can keep scaling — a durable transition, unlike last month's temporary tungsten-gas squeeze.

The one open argument — Global Semi's "buildout may stop being rewarded" caution vs. SemiAnalysis's "demand is early, not late" — stays unresolved, but this run's flood of real demand commitments (TeraWulf's $19B Anthropic lease, SK Hynix's oversubscribed $28B raise, Meta's five titans) tilts the near-term evidence toward the buildout bulls. Watch GPU rental rates and Meta's Q3/Q4 contracting for the first sign it turns.
