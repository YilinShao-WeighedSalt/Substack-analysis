HBM: High-Bandwidth Mistake
2026-05-31 4,237 words
LONG Samsung CAUTIOUS MU / SK Hynix LONG DRAM (18-month horizon)

Full Summary

The author — a semiconductor engineer turned investor with experience since 2011 — opens with a partial capitulation on his longstanding negative DRAM view. He stakes out three spicy takes: (1) HBM is an engineering mistake that will be phased out within 7–10 years (90% volume decline from peak); (2) DRAM stocks probably have another double or triple remaining; and (3) DRAM stocks will still eventually drawdown 70%+ from cycle peak. The post is divided into four sections: Engineering, Entertaining History, Agentic Reality, and a Samsung pitch.

Engineering (Section 1): The author argues that HBM is fundamentally a PHY (physical interface) problem that was solved incorrectly. All DRAM variants (DDR, LPDDR, GDDR, HBM) share four core attributes: latency, bandwidth, capacity, and power consumption. HBM's vertical stacking via TSVs (through-silicon vias) delivers high bandwidth but at accelerating cost — the HBM4 11 Gbps/pin demand by Nvidia immediately exposed industry-wide failures. JEDEC's 8 Gbps/pin spec was obsolete on release. Samsung used its SRF4X logic node (roughly N6/N7P equivalent), giving it most margin to fix issues. SK Hynix chose TSMC N12 and endured 6 re-spins. Micron catastrophically stayed on its internal DRAM process for the base die — the author calls this "going full retard."

The root cause of the HBM4 fiasco is bump capacitance: each layer of a HBM stack creates a parasitic capacitive speed bump, visualized via TDR (time-domain reflectometer) traces. The only proper fix is hybrid bonding, which eliminates micro-bumps. But this frames hybrid bonding as correcting a design flaw, not an optional improvement. The author dismisses SK Hynix's thermal pillar workaround as a kludge ("like eating while wrapping your arm behind your head").

The optimal long-term solution, per the author, is optically-disaggregated LPDDR memory pools. At ISSCC 2026, Nvidia showed clock-forwarded SerDes directly driving co-packaged optics — <3 pJ/bit, up to 30-meter reach, no FEC latency penalty. This enables memory to be relocated off the ASIC die entirely, connected via optical fiber and an OCS switch. This reverses the HBM trade ratio (currently ~3:1 DRAM destroyed per GB of HBM produced): if LPDDR replaces HBM, DRAM supply effectively triples. The author argues this is a win-win: DRAM makers get 3x revenue at slightly lower margins, netting 2.25x profits, while customers get 3x supply at palatable prices.

He highlights one startup, Positron, as solving the memory bandwidth problem "the correct way" — using only commodity LPDDR5X, no HBM, with the highest bandwidth of any AI ASIC. He's under NDA and can only confirm this. He dismisses Etched and MatX more tepidly.

Entertaining History (Section 2): A roast of Micron CEO Sanjay Mehrotra across every earnings call from June 2017 to June 2025. The author documents the classic DRAM cycle: 2017 upcycle, 2018 top ($60/share), collapse to $32 in Dec 2018, recovery, COVID pull-forward creating 2021 peaks ($94), then the brutal 2022-2023 downcycle with Micron slashing wafer starts from "mid single digits" to 30%. The pattern: management consistently denies the cycle is turning, then panics. The author is entertained by this but also uses it as warning: "At some point in the next 3–10 years, DRAM stocks will drawdown at least 70% from peak."

Agentic Reality (Section 3): What changed the author's view was a Coatue video by Nick Gagnet (Sector Head) arguing memory demand could 5x in 5 years due to agentic AI. The author acknowledges he was told a similar thesis when MU was at $400/share and dismissed it. His revised view: the top is at least 18 months away (from May 2026), assuming agentic AI is a real, permanent demand driver rather than a pull-forward of human demand. This is not a commodity cycle argument — it's a structural demand argument. But the author explicitly says "that is a problem for 18-months-from-now me to figure out."

"If you see a bubble forming, run towards it" — George Soros (mis-attributed to a random hedge fund minion at GTC 2024)

Samsung Pitch (Section 4): Among MU, SK Hynix, and Samsung, the author prefers Samsung for three structural reasons: (1) Samsung's SF4X logic process is competitive with TSMC N7/N6, giving it the best base die economics for HBM4 — confirmed by Samsung's superior yield vs SK Hynix's 6 re-spins; (2) Samsung's in-house NVMe controller design gives it logic design experience neither MU nor SK Hynix can match; (3) Samsung has an internal silicon photonics process and CPO design group that the author believes is ahead of GlobalFoundries and ahead of AMD's CPO group. Samsung is uniquely positioned for the eventual optical-disaggregated-LPDDR future.

The author is personally considering a $100K–$200K position: either Korean equity via IBKR, KORU (3x leveraged Korea ETF), or MU options (rejected as chain looks poor). He explicitly invites feedback from 20+ finance contacts.

Independent Analysis

What holds up: The engineering critique of HBM PHY is among the most technically credible investment analyses published this week. The TDR/bump-capacitance argument is first-principles, not borrowed. The ISSCC 2026 NVLink CPO data is public and supports the optical-disaggregation thesis. Samsung's structural advantage in HBM4 base die (SRF4X vs SK Hynix TSMC N12 disaster vs Micron's internal-process gamble) is documented by the HBM4 qualification chaos the author describes. The historical Sanjay earnings roast is genuinely informative as a cycle behavior map.

Unstated assumptions and problems:

  • 7–10 year HBM phase-out is not a trading signal. If HBM is replaced by optically-disaggregated LPDDR in 7–10 years, that means HBM remains dominant for 5–8 more years. The author is simultaneously bullish on DRAM near-term and bearish on HBM long-term, which is internally consistent but creates confusion about what to actually buy.
  • The Samsung pitch is underdeveloped. Samsung Electronics trades as a single entity encompassing foundry, DRAM, NAND, mobile phones, and consumer electronics — the DRAM upside is diluted by perpetual Foundry losses and mobile margin compression. No price target, no valuation, no explanation of how to access it efficiently. KORU gives 3x leveraged exposure to the entire Korean market, not just Samsung DRAM.
  • The optical-disaggregated-LPDDR thesis assumes latency is solvable. The author acknowledges Nvidia's ISSCC 2026 demo is still limited (0.4 Tbps/mm unidirectional, constrained by photonic thermal crosstalk), and Nvidia is 2–3 years from commercialization. For a memory-bandwidth-bound workload, adding even small optical latencies could degrade inference performance materially. This is not a near-term risk but undermines the certainty of the long-term thesis.
  • Conflict of interest: Author says "Irrational Analysis is heavily invested in the semiconductor industry" and is actively soliciting views from 20+ hedge fund contacts while writing a piece that leads to a Samsung pitch. The transparency is appreciated, but directional bias is present.
  • "Run towards the bubble" framing is a momentum strategy, not a value thesis. The author explicitly acknowledges he believes DRAM will drawdown 70%+ eventually — he's riding the wave, not underwriting the fundamentals. That's a legitimate strategy but very different from a value or quality growth investment.

Bull case killer: Samsung's Foundry business has been a persistent destroyer of value; if foundry losses accelerate while DRAM margins mean-revert, the stock could be flat even as DRAM fundamentals improve. The conglomerate discount is real.

AI Dark Output: The Visible Cost of Invisible Output
2026-05-29 Malcolm Spittler & Dylan Patel 4,492 words
MACRO / NO DIRECT TICKERS

Full Summary

This is an economics research piece rather than an investment thesis. The core argument: AI creates real economic value at scale, but current GDP measurement methodology cannot see most of it — the authors call this "Dark Output." The analogy is Robert Solow's 1987 quip: "You can see the computer age everywhere, but in the productivity statistics." A similar lag is now occurring with AI, but the authors argue the magnitude of measurement error dwarfs all prior cases.

Two types of Dark Output:

  • Substitution Dark Output — work previously done by humans, now done by AI. Example: a legal brief that cost $400 now costs $0.50 in tokens. GDP sees the receipts vanish but not the equivalent output. The authors estimate ~$1.5 trillion in tasks (Tier 4+ evidence) where current AI has credible displacement potential — this is "exposed labor," not confirmed displacement.
  • New Dark Output — work that didn't happen before because it was too expensive. A literature review that cost $2,000 at $2 doesn't get done the same number of times: it gets done before every project. This "new work" may already represent the majority of token consumption, but because it was never priced by a human, it leaves no GDP trace beyond the token cost.

A third category, Captured AI Output, exists where firms maintain pricing power and charge customers the same fee even as AI cuts their internal costs — capturing the AI margin spread rather than passing it to customers. This correctly shows up in GDP as margin expansion, but may be rare outside of highly concentrated industries.

Why services are uniquely hard to measure: Manufacturing automation gave statisticians physical quantities to count (screws per hour). Services have no equivalent unit. GDP for legal services, consulting, and healthcare is typically estimated by dividing dollar receipts by a price index — when AI eliminates the receipt (e.g., a law firm uses AI instead of a billed hour), both the receipt and the output disappear from accounts simultaneously, reading as a decline even as work gets done.

Fingerprints of Dark Output in current data: The authors track a "mismatch" fingerprint: employment in AI-exposed sectors is declining relative to the broader economy, but wages in those same sectors are rising. This is consistent with lower-paid workers (who handled the automatable tasks) leaving the sample, pushing average wages up even as headcount falls — a classic Dark Output artifact.

At Anthropic's Economic Index (March 2026): 37% of tokens are used in "computers and mathematics," but software investment in GDP has not broken its pre-AI trend and wasn't at an all-time high. This suggests much of the current token spend is creating new dark output rather than displacing visible-to-GDP work.

The Evidence Ladder framework: the authors rank evidence from Tier 1 (AI benchmarks — weakest, backward-looking) to Tier 6 (insurers underwriting AI work — strongest, third-party price discovery of failure modes). Most of the $1.5T exposure identified is at Tier 4 (business stating tools are in production use), not Tier 5 (successful court defense of AI work) or Tier 6. They explicitly note: "Most evidence collected to date points to AI augmentation, not AI replacement."

The policy/investment implication: if AI is creating an event on the scale of the Industrial Revolution, macro data will systematically underestimate productivity growth, potentially causing the Fed to overtighten, investors to dismiss AI as a bubble based on stagnant GDP, and politicians to misread the impact. The incoming Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh acknowledged this risk in December 2025.

Appendix: A detailed comparison with Feminist Economics' longstanding critique that GDP excludes household production (unpaid care work worth $11T/year per ILO, or 63% of UK GDP equivalent). The authors argue AI pushes a large fraction of work into the same "produced but unpriced" category, making the existing measurement problem dramatically worse.

Independent Analysis

Intellectual quality: This is the strongest analytical piece of the week by a significant margin. The GDP measurement problem is real, well-documented in academic literature, and the authors deploy genuine economic scholarship (Griliches, Waring, Stiglitz Commission, BEA methodology notes) rather than hand-waving. The feminist economics parallel is intellectually honest and genuinely illuminating.

What to challenge:

  • The $1.5T "exposed labor" estimate is methodology-dependent and should be read carefully. It's based on Tier 4 evidence (business claims of production use) matched to O*NET work activities and BLS wages. This involves multiple approximation steps, and the authors are transparent about it. It is not a forecast of displacement — it is a map of pressure points.
  • The document acknowledges but doesn't resolve the key investment question: if GDP systematically understates AI's value contribution, what happens to asset prices when this becomes visible? The authors suggest measurement reform is needed but don't model the market implications.
  • "Most evidence points to augmentation, not replacement" is buried — the headline framing emphasizes the scale of the dark output problem, but the honest admission is that $1.5T is "exposed labor," not confirmed dark output. The most dramatic claims in the piece are about the future, not the present.
  • The Solow Paradox parallel cuts both ways. The 1990s saw real productivity gains eventually show up in data — but only after a decade of measurement lag. If the same is true for AI, the "dark output" period may be exactly the accumulation phase before fundamentals make themselves visible. Alternatively, if AI is overhyped, dark output framing provides intellectual cover for non-existent productivity gains.

Investment implication (the report doesn't make explicitly): If macro data systematically understates AI productivity, the Fed is likely behind the curve in rate cuts, the economy is stronger than reported, and companies with high AI exposure should command higher multiples than GDP/productivity statistics alone would suggest. This is actually the strongest macro bull case for the entire AI infrastructure trade — and SemiAnalysis leaves it implicit rather than stating it.

CONFIDENCE: N/A (no investment thesis) — Framework quality: HIGH. This is essential reading for understanding why traditional macro analysis may systematically undervalue AI's economic contribution.

🎙️ Huawei's Tau Scaling Law: Is the "EUV Killer" Real?
2026-05-29 Vik Sekar & Austin Lyons — Podcast Transcript 6,430 words
LONG ASML LONG Advanced Packaging (BESI) LONG EDA (SNPS/CDNS)

Full Summary

HiSilicon head He Tingbo presented Huawei's "tau scaling law" at ISCAS 2026 in Shanghai. The internet immediately declared ASML dead and EUV controls rendered useless. Vik and Austin systematically dismantle this interpretation while giving Huawei genuine credit for the engineering achievement.

What tau scaling actually is: Tau (τ) is delay — in transistors, circuits, chips, interconnects, memory buses, and networking. Moore's Law scaled transistor delay by shrinking geometry. Huawei's reframe: since they can't scale geometry (no EUV), why not scale down system-level delay across every other dimension? Their equation: τ_next = τ_current / α, where α = 1.3x/year for mobile, 1.5x for auto, and potentially 10x for AI workloads. This is not a physical law — it's a target and a research agenda.

Logic folding (the real achievement): Huawei's Kirin 2026 SoC demonstrates logic-on-logic stacking via hybrid bonding — two logic dies bonded face-to-face, doubling transistor count in the footprint of one. At 1.5µm pitch, hybrid bonding enables millions of connections per die. This is what enables their "1.4nm-class performance" claim without EUV — they're not shrinking transistors; they're doubling them by stacking. The hosts correctly identify this as analogous to (though more complex than) Intel Foveros Direct and AMD V-Cache.

Who builds it and can tools be export-controlled? The manufacturer is assumed to be SMIC (whose stock rallied on the news), but Huawei never confirmed this. The key tool is a hybrid bonding machine from BESI (Netherlands), and crucially, hybrid bonding equipment is NOT currently under export controls — unlike EUV. BESI reported 35% of its business is China-based as of the latest quarter. Vik notes die-to-wafer bonding (needed for logic-on-logic) is a newer capability than wafer-to-wafer (used in NAND stacking), so China's capability here may lag somewhat.

Why this is bullish for ASML, not bearish: China was never buying EUV machines from ASML — export controls already prevented that. Tau scaling requires TWO wafers to make one device's worth of transistors, meaning twice the DUV machine-hours per chip. More chips being made in China (for this approach) = more ASML DUV orders, not fewer. "This is a positive for ASML, right?"

Other tau knobs Huawei proposes: (1) A unified memory bus eliminating protocol translation latency (industry is already moving this direction via RDMA, UCIe, etc.); (2) Near-packaged optics to eliminate DSPs and high-latency FEC — "DSP is the tau killer." The hosts note neither of these is Huawei-invented, but the framing as a unified "tau reduction" agenda is valuable reorientation.

Key takeaways: Bullish on advanced packaging (more complex connections), bullish on EDA (multi-physics simulation of stacked logic is dramatically harder than planar), and bullish on BESI/hybrid bonding tool vendors. EUV adoption leaders (TSMC, Samsung) should accelerate logic stacking in advanced nodes — stacking 2nm wafers would dramatically widen the gap over Huawei's DUV-only approach.

Independent Analysis

The strongest analytical journalism of the week on China tech. Vik and Austin correctly identify that the internet's "ASML dead" narrative is backwards, and walk through the mechanism clearly. The BESI 35% China revenue factoid is a genuinely useful data point that most coverage missed.

Assumptions to stress-test:

  • Kirin 2026 performance claims are unverified. "1.4nm-class performance" is a marketing benchmark. The paper describes doubling transistor count via stacking — but performance depends on power density, thermal management, and interconnect bandwidth between layers. Thermal management of stacked active logic is genuinely hard, and the hosts acknowledge this without quantifying the penalty.
  • The 10x AI workload tau improvement is hand-wavy. The paper provides this as a target, not a measured result. Actual system-level latency reduction from all these knobs combined has not been demonstrated at data-center scale.
  • Export control risk on hybrid bonding is real. The hosts note EV Group (Austria) is a key wafer-to-wafer bonder not currently restricted. This could change — the US has been tightening controls continuously since 2022. If hybrid bonding machines get added to the Entity List, the entire Huawei tau scaling plan faces serious supply chain risk.
  • The "US leads will widen" argument is correct in direction but timing is uncertain. TSMC and Samsung are not already doing high-density logic-on-logic stacking at scale for advanced nodes — there are reasons it hasn't been commercialized yet (yield, thermal, design tool complexity). Huawei may be 2–3 years ahead in experience with this specific technique due to being forced to optimize it.
CONFIDENCE: HIGH for ASML bullishness and EDA/advanced packaging angle. MEDIUM for Huawei's actual capability claims — more silicon data needed.
