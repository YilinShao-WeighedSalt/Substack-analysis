Background: State of the Book

For a reader coming in fresh, here is what you need to know about the semiconductor landscape heading into this scan — explained from the ground up.

What is a "foundry" and why does it matter?

Most companies that design computer chips do not actually manufacture them. They send their designs to a foundry — a specialized factory that turns those designs into physical silicon chips. TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company, traded as TSM, ~$462) is by far the world's dominant foundry: it makes chips for Apple, Nvidia, AMD, and nearly every other leading tech company. Its dominance means that if TSMC's factories are full (which they are), every other chip company has a problem.

Intel (INTC, ~$134) is historically different: it both designs and manufactures its own chips. For years, Intel's manufacturing fell behind TSMC, and its stock languished. But under CEO Lip-Bu Tan (appointed March 2025), Intel has been trying to reinvent itself as a foundry that makes chips for other companies too — not just its own. This "Intel Foundry Services" (IFS) turnaround is the single most tracked story across the five newsletters we follow.

Advanced Packaging: The new bottleneck

"Advanced packaging" is the technology that connects multiple chips together into one larger system. Think of it as the wiring and plumbing that lets several chips — say, a computing chip and a memory chip — talk to each other at extremely high speed. In the AI era, chips have become so complex and power-hungry that how you package them together matters just as much as how you build the individual chips.

The two dominant approaches are:

  • CoWoS (Chip on Wafer on Substrate) — TSMC's method. It places chips on a large silicon "interposer" (a middle layer with very fine wiring) that sits on a base layer. CoWoS is proven and widely used for every major AI chip today (Nvidia, Google, AMD), but it is expensive and capacity-limited because it uses actual silicon wafers.
  • EMIB (Embedded Multi-Die Interconnect Bridge) — Intel's approach. Instead of a full silicon middle layer, Intel embeds tiny silicon "bridges" directly into a cheaper organic base, only placing high-precision silicon where chips need to connect. This is potentially cheaper, more scalable (not limited by silicon wafer size), and can be done on larger rectangular panels rather than round wafers. Intel has used EMIB in its own products for a decade.

The key debate: EMIB has worked inside Intel for years, but hasn't been proven at scale for other companies' chips. That is changing — Google's reported 3-million TPU order through MediaTek using EMIB packaging at Intel's fabs is the first major external validation.

Their goal: make designing custom chips as accessible as TSMC has made manufacturing them. Currently, designing a custom AI chip requires massive teams and deep expertise — Broadcom CEO Hock Tan has staked his company's moat on the claim that "no one knows how to design chips like we do." If Architect Labs succeeds, it could democratize access to custom chips for more companies.

Semi Doped Podcast: Advanced Packaging
2026-06-19 • semidoped.substack.com • ~8,700 words (podcast transcript)

Summary

This is a comprehensive educational episode where Vik Sekar and Austin Lyons explain advanced packaging from first principles, then build to the investment-relevant punchline about Google's EMIB order through MediaTek. Here are the key points:

Simple Packaging (the old way): After chips are manufactured on silicon wafers, they need to be "packaged" — cut apart, connected to power and data lines, and enclosed in a protective casing. The oldest method is wire bonding (literally soldering thin metal wires from the chip to a circuit board), which is cheap but creates long, slow connections. A better method is flip chip: turn the chip upside down and use tiny solder balls to connect directly, creating shorter, faster connections. This work was traditionally done by OSATs (Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test companies like Amkor and ASE) — factories that specialize in packaging, not chip manufacturing.

Why "advanced" packaging exists: Modern AI chips are so complex that a single chip can't be big enough. There's a physical limit called the reticle limit (~858mm², about the size of a large postage stamp) — the maximum area a lithography machine can pattern in one shot. Nvidia's GPUs already hit this limit. To get more computing power, you need to connect multiple chips together and make them behave as one. Advanced packaging uses semiconductor-class manufacturing (the same precision as making chips themselves) to create extremely fine connections between chips — vastly better than crude wire bonds or even flip chips.

CoWoS variants explained:

  • CoWoS-S (Silicon interposer): The original. A big piece of silicon with fine metal wiring sits between the chips and the base. Excellent connection quality, but expensive (uses precious silicon wafers) and limited by the reticle size of the interposer itself.
  • CoWoS-R (RDL / redistribution layer): Uses cheaper organic material instead of silicon for the middle layer. Much cheaper and not reticle-limited, but the wiring can't be as fine — not good enough for AI accelerators.
  • CoWoS-L (Local silicon bridges): A hybrid. Uses cheap organic material for most of the middle layer, but places small silicon "bridges" only where chips need high-density connections. Gets most of the performance benefits of CoWoS-S with better cost and scalability.

EMIB explained: Intel's approach goes one step further than CoWoS-L. It eliminates the separate middle layer entirely — chips sit directly on a substrate (base layer), and tiny silicon bridges are embedded into that substrate wherever connections are needed. Key advantages:

  • Two layers vs. three: Fewer manufacturing steps, lower cost.
  • Panel-based substrates: Intel uses large rectangular panels (500mm×500mm) instead of round wafers (300mm diameter), dramatically reducing material waste. (Analogy from the podcast: cutting square cookies from a square pancake wastes less than cutting them from a round one.)
  • Scalability: Because each bridge is tiny and high-yield, you can tile bridges in any pattern — 2×4 grids, 3×3 grids, whatever your chip design needs. Current sizes: up to 8x reticle, targeting 12x+ by 2028.
  • EMIB-T (with through-silicon vias): Allows power and signals to pass through the bridge, critical for AI accelerators.
  • EMIB-M (with embedded capacitors): Includes tiny capacitors for clean power delivery to the chips.

The yield debate: Some analyst reports claim EMIB yield is "only 90%." At first glance this sounds terrible — losing 1 in 10 advanced AI chips (worth tens of thousands of dollars each) to a packaging defect would be unacceptable. But Semi Doped pushes back: Intel has shipped millions of chips using EMIB for a decade in its own products (Sapphire Rapids, Granite Rapids server CPUs). If yield were truly 90%, those products couldn't have shipped profitably. The hosts suspect the 90% figure refers to early EMIB-T/EMIB-M variants and may not reflect the current state.

The Google TPU punchline: Google has reportedly committed to having ~3 million TPUs (its custom AI chips) packaged using Intel's EMIB, targeted for ~2028. The TPU chips themselves would be manufactured at TSMC and designed with MediaTek (traded as 2454.TW, ~4,390 TWD) — not Broadcom, which has historically been Google's primary chip design partner. Semi Doped frames this as a major competitive shift:

“MediaTek is becoming an increasing threat to Broadcom's custom ASIC model, especially with Google TPUs — something to really watch out for. That's why Broadcom stock has been low lately.”

Additionally, SK Hynix is reportedly testing EMIB for HBM (High Bandwidth Memory — the specialized fast memory stacked alongside AI chips) integration. And Semi Doped noted that Intel has a little-known capability in optical chip manufacturing that could make it a player in co-packaged optics (CPO — embedding light-based connections directly into chip packages, the next frontier beyond electrical wiring).

Scaling roadmap: Today's most advanced packages are ~5.5x the reticle size (for Nvidia's Blackwell Ultra and upcoming Rubin chips). TSMC plans 9.5x next, with "System on Wafer" targeting 40x. Intel's EMIB is at ~8x today, targeting 12x+ by 2028. Long-term, wafer-scale computing (an entire wafer as one chip — what startup Cerebras already does) is on the horizon.
