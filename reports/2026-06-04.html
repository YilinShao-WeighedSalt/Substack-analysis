Jun 03, 2026 | ~9,400 words (free preview) | 10 co-authors | Tickers: None directly promoted

Full Summary

This is a massive, first-principles analysis of orbital compute economics, tied to the launch of SemiAnalysis's new "AI Space Datacenter TCO Model." The piece spans four major sections, building the case that space datacenters are theoretically viable but currently 4x+ more expensive than terrestrial alternatives, with parity not expected until ~2040 in the base case.

Context & Motivation: Elon Musk has been aggressively pushing orbital compute — in a February 2026 Dwarkesh Podcast appearance, he predicted "a few hundred gigawatts per year of AI in space" within five years. SpaceX's May 2026 S-1 filing (targeting $1.75T valuation at $135/share) explicitly states a goal of "100 gigawatts of compute to space each year." The xAI-SpaceX merger and Terafab initiative are framed as the corporate machinery behind this vision.

Debunking Four Casual Arguments:

  • "Free 24-hour solar" — False for LEO (~60% sun exposure). Sun-Synchronous Orbit (SSO) gets near-constant sun but still requires battery backup for 35-minute eclipse periods.
  • "Free cooling" — The biggest misconception. Space is a vacuum — no convective cooling. Heat can only be radiated away. The ISS's radiator system costs $340-500M to reject just 70kW (less than half an NVL72 rack). Cooling is the "largest structural constraint for orbital compute."
  • "Lowest latency" — Only true for 5-7 minutes per day when a LEO satellite passes overhead. Inter-satellite links add 30-80ms one-way delay for distant connections.
  • "No permitting needed" — Dawn-dusk SSO is actually a very constrained orbital subset. Capacity limits will eventually create "permitting" equivalents.

Five Layers of Terrestrial Supply: The article introduces a framework for when space becomes necessary: (1) Grid-connected supply ($12-15M/MW, but 5-7 year queues); (2) Converted capacity/powered land (crypto miners, 8-10 GW near-term); (3) Behind-the-meter generation (projected to supply 50% of new AI DC power by 2028); (4) Industrial production expansion ($20M+/MW); (5) Semiconductor ceiling (the binding constraint — TSMC N3 and HBM production limits).

TCO Analysis (2026 baseline): A 30.5kW B300 cluster (16 GPUs) costs $4.1M to deploy in space vs. $1.4M terrestrially. Monthly ownership: $100,925/month (space) vs. $27,724/month (terrestrial). On a GPU-hour basis: $8.64/hr/GPU (space) vs. $2.37/hr/GPU (terrestrial). After accounting for radiation availability and redundancy requirements, Levelized Cost of Compute (LCOC): $10.91/hr/GPU (space) vs. $2.49/hr/GPU (terrestrial).

Path to Parity: Base case: parity ~2040, driven by launch cost drops (Falcon 9 ~$1,400-1,800/kg to Starship ~$250/kg), radiator improvements, and solar cost reductions. "Elon Musk case": near-parity by early 2030s, assuming terrestrial capacity peaks in 2028. By the early 2030s, space could be "only ~30% more expensive."

Terafab Analysis: Musk's $20-25B Austin fab targets 100K WSPM initially, scaling to 1M WSPM (~70% of TSMC's current global output). The authors are cautious: Tesla has "no manufacturing IP," process IP is the deeper constraint, and memory fabrication from scratch is "the hardest to square." They note 1M WSPM would be "24% of global foundry capacity" — unprecedented for any single fab. The piece concludes that Terafab at even a fraction of its target would be meaningful, but the chip constraint will bind before the datacenter constraint.

Critical Analysis

Logic Assessment: Exceptional analytical rigor. The piece systematically debunks simplistic arguments while building a fair framework that acknowledges space compute's long-term viability. The five-layer terrestrial supply framework is original and useful. The TCO breakdown is granular and first-principles-based. The Terafab section appropriately challenges Musk's claims while acknowledging his track record.

Unstated Assumptions: The base case assumes "considerable resources mustered to add semiconductor manufacturing capacity" — this is explicitly acknowledged as optimistic. The model also assumes radiation and reliability challenges are "sufficiently solved or mitigated by ~2040," which is hand-waved. The "Elon Musk scenario" requires terrestrial capacity to peak in 2028, which contradicts the massive BTM and grid expansion the article itself documents.

Potential Bias: SemiAnalysis is launching a paid TCO Model product alongside this article. The piece is partially a product launch disguised as research. Nearly every section ends with a reference to the paid model. However, the free preview is sufficiently detailed to be valuable standalone.

Investment Implications: While no tickers are directly promoted, the analysis is bullish for terrestrial datacenter infrastructure plays (power, cooling, networking) for the next 5-10 years, and implicitly bullish for TSMC/semiconductor equipment makers as the binding constraint. The SpaceX IPO angle (S-1 at $1.75T) is the most directly investable takeaway — the piece provides an analytical framework for evaluating SpaceX's orbital compute claims.

What Would Need to Be True for This to Be Wrong: If Starship achieves $50/kg (not $250/kg) and radiator technology has a breakthrough, the timeline could compress dramatically. If terrestrial permitting gridlock worsens faster than expected, space becomes necessary sooner.

Confidence: HIGH — best analytical framework in this scan. The product-launch angle doesn't undermine the research quality.

The OCS Problem: Optical Circuit Switches improve network reconfigurability and expand scale-up/scale-out interconnect scale, but each OCS node adds 1.5-3 dB of insertion loss. Conventional IMDD (Intensity Modulation Direct Detection) links have only a few dB of link budget — after subtracting OCS loss plus fiber/connector losses, IMDD has "almost no remaining link margin" in the 2.4T/3.2T module generations. The more OCS becomes central to AI cluster networking, the more exposed IMDD's limitation becomes.

Coherent Lite's Role: Through a simplified coherent detection scheme, Coherent Lite recovers the link budget margin consumed by OCS, allowing OCS architecture to continue scaling into 2.4T/3.2T. The thesis: "DCI/LR is the surface-level application; OCS-paired links are Coherent Lite's most central and scale-driven deployment scenario in AI infrastructure."

Value Chain Mapping:

  • InP beneficiaries: Nokia/Infinera, Lumentum, Coherent Corp, Sumitomo Electric
  • Core DSP beneficiary: Marvell
  • TIA beneficiary: Semtech
  • Transceiver beneficiaries: TeraHop/InnoLight, Lumentum, Coherent Corp
  • Substrate/foundry: TSEM (SiPh foundry), Soitec (Photonics-SOI substrate), AXT (InP substrate)

TAM Estimate: "In 2028, Coherent Lite may bring $15-20 billion of incremental industry value, and this figure will rise further in 2029."

Critical Analysis

Logic Assessment: The OCS-paired argument is technically sophisticated and differentiated.
