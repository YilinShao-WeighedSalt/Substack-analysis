Co-Packaged Optics Is Not Delayed. SemiAnalysis Is Just Wrong
Jun 10, 2026 • Paid (free preview) • ~3,500+ words
LITE Long COHR Long AAOI Long GLW Long MRVL Long

Summary

Global Semi Research fires a direct broadside at SemiAnalysis's June 9 report "Powered Down, Lights Off," which claimed co-packaged optics (CPO) is going to be late — scale-out CPO shipments revised down through 2027, scale-up pushed to 2029, and the entire photonics "bottleneck trade" overcrowded and due for a violent unwind. GSR says unequivocally: CPO is not being delayed, and the delay thesis is "a positioning call wearing a supply-chain costume."

The 19% yield trick. SemiAnalysis's core case rests on a single calculation: assume 95% optical-engine attach yield, compound across 32 COUPEs per Spectrum-6 ASIC, and 0.9532 ≈ 19% system yield. GSR calls this "a magic trick" — it freezes yield at one pessimistic snapshot, assumes no screening, no binning, no redundancy, and no learning curve. Critically, GSR points out that SemiAnalysis's own report contradicts itself: it observes Nvidia's Quantum-X (3 COUPEs) is "in comparatively better shape" due to module screening, which is an admission that 0.9532 is an artifact of module granularity, not physics. Spectrum-X carries redundant engines (32 active plus spares), so a single bad coupling doesn't brick the switch. TSMC's COUPE production is running ahead of expectations per GSR's supply chain checks.

GSR concedes the legitimate concerns: soldered switch substrates have no rework path, Spectrum-6 reportedly showed >3.5 dB insertion loss in system-level testing (enough to eat the entire optical channel budget), integration at 32 engines is genuinely hard, and neither Nvidia nor TSMC had identified the root cause at time of writing.

The supply chain evidence that detonates the delay thesis:

  • Laser orders: Nvidia's guidance to Coherent and Lumentum for high-power CW (continuous-wave) lasers climbed from ~40M units in January to ~100M units by April/May — a 2.5x jump likely pulling some 2027-2028 demand forward.
  • Translation: ~100M lasers implies 10M+ ELSFP external-laser units, which back-solves to CPO shipments of 200K-300K switches in 2027, scaling to 600K-800K in 2028. This squares with TSMC's COUPE production plan.
  • Lumentum announced its largest ELSFP purchase order ever. Both Lumentum and Coherent state they expect to ship into scale-up hardware by 2027.
  • Lumentum has booked out an entire FA (final assembly) production line for this demand.

GSR's core argument: You don't 2.5x your laser orders and book out entire production lines for a technology that's "delivering meaningfully below prior expectations." The supply chain commitments are observable, contractual, and capital-intensive — they don't happen for speculative demand.

"Nvidia does not scale high-power laser orders from 40 million to 100 million units, and Lumentum does not book out an entire FA production line, for a technology that is supposedly delivering 'meaningfully below prior expectations.'"

Critical Analysis

Where GSR is compelling: The supply-chain evidence remains GSR's strongest card, and this update reinforces it. Order volumes are observable, contractual, and hard to fake. A 2.5x ramp in laser orders is a concrete, verifiable data point that is very hard to reconcile with a "CPO is delayed" thesis. The logical argument — why would Nvidia massively scale orders and Lumentum book out production lines for delayed tech? — is sound and difficult to dismiss.

Where GSR overreaches: The article dismisses the 3.5 dB insertion loss too quickly. This is not a yield problem that improves with learning curves — it's a physics/design constraint that could require a generation-level fix. GSR's "TSMC running ahead of expectations" claim is based on anonymous supply chain checks with no independent verification. The article also doesn't address whether Nvidia could be over-ordering as a hedge (ordering lasers for both CPO and pluggable fallback paths simultaneously).

Bias and conflict: This is a public feud between two paid newsletters with opposing positions. GSR has historically been bullish on photonics and likely holds positions in LITE/COHR. SemiAnalysis has a track record of provocative bearish calls that generate attention and drive subscriptions. Both have financial incentives beyond accuracy.

What would make this thesis WRONG: (1) If the 3.5 dB insertion loss proves to be a fundamental design flaw requiring a full re-spin, laser orders could become inventory write-downs. (2) If Nvidia is hedging by ordering for both CPO and pluggable paths, total demand may be overstated. (3) "Demand pull-forward" cuts both ways: accelerated orders from 2027-2028 into 2026 could create future quarter disappointments even if CPO works.

Verdict: MEDIUM-HIGH Confidence — Supply chain evidence strongly supports GSR's position. The insertion-loss issue remains underexplored and is the real swing factor.

Intel Should Raise Capital
Jun 11, 2026 • Paid (free preview) • ~4,000+ words
INTC Cautious-Long

Summary

SemiAnalysis (Doug, Sravan Kundojjala, Dylan Patel) makes the case that Intel should seize the current equity market window to issue approximately $25 billion in new shares — about a 4-5% dilution — to fund its Terafab buildout and capitalize on its strongest financial position in years.

The board transformation thesis. Intel's board has been completely overhauled. Franky Yeary stepped down after 17 years, replaced by people who understand technology: the new chair is ex-Qualcomm, Lip-Bu Tan is CEO, and the board now includes Steve Sanghi (Microchip), Stacey Smith, and Eric Meurice (ex-ASML). SemiAnalysis credits the new board with the turnaround that has "partially begun."

Capital already raised — at lower prices. The US government took 433M shares at $20.47 (9.9% stake), SoftBank paid $23.00, Nvidia paid $23.28. All of these anchor investors are already substantially above water at today's ~$123 price. Issuing stock now, well above those entry marks, lifts book value per share and validates earlier investors. Intel is "one of the very few companies on earth that can sell size into a hot tape with the U.S. government holding the floor."

Why other funding sources are exhausted:

  • SCIPs are dead: Intel just bought back Apollo's 49% of Fab 34 for $14.2B (cash + $6.5B bridge loan), proving that selling fab stakes to financial sponsors was expensive money all along. Management called the unwind accretive.
  • Debt is maxed: $45B already on the balance sheet, rising to ~$51.5B with the Apollo bridge loan.
  • Asset sales exhausted: Mobileye and Altera already sold or majority gone.
  • Equity is cheapest: At current valuations (Intel has "pretty much never traded this expensive on a trailing twelve month basis since the 2000 bubble"), equity is the cheapest capital available.

What the capital is for:

  • Terafab: The full multi-phase project could cost up to $119 billion. SpaceX is putting up initial capital, but Intel must contribute meaningfully. Even marginal capital matching means tens of billions not previously in the capex decision matrix.
  • 14A capacity: SpaceX, Tesla, and Terafab solve the 14A customer problem. Initial target of 100K WSPM scaling to 1M (SA acknowledges this will be "extremely rough" in terms of capital).
  • Agentic CPU demand: New wins include Nvidia DGX Rubin NVL8 (dual Xeon 6), Google multi-year Xeon/IPU deal, SambaNova inference.

The market window is open: Intel's ~$498B market cap (SA's figure — actual cap is now ~$565B+) easily supports a large follow-on. SA notes: "If Cerebras can raise $5.55 billion, Intel can raise $25 billion."

"It's time to undo a decade of financial engineering and issue equity now. Because while the ramp is exciting, it's going to cost a lot."

Critical Analysis

Where SemiAnalysis is compelling: The logical framework is sound. Intel has exhausted cheaper alternatives (SCIPs proven expensive by the Apollo buyback, debt near capacity, assets sold). The equity window is genuinely open — Intel is trading at extreme multiples (~100x forward P/E) on thin earnings ($0.29 adjusted EPS). BofA just double-upgraded to Buy with a $135 target. The board transformation is real and verifiable. The SpaceX/Terafab partnership creates genuine capital needs that didn't exist a month ago.

Where the thesis stretches:

  • Market cap discrepancy: SA cites ~$498B, but Intel's actual market cap is $560-620B. This doesn't change the thesis but suggests the piece was drafted before the latest rally.
  • Execution risk is enormous: A $119B Terafab project is the largest semiconductor capital program ever attempted. The 100K-to-1M WSPM ramp target is acknowledged as "extremely rough" but not quantified. What if SpaceX/Tesla demand doesn't materialize at projected volumes?
  • Dilution at peak valuation: SA argues dilution rewards existing investors, but this assumes the stock stays elevated. At ~$123, Intel is trading well above the average analyst target of $85-93. If the stock corrects before or after a raise, dilution at the peak could be seen as a classic sell signal.
  • 18A yield is the elephant: SA doesn't address 18A manufacturing yield, which is the single biggest risk to Intel Foundry's external customer ramp. Without competitive yields, external revenue won't materialize regardless of capital invested.
  • "Never traded this expensive since 2000" — SA uses this as a reason to issue equity, but it's equally a reason the stock is overvalued. The same data point supports opposite conclusions depending on your prior.

What would make this thesis WRONG: (1) If Intel announces an equity raise and the stock drops 20%+, confirming that the valuation was unsustainable. (2) If 18A yields don't reach competitive levels by late 2027, making the Terafab capital largely speculative. (3) If SpaceX/Tesla shift to TSMC for their primary foundry needs after using Intel as leverage.

Author bias: SemiAnalysis has been "vocally right" on Intel's product mishaps and "supportive and excited for the turnaround." They're emotionally invested in Intel's success ("a firm near and dear to our heart") which may reduce skepticism about the turnaround's sustainability.

Verdict: MEDIUM Confidence — The capital structure argument is logical, but issuing equity at historically extreme valuations is a double-edged sword. The 18A yield question is conspicuously absent.

Semi Doped

Computex Mania 2026: Optics and Power
Jun 12, 2026 • Podcast • ~8,200 words (transcript)
MRVL Long INTC Long AVGO Neutral

Summary

Austin Lyons (Chipstrat) and Vik Sekar (Vik's Newsletter) deliver a comprehensive Computex 2026 debrief, covering optics, power delivery, prefab infrastructure, and Intel Foundry. This is their first in-person meeting after six months of podcasting.

Jensen's $100 Billion Sentence. Jensen Huang made a surprise appearance at Marvell's networking keynote, declaring Marvell "the next trillion dollar company." Vik was in the front row filming the leather jacket when MRVL's market cap jumped approximately $100 billion. Vik laments: "My video cost me thousands of dollars. I should have bought." Matt Murphy (Marvell CEO) had just noted three trillion-dollar memory companies had recently emerged in the AI boom. Jensen's endorsement caused MRVL to surge ~30% immediately. Austin notes: "At this point [Jensen] doesn't have to give two billion, he just has to give compliments — and maybe that's even more valuable."

Marvell's Copper Wall and Teralynx. Marvell's keynote positioned the company as an interconnect company, not just an ASIC maker. Matt Murphy drew the line: everything within the rack is now going optical. They unveiled the 102.4 Tbps Teralynx switch (competing with Broadcom's Tomahawk 6), a large tray of pluggables vs. a much smaller CPO module ("the size of your hand"). Marvell's data center revenue is now 76% of total, with interconnect as the lead story. Austin felt Marvell missed an opportunity to explain how their ASIC and interconnect businesses cross-sell.

CPO vs. XPO vs. NPO debate — live from the show floor.

  • XPO modules: Vik saw XPO in person for the first time — "as big as my hand," liquid-cooled, equivalent to 8 OSFP pluggable modules in one. He thinks XPO has "a shot at being an alternative to CPO if the ecosystem wants to stay pluggable."
  • Ayar Labs' CPO scale-up rack: A fully optical CPO rack demo with external laser modules in the faceplate and optical fiber from co-packaged modules inside. Not in production, but a compelling concept of what data centers will look like in 3-5 years.
  • CPO yield concerns: Vik noted that 99% yield in engineering samples doesn't mean you can scale to millions. Testing optical engines is hard — two-sided dies require simultaneous electrical/optical alignment, and current testers can't do this fast enough. "CPO is definitely coming because we have to go to optical — there's no way around it. But there are a lot of challenges."
  • MediaTek microLEDs: Still nascent — only ~100 LEDs running at 1-4 Gbps per lane vs. Avicena's 400-LED demos. Vik: "I see blinky things every day."

800V Power Everywhere. Every rack provider at Computex had 800V systems. Austin and Vik saw a wall of power converter chips from multiple vendors. Key finding from LiteOn's booth: grid-to-800V conversion uses silicon carbide, but everything from 800V down (to 48V, 12V, 6V) is plain silicon. GaN is "still new" and not deployed. This is a significant data point against the GaN hype — silicon is doing the heavy lifting in the actual power delivery chain.

Prefab AI Infrastructure. Vertiv, Delta, Schneider Electric, and Eaton all showed prefabricated AI infrastructure blocks — essentially modular container homes pre-fitted with cooling and power infrastructure. A Delta representative said these reduce deployment time by half. Ketaflow Technologies showed an edge AI deployment that's literally a chest freezer filled with non-conductive liquid, with GPUs fully immersed inside.

Intel Foundry Flex: Clearwater Forest. Austin attended Lip-Bu Tan's keynote (Lip-Bu spoke in Mandarin initially, audience loved it). Clearwater Forest (Xeon 6 Plus with E-cores) is a 17-chiplet design: 2 I/O tiles on Intel 7, 3 active base tiles on Intel 3, and 12 compute tiles on Intel 18A (Darkmont E-cores). Connected via EMIB + Foveros Direct 3D (hybrid bonding, logic-on-logic stacking). Austin's key insight: this is actually an Intel Foundry story masquerading as a product launch. The I/O tiles are reused from Granite Rapids. SRAM doesn't scale, so stays on Intel 3. Only compute uses 18A. This demonstrates Intel Foundry's EMIB + Foveros + 18A + PowerVia (backside power) + RibbonFET (gate-all-around) capabilities all in one shipping product.

Vik adds that Google is engaging Intel Foundry for TPUs (unclear if 18A wafers or just EMIB packaging). Both hosts agree a second packaging option beyond TSMC CoWoS is needed for the industry: "I would hate to see [AI] constrained by the inability to package something."

Critical Analysis

Exceptional ground-truth reporting. The show-floor observations on 800V power (silicon carbide at grid, plain silicon below, no GaN) are the kind of data you can't get from analyst notes. The XPO hands-on and Ayar Labs rack walkthrough provide visual context that's uniquely valuable.

Jensen's market-moving ability is real but dangerous. A single sentence adding ~$100B to MRVL's market cap illustrates the reflexivity problem in AI semis. The endorsement is not new information — Nvidia had already invested $2B in Marvell. Jensen's words are being priced as if they contain forward-looking insider knowledge, which creates fragility if sentiment shifts.

The Intel Foundry read is insightful but optimistic. Austin correctly identifies Clearwater Forest as a packaging technology showcase. However, shipping a 17-chiplet product internally is different from winning external foundry customers at competitive yields and pricing. The Google/Intel Foundry engagement is unconfirmed and could be limited to packaging (EMIB) rather than leading-edge 18A wafer fabrication.

CPO timeline reality check: Vik's candid admission that "nobody really knows if you can produce CPO at scale" and that testing at speed is an unsolved problem is more honest than either side of the SemiAnalysis/GSR debate. The truth is likely between the two extremes.

Verdict: HIGH Confidence — As on-the-ground reporting, this is high quality. As investment thesis, the podcast correctly identifies the key debates without over-committing.

Daily Update — June 12th, 2026

Summary

TSMC raises 3nm prices 15% in H2 2026. Monthly 3nm output has hit a record 175,000 wafers, but supply still falls short of orders from Nvidia, AMD, and Apple. TSMC is expanding Fab 18 in Tainan and ramping Arizona output. Austin does the math: at an assumed ~$20K/wafer, a 15% hike is ~$3K incremental per wafer, or ~$525M/month additional revenue ($1.5B/quarter). "If there's ever a time for it... it's now."

Google taps Samsung for next AI chip. Google is in talks with Samsung to fabricate part of its next-gen TPU, per The Information. This is a supply diversification move as TSMC capacity tightens. Vik is skeptical: "If it's 2nm GAA in Samsung, I don't buy that the yields are up to snuff." Austin notes the Tesla/Samsung connection may have opened the door.

Nasdaq 100 adds Nebius, Astera Labs, Rocket Lab, Teradyne, CoreWeave. More AI infrastructure companies entering index funds means "safe" investments are becoming increasingly exposed to the AI boom. Austin: "ETF buyers get exposure and probably don't know it. Incremental mechanical buying will probably help boost prices near term."

UBS sees semis hitting $2.38T by 2027. UBS forecasts 118% growth to $1.62T in 2026 and $2.38T in 2027. WSTS revised its 2026 forecast from $975B to $1.51T. Austin: "Not long ago we were just talking about $1T by 2030. Now $2T in 2027. Memory price increases are doing a lot of the heavy lifting."

Corning inks multibillion-dollar AWS fiber deal. Custom optical fiber and connectors for AWS data centers — higher-density fiber and smaller connector form factors. Vik: "Its really a 100-year-old company that's more than relevant in the AI age." Austin: "Amazon isn't pulling SKUs off Corning's telecom catalog — they're buying fiber spec'd to AWS rack architecture, which means higher ASPs and a customer that can't switch without re-engineering the datacenter."

Other notable items: onsemi launches GaNEXUS GaN portfolio; Micron selects Bechtel for NY fab; Applied Materials expands Singapore; Supermicro announces $7B equity/debt raise; AMD CFO confirms strong AI demand visibility through 2027; DDR4 production restart signals deepening memory shortages; Astera Labs says switch silicon will overtake retimers by end of 2026.

Critical Analysis

TSMC pricing power is validated by math. Austin's back-of-envelope — $525M/month incremental from 15% on 175K wafers — understates the revenue impact because it uses a conservative $20K/wafer assumption. N3 wafers are likely $22-25K, making the quarterly uplift closer to $2B. TSMC's ability to raise prices while demand exceeds supply confirms the structural shortage narrative.

Google/Samsung skepticism is warranted. Samsung's 2nm GAA yields are widely reported as problematic. However, Google could be seeking Samsung for packaging or older-node components (I/O chiplets) rather than leading-edge compute dies. The market will likely read this as a Samsung positive, but execution risk is high.

UBS $2.38T forecast deserves scrutiny. These numbers are driven heavily by memory price inflation. If DRAM/NAND prices normalize (supply additions in 2028), the headline "semis at $2.38T" could be achieved via pricing rather than unit growth, which would make it fragile.

Verdict: HIGH Confidence — High-signal daily digest with good quantitative takes. TSMC price-hike math and Corning lock-in analysis are particularly useful.

Colt and Ciena complete quantum-safe transatlantic optical trial — the fastest quantum-resistant long-distance transmission to date, over 5,500 km of subsea fiber using Ciena's WaveLogic 6 Extreme. Vik explains the "harvest now, decrypt later" attack vector: adversaries intercept and store encrypted data today, waiting for quantum computing to crack it. China imposed export licensing as part of critical materials controls. Vik warns: "No InP lasers, no optics." This is a genuine supply chain risk that the CPO bulls (including GSR) may be underweighting.

Oracle slides on $40B AI CapEx plans. Oracle plans to raise ~$40B in debt and equity in FY2027, including a $20B ATM equity issuance. The financing overshadowed strong AI/cloud results. Vik notes the capex paradox: "Spend too much, investors ding you. Spend too little, investors ding you for being a declining business." Austin praises Oracle's strategy of hosting its infrastructure within competitors' clouds.

Quick Hits: Lumentum CEO teased a new NPO (near-packaged optics) market opportunity potentially larger than CPO. Astera Labs says switch silicon will overtake retimers by end of 2026. Taiwan data center suppliers push back on 800VDC delay rumors. Omdia reports semiconductor revenue surpassed $300B quarterly for the first time in Q1 2026.

Critical Analysis

The InP supply chain risk is the sleeper issue. While everyone debates CPO yield and timing, China controls 60-70% of InP substrate supply through AXT/Sumitomo. This is a material choke point that could constrain laser production regardless of demand. Neither GSR's bullish thesis nor SemiAnalysis's bearish thesis adequately addresses what happens if InP supply is weaponized more aggressively.

Lumentum's NPO tease is strategically significant. If the CEO is positioning NPO as potentially larger than CPO, it suggests Lumentum is hedging its bets on CPO timing — which somewhat validates SemiAnalysis's concern that CPO may be further out than bulls expect, even as Lumentum's order book validates near-term demand.

CoPoS pull-in is bullish for advanced packaging. Moving from 2029 to H2 2028 confirms the urgency of next-gen packaging. Glass substrates enable larger packages (critical for Feynman-class GPUs), and TSMC accelerating this roadmap suggests they see the demand pull.

Verdict: MEDIUM-HIGH Confidence — Strong daily coverage with the InP angle being the most original and underappreciated risk factor.
